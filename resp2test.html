Arterial Blood Gases & PFTs

Arterial Blood Gases

Normal ranges
  • pH: 7.35 - 7.45
  • HCO3: 22 - 26 mEq/L
  • PCO2: 35 - 45 mmHg
  • PO2: 80 - 100 mmHg

Alveolar-arterial O2 gradient (A-a gradient)

Calculation

PAO2 = FiO2 x (PB – PH20) – (PaCO2 / R)

Where: FiO2 = fraction inspired O2, PB = barometric pressure, PH2O = water vapor pressure (47 mmHg), PaCO2 = arterial CO2, R = respiratory quotient (~0.8)

Simplified (sea level, room air): PAO2 ≈ 150 - (1.25 x PaCO2)

A-a O2 gradient = PAO2 – PaO2

Normal gradient increases with age, roughly (Age/4) + 4.

Causes of Increased A-a Gradient (V/Q mismatch, Shunt)

V/Q mismatch occurs in:

  • COPD (Chronic Bronchitis, Emphysema)
  • Asthma
  • Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
  • Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)

Shunts occur in: (Extreme V/Q mismatch, unresponsive to O2)

  • Congestive Heart Failure (CHF) with pulmonary edema
  • Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)
  • Lobar Pneumonia
  • Intracardiac shunts

Note: Hypoventilation and low FiO2 cause hypoxemia with a NORMAL A-a gradient.

Acidosis and Alkalosis

Basic Compensation
  • Metabolic Acidosis: Lungs compensate by increasing ventilation (↓PCO2). Kidneys compensate slowly by generating HCO3-.
  • Metabolic Alkalosis: Lungs compensate by decreasing ventilation (↑PCO2). Kidneys compensate slowly by excreting HCO3-.
  • Respiratory Acidosis: Kidneys compensate slowly by retaining HCO3-.
  • Respiratory Alkalosis: Kidneys compensate slowly by excreting HCO3-.
  • If pH is normal, but PCO2 or HCO3- are abnormal → suspect fully compensated or mixed acid-base disorder.
Approach to the Patient (Anion Gap)

Calculate anion gap: AG = Na+ - (Cl- + HCO3-)

  • Normal range 8-12 mEq/L (varies slightly by lab).
  • Increased AG Metabolic Acidosis (MUDPILES): Methanol, Uremia, DKA, Propylene glycol, Iron/Isoniazid, Lactic acidosis, Ethylene glycol, Salicylates.
  • Normal AG Metabolic Acidosis (HARDUPS): Hyperalimentation, Acetazolamide, Renal tubular acidosis, Diarrhea, Ureteroenteric fistula, Pancreaticoduodenal fistula, Spironolactone.
Treatment Considerations

Respiratory Acidosis (Hypercapnia):

  • Treat underlying cause (e.g., COPD exacerbation, opioid overdose).
  • Improve ventilation: Non-invasive ventilation (BiPAP) or tracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation.
  • Oxygen titrated carefully (avoid worsening hypercapnia in chronic retainers).

Respiratory Alkalosis (Hypocapnia):

  • Treat underlying cause (e.g., anxiety, pain, hypoxia, PE, sepsis).
  • Rebreathing from a paper bag (use cautiously only if hyperventilation syndrome confirmed, generally discouraged).
  • Address anxiety/pain. Correct hypoxia.

Methacholine challenge

Purpose and Procedure

Bronchoprovocation test used to help diagnose asthma, particularly when baseline spirometry is normal but symptoms suggest asthma.

Procedure: Baseline spirometry is performed. Patient inhales increasing concentrations of methacholine (a cholinergic agonist that causes bronchoconstriction). Spirometry is repeated after each dose.

Positive Result Criteria

The test is considered positive for airway hyperresponsiveness (suggestive of asthma) if the FEV1 drops by 20% or more from baseline at a specific concentration of methacholine (PC20).

Imaging Studies & Procedures

Radiography (X-Ray)

Initial evaluation of chest symptoms. Detects gross abnormalities: consolidation (pneumonia), effusions, pneumothorax, masses, atelectasis, cardiomegaly, tube/line placement.

Ultrasound (US)

Excellent for pleural abnormalities (effusions, loculations), guiding thoracentesis/biopsies. Evaluates diaphragmatic motion. Point-of-care US (POCUS) for pneumothorax, effusions, consolidation.

Nuclear Medicine (V/Q Scan)

Ventilation/Perfusion scan. Primarily used for suspected Pulmonary Embolism (PE) when CT contrast is contraindicated (allergy, renal failure). Assesses probability of PE based on matched/mismatched defects.

Computed Tomography (CT)

Detailed cross-sectional images. Excellent for lung parenchyma, mediastinum, pleura, chest wall.

  • Standard CT: Detects masses, nodules, adenopathy, effusions.
  • High-Resolution CT (HRCT): Thin slices, best for evaluating lung parenchyma (Interstitial Lung Disease - ILD, emphysema, bronchiectasis, ground-glass opacities - GGO).
  • CT Angiography (CTA): With IV contrast, gold standard for diagnosing Pulmonary Embolism (PE). Also evaluates aorta, other vessels.
  • Low-Dose CT (LDCT): Used for lung cancer screening in high-risk individuals.
Virtual Bronchoscopy

CT reconstruction technique providing 3D view inside airways. Can help evaluate airway stenosis, masses, plan for real bronchoscopy.

PET scan (Positron Emission Tomography)

Uses radioactive tracer (FDG) to detect metabolically active tissue. Used for evaluating solitary pulmonary nodules (malignancy risk), staging lung cancer, detecting metastatic disease.

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)

Less commonly used for primary lung evaluation (poorer spatial resolution, motion artifact). Useful for: evaluating chest wall/mediastinal invasion by tumors (e.g., Pancoast tumor), evaluating vascular structures (MRA - Magnetic Resonance Angiography), cardiac evaluation.

Pulmonary Angiography

Invasive procedure involving catheter insertion into pulmonary arteries and contrast injection. Historically gold standard for PE, now largely replaced by CTA. Used occasionally for complex vascular issues or interventions.

Sputum Analysis

Microscopic examination (Gram stain, AFB stain) and culture of expectorated or induced sputum. Identifies bacterial, fungal, mycobacterial pathogens. Cytology for malignancy.

Percutaneous Needle Aspiration/Biopsy

CT or US-guided needle insertion through chest wall to biopsy peripheral lung nodules/masses or pleural abnormalities.

Thoracentesis

Needle aspiration of pleural fluid from pleural space. Diagnostic (analysis of fluid - Light's criteria, cell count, culture, cytology) and therapeutic (drain large effusions for dyspnea relief).

Bronchoscopy (General)

Direct visualization of airways using a bronchoscope. Can be rigid (less common, for foreign body removal, massive hemoptysis control) or flexible.

Flexible Fiberoptic Bronchoscopy

Most common type. Allows visualization down to segmental bronchi. Used for: evaluating hemoptysis, chronic cough, suspected malignancy, foreign body, infection. Can perform procedures:

  • Washings/Lavage (BAL): Saline instilled and suctioned back for culture/cytology.
  • Brushings: Brush scrapes cells for cytology/culture.
  • Biopsies (Endobronchial/Transbronchial): Forceps obtain tissue samples from visible lesions or lung parenchyma.
Transbronchial Needle Aspiration (TBNA)

Needle passed through bronchoscope wall into adjacent structures (lymph nodes, masses) to aspirate cells for diagnosis/staging.

Endobronchial Ultrasound (EBUS)

Bronchoscope with ultrasound probe at tip. Allows real-time visualization of structures outside airway wall (lymph nodes, vessels). Used to guide TBNA (EBUS-TBNA) for accurate sampling of mediastinal/hilar nodes for cancer staging or diagnosing other conditions (sarcoidosis, lymphoma).

Medical Thoracoscopy (Pleuroscopy)

Minimally invasive procedure using scope inserted into pleural space. Allows direct visualization of pleura, biopsy of pleural lesions, drainage of complex effusions, pleurodesis (scarring pleura together).

Mediastinoscopy / Mediastinotomy

Surgical procedures involving incision in neck (mediastinoscopy) or chest (mediastinotomy) to directly access and biopsy mediastinal lymph nodes or masses. Primarily for cancer staging.

Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery (VATS)

Minimally invasive surgical technique using small incisions and camera. Used for lung biopsies (wedge resection), lobectomy, pleural procedures (decortication, pleurodesis), mediastinal mass resection.

Thoracotomy

Open chest surgery involving larger incision. Used for complex resections, procedures not feasible with VATS.

Hoarseness

Case 1:

28 yo female with cc of sinus pressure and pain. Unable to work recently since she can't concentrate and is very tired. She is a receptionist, and her voice is hoarse to the point of being hard to understand. Feels pain in her cheeks every step she takes steps up the stairs to the third floor of her apartment. Pain started 10 days ago, getting worse. She has tried IBU and acetaminophen. Sleeping is hard due to bad cough. This has happened 3-4 times/year for last year. She's been treated with ABX 4 times this year. Three episodes of this in spring. She has felt warm but has not taken her temp. Her R ear is occasionally painful, "stuffy". She has been using the “neti pot" but hasn't helped. Has no allergies and isn't taking any meds. PE: BP 110/68, pulse 85, temp 38°C, RR 20 and unlabored. Hoarse hypo nasal voice with diminished volume, dark circles surround her eyes, mouth breathing during interview. R tympanic membrane is retracted and lacks a good cone of light. Erythematous, boggy inferior turbinates and thick clear nasal discharge, posterior oropharynx shows post-nasal drainage and cobblestoning. Indirect laryngoscopy with mirror shows edema of true and false vocal cords. High arched palate with crowding of maxillary teeth. No cervical lymphadenopathy is appreciated. Lungs show diffuse wheezing.

What is cobble stoning?

Inflammation of minor salivary glands in posterior oropharynx, giving appearance similar to a cobble stone road. Can be caused by post-nasal drip or laryngopharyngeal reflux (LPR).

The most common causative bacteria in acute sinusitis is?
  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae → Strep pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae (non-typeable), and Moraxella catarrhalis are the three most common bacterial causes.
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Mycoplasma pneumoniae
  • Streptococcus viridans group
Timeframe for Sinusitis
  • Acute: < 4 weeks
  • Subacute: 4-12 weeks
  • Chronic: > 12 weeks
  • Recurrent Acute: ≥ 4 episodes/year with resolution between episodes.
Distinguishing Viral vs Bacterial Sinusitis

Features suggesting Bacterial Acute Rhinosinusitis (ARS):

  • Persistent symptoms or signs of ARS lasting ≥ 10 days without improvement.
  • Onset of severe symptoms (e.g., fever ≥ 39°C/102.2°F, purulent nasal discharge, facial pain) lasting for ≥ 3-4 consecutive days at beginning of illness.
  • Worsening symptoms ("double sickening") after initial improvement of a typical viral URI (new onset fever, headache, or increase in nasal discharge after 5-6 days).

Note: Color of discharge (yellow/green) and presence of fever are NOT reliable distinguishing features alone. Facial pain/pressure is common in sinusitis vs simple URI.

Major and Minor Criteria for Sinusitis (Case 1 Patient)

Major Criteria Examples:

  • Facial pain/pressure/fullness
  • Nasal obstruction/blockage
  • Nasal discharge (purulent or discolored postnasal drip)
  • Hyposmia/anosmia (decreased/lost sense of smell)
  • Fever (as major symptom)

Minor Criteria Examples:

  • Headache
  • Fever (as minor symptom)
  • Halitosis
  • Fatigue
  • Dental pain
  • Cough
  • Ear pain/pressure/fullness

Case 1 Patient Findings:

  • Major: Facial pain/pressure, Nasal discharge (postnasal drip noted).
  • Minor: Cough, Ear pain, Fever (38°C), Fatigue.

Diagnosis Criteria (Clinical):

  • ≥ 2 major criteria OR
  • 1 major and ≥ 2 minor criteria

This patient meets criteria based on symptoms.

Pathophysiology of Bacterial Sinusitis

Often follows viral URI. Viral inflammation → mucosal edema → sinus ostia obstruction → impaired drainage/ventilation → negative pressure, retained secretions → favorable environment for bacterial overgrowth → bacterial infection and further inflammation.

Treatment Goal and Approach

Goal: Reduce inflammation, promote drainage, eradicate infection (if bacterial).

Viral ARS: Supportive care (analgesics, saline irrigation, intranasal steroids may help symptoms).

Bacterial ARS:

  • Watchful waiting vs. Antibiotics (based on severity/duration).
  • Antibiotics: Amoxicillin-clavulanate first-line.
  • Adjunctive: Intranasal corticosteroids, saline irrigation.
  • Oral/Topical Decongestants: Limited benefit, potential side effects (oral - BP elevation; topical - rhinitis medicamentosa).

Case 2:

28 yo female, cannot breathe through her nose, both ears plugged, every spring and early fall she has similar symptoms. She was asymptomatic for several years after giving her birth to her first child. Vital signs normal. BP: 140/94, RR 16. PE: b/l retracted tympanic membrane with air fluid levels in middle ear. Inferior turbinates are blue, boggy, thin rhinorrhea. High arched palate, cobblestoning of posterior oropharynx and visible postnasal drainage.

Discussion points

Presentation strongly suggests Allergic Rhinitis with secondary Eustachian Tube Dysfunction (ETD) causing middle ear effusion/retraction.

  • Seasonal pattern (spring/fall).
  • Symptoms: Nasal congestion ("cannot breathe through nose"), rhinorrhea, plugged ears.
  • PE findings: Pale/bluish, boggy turbinates (classic allergic), clear rhinorrhea, cobblestoning (from postnasal drip), retracted TMs with air-fluid levels (ETD).
  • High arched palate can suggest chronic mouth breathing, often associated with chronic nasal obstruction from childhood.
  • Elevated BP (140/94) needs consideration for medication choices.
Which med is indicated for nasal congestion due to allergic rhinitis?
  • Cromolyn sodium (Mast cell stabilizer - less effective, prophylactic).
  • Ipratropium bromide nasal spray (Targets rhinorrhea, not congestion).
  • Fluticasone nasal spray → Intranasal corticosteroids are most effective single agent for allergic rhinitis, especially congestion.
  • Azelastine nasal spray (Topical antihistamine - good option, effective for congestion, but generally second-line to INS).
  • Diphenhydramine (Oral 1st gen antihistamine - sedating, poor for congestion).
1st versus 2nd generation antihistamines
  • 1st gen (e.g., Diphenhydramine, Chlorpheniramine, Hydroxyzine): Lipophilic, readily cross Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB) causing sedation, cognitive impairment. Significant anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, urinary retention, blurred vision).
  • 2nd gen (e.g., Loratadine, Cetirizine, Fexofenadine, Levocetirizine, Desloratadine): Less lipophilic, less BBB penetration (minimal sedation, though cetirizine/levocetirizine can cause some). Minimal anticholinergic activity. Preferred for allergic rhinitis.
Which med would be CONTRAINDICATED given her classical presentation (HTN)?
  • Guaifenesin (Expectorant - irrelevant).
  • Pseudoephedrine → Systemic decongestant (alpha-1 agonist), causes vasoconstriction, can significantly increase BP. Contraindicated in uncontrolled HTN, use with caution in controlled HTN.
  • Diphenhydramine (Antihistamine - no direct BP effect).
  • Mometasone nasal (Intranasal corticosteroid - minimal systemic absorption/effect on BP).
  • Montelukast (Leukotriene receptor antagonist - no direct BP effect).
Initial approach to treatment of her allergies?

Multifaceted approach:

  1. Allergen Avoidance: Identify potential triggers (pollens, dust mites, molds) and implement avoidance strategies (e.g., stay indoors during high pollen, use HEPA filters, dust mite covers).
  2. Pharmacotherapy:
    • Intranasal Corticosteroids (INS): First-line (e.g., Fluticasone, Mometasone). Most effective for all symptoms, especially congestion.
    • Oral Antihistamines (2nd gen): Good for itching, sneezing, rhinorrhea (e.g., Loratadine, Cetirizine).
    • Topical Antihistamines (Nasal/Ocular): Azelastine, Olopatadine. Rapid onset.
    • Saline Irrigation: Helps clear mucus and allergens.
  3. Immunotherapy (Allergy Shots/Drops): Consider if symptoms severe, persistent, refractory to meds, or patient preference. Modifies immune response long-term.

Investigating her environment is part of avoidance strategies.

Systemic steroids adverse effects

Generally avoided for allergic rhinitis due to side effect profile.

  • Short-term: Hyperglycemia, insomnia, mood changes (irritability, euphoria, psychosis), increased appetite, fluid retention, HTN.
  • Long-term: Cushingoid appearance (moon facies, buffalo hump), weight gain, osteoporosis/fractures, adrenal suppression, cataracts, glaucoma, immunosuppression, skin thinning/bruising, myopathy, avascular necrosis.
Immunotherapy indications in allergic rhinitis
  • Symptoms inadequately controlled by pharmacotherapy and avoidance measures.
  • Patient preference to avoid long-term medication use or side effects.
  • Presence of comorbid conditions like allergic asthma.
  • Desire for potential long-term remission of symptoms after discontinuation.
  • Requires confirmed IgE-mediated allergy to relevant allergens.

Case 3:

45 yo male with cc left ear pain. Noticed this three months ago after a trip to the lake. Seen by PCP who said nothing was wrong. It has progressively gotten worse over time, and has a dull burning quality. Modulating factors that precipitated include chewing and swallowing food. Hx of bad ear infections as child. Has been to loud concerts with loud ringing. Lost appetite and 10 lb in last month. No meds. Smoker 1ppd for 28 years, occasional alcohol, and marijuana. Vitals okay, slightly HTN. PE: ears show no sign of infection, good cone of light. Pretty good.

With no positive findings thus far, what additional physical exam should be performed?

Focus on potential sources of referred otalgia (ear pain originating elsewhere).

  • Palpate Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ) during opening/closing (Pain with chewing suggests TMJ dysfunction).
  • Examine teeth and gums (Dental pathology is common cause).
  • Visualize oropharynx and nasopharynx (mirror or flexible scope - looking for tonsillitis, tumor, inflammation).
  • Palpate neck for lymphadenopathy or masses.
  • Cranial nerve exam (esp. V, VII, IX, X which innervate ear/pharynx).
  • (Rigid rhinoscopy, thyroid palpation, carotid auscultation are less likely initial steps based on chewing pain).
What nerves provides sensation to the ear?

Complex innervation, leading to referred pain potential:

  • Auriculotemporal nerve (branch of CN V3 - Trigeminal): Supplies external auditory canal (EAC), anterior auricle, TMJ.
  • Facial Nerve (CN VII): Small sensory branch to EAC and concha.
  • Glossopharyngeal Nerve (CN IX - Jacobson's nerve): Supplies middle ear, mastoid air cells, Eustachian tube.
  • Vagus Nerve (CN X - Arnold's nerve): Supplies EAC and concha.
  • Cervical Plexus (C2, C3 - Great Auricular, Lesser Occipital): Supply posterior auricle and skin over mastoid.
What additional tests?

Based on Hx (smoking, weight loss, chronic pain) and exam:

  • Dental referral for evaluation of teeth & TMJ: High yield given pain with chewing.
  • Flexible fiberoptic laryngoscopy/nasopharyngoscopy: To visualize pharynx, larynx, nasopharynx thoroughly, rule out malignancy (esp. given smoking history and weight loss).
  • Consider CT/MRI of neck/skull base if suspicion for tumor remains high after initial eval.
  • (EMG, neuro referral, barium swallow less likely initial steps).

Case 4:

Obese 63 yo male with cc "I can't talk right". Change in voice for two months. Course breathing quality to his voice with intermittent “cracking". It has progressed gradually and is worse first thing in the morning, improves slightly during the day. Denies fever, chills, or night sweats. Can only read 5-6 sentences before having to take a breath. Hx includes COPD, degenerative joint disease, diabetes, chronic pain. Surgery: C5-C6 anterior cervical fusion spine, cholecystectomy, tonsillectomy as a child. Meds: oxycodone, ipratropium bromide, insulin, and metformin. Social: 1ppd x 45 years, occasional few beers/week, gained 30 lbs in the last year. 144/83 BP, RR 24, pain 0/10, BMI 36. PE: appears to be older than stated age, breathy quality to voice with intermittent bursts of harsh graveled tones. Could not see larynx with mirror due to gag reflex.

What term best characterizes the voice change?
  • Dysphonia: General term for any impairment or abnormality of the voice. (Most appropriate broad term).
  • Dyplophonia: Producing two distinct pitches simultaneously.
  • Aphonia: Complete loss of voice.
  • Stridor: High-pitched breathing sound due to upper airway obstruction (not primarily a voice change).
  • Hoarse: Subjective quality (breathy, rough, strained) - often a component of dysphonia.
Which historical factor would be most helpful in determining the cause?
  • Recent cold like symptoms (Suggests acute laryngitis - less likely given 2-month duration).
  • Works as an auctioneer (Suggests vocal overuse/nodules - possible, but other factors stronger).
  • Heartburn (Suggests Laryngopharyngeal Reflux - LPR, common cause, fits morning worsening).
  • Itchy, watery nose, and eyes (Suggests allergies - less likely direct cause of persistent dysphonia).
  • Onset of symptom after his recent surgery (Anterior cervical spine surgery carries risk of Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) injury, leading to vocal cord paresis/paralysis - strong possibility given timing and breathy voice).
Knowing his left vocal cord is not functioning properly (implied), what test should be ordered?

If left vocal cord paresis/paralysis is confirmed (e.g., by flexible laryngoscopy), need to evaluate the entire course of the RLN.

  • Mononucleosis titers (Irrelevant).
  • CT scan of the brain with contrast (Only if central cause suspected - rare).
  • EKG (Irrelevant).
  • Barium swallow study (Assess swallowing function/aspiration risk due to poor glottic closure, but not diagnostic for nerve cause).
  • Pulmonary function test (May show reduced flows if glottic incompetence severe, not diagnostic for cause).
  • CT Scan of Neck and Chest with contrast: To visualize the RLN path from skull base through neck into chest (around aortic arch on left), looking for compression, mass, surgical changes, aneurysm etc. This is the standard imaging workup for unexplained vocal cord paralysis.
Along with left vocal cord paresis, you notice reflux findings on exam. What therapies are most beneficial?

Requires addressing both issues:

  • Voice Therapy: To help compensate for vocal cord weakness/paralysis.
  • Treatment for LPR:
    • Lifestyle changes: Elevate head of bed, avoid late meals, trigger foods (caffeine, alcohol, acidic, fatty).
    • Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs): Often requires higher doses/longer duration (BID dosing for ≥3 months) than for typical GERD.
  • Consideration for Vocal Fold Injection/Medialization: Surgical options if voice therapy insufficient and paralysis persistent.
Typical laryngeal findings with LPR (Laryngopharyngeal Reflux)
  • Erythema and edema, especially of the posterior larynx (interarytenoid area, arytenoid cartilages).
  • Posterior commissure hypertrophy ("cobblestoning").
  • Ventricular obliteration (swelling of false cords).
  • Diffuse laryngeal edema.
  • Vocal fold edema/erythema.
  • Pseudosulcus vocalis (subglottic edema).
  • Granulomas or contact ulcers (often on vocal processes).
  • Thick endolaryngeal mucus.

Hoarseness Causes

General Categories (MNEMONIC: VINDICATE - adapted)
  • Vascular (rare - e.g., aortic aneurysm compressing RLN)
  • Inflammatory/Infectious (Laryngitis - viral, bacterial, fungal; LPR; Allergies; Autoimmune)
  • Neoplastic (Benign - nodules, polyps, cysts, papillomas; Malignant - Squamous cell carcinoma)
  • Degenerative / Dysfunctional (Vocal fold atrophy - presbylaryngis; Muscle tension dysphonia)
  • Iatrogenic/Intoxication (Post-surgical nerve injury; Intubation trauma; Smoking; Medications)
  • Congenital (Webs, cysts, laryngomalacia)
  • Autoimmune (e.g., Rheumatoid arthritis affecting cricoarytenoid joint)
  • Trauma (Vocal overuse/abuse; External neck trauma)
  • Endocrine/Metabolic (Hypothyroidism - myxedema; Amyloidosis)
  • Neurologic (Vocal cord paralysis/paresis - RLN injury, stroke, ALS, Parkinson's; Spasmodic dysphonia)
Most common causes
  • Acute Laryngitis (usually viral)
  • Benign Vocal Fold Lesions (Nodules, Polyps, Cysts - often related to voice use)
  • Laryngopharyngeal Reflux (LPR)
  • Muscle Tension Dysphonia
  • Vocal Fold Paralysis/Paresis
  • Presbylaryngis (Age-related changes)
  • Malignancy (especially in smokers or those with other risk factors)
When to refer to Otolaryngology (ENT)?

Referral for laryngoscopy is indicated for:

  • Hoarseness lasting > 2-4 weeks (especially in absence of obvious acute URI).
  • Associated symptoms like: significant throat pain, dysphagia, odynophagia, hemoptysis, neck mass, shortness of breath/stridor, significant weight loss.
  • Professional voice users with persistent hoarseness.
  • Suspicion of malignancy (e.g., smoker with new persistent hoarseness).
  • History of neck surgery or trauma preceding hoarseness.

Pain (Referred Otalgia Context)

Two major types of ear pain

Primary Otalgia: Pain originating from the ear itself (e.g., Otitis Media, Otitis Externa, Mastoiditis, Trauma, Cerumen impaction).

Referred Otalgia: Pain felt in the ear but originating from another site innervated by nerves shared with the ear (CN V, VII, IX, X, C2, C3). Common sources include TMJ, dental problems, pharyngitis/tonsillitis, laryngitis, tumors, cervical spine issues.

Four key areas to examine for referred otalgia
  1. Ear: Rule out primary ear pathology (Otoscopy).
  2. Oral Cavity/Teeth: Check for dental caries, abscesses, mucosal lesions.
  3. TMJ: Palpate joint for tenderness, clicking; assess jaw movement.
  4. Oropharynx/Larynx: Visualize tonsils, pharynx, base of tongue, larynx (mirror or scope) for inflammation, masses, asymmetry.
Treatment Principle

Identify and treat the underlying cause of the otalgia, whether primary or referred.

Radiology Basics

Nuclear Medicine

V/Q Scanning

Used for suspected thromboembolism (Pulmonary Embolism - PE), especially when CT contrast allergy or severe renal failure prevents Contrast-Enhanced CT Angiography (CTA). Compares distribution of inhaled radioactive gas (Ventilation) to injected radioactive particles (Perfusion). Mismatched defects (perfused but not ventilated) suggest PE.

Technique Selection

Portable Chest Radiograph (CXR)

Uses
  • Monitoring cardiopulmonary status in ICU/inpatients.
  • Confirming position of central lines, endotracheal tubes, nasogastric tubes.
  • Monitoring disease progression or response to therapy (e.g., pneumonia, heart failure).
  • Initial evaluation of acute chest symptoms when standard PA/Lat views not feasible.
  • Limitations: Technically inferior to standard views (AP projection, often supine/semi-erect, less inspiration).

Fluoroscopy

Uses
  • Real-time X-ray imaging.
  • Assessing diaphragmatic motion ("sniff test") for suspected paralysis.
  • Guiding procedures like biopsies, catheter placements.
  • Identifying calcifications (e.g., within pulmonary nodules, coronary arteries - less common now).

CT (Computed Tomography)

Uses
  • Detailed evaluation of mediastinal and chest wall abnormalities.
  • Assessing extent of known disease (e.g., cancer staging).
  • HRCT: Evaluating interstitial lung disease (ILD - fibrosis, honeycombing), emphysema, bronchiectasis, ground-glass opacities (GGO), consolidation.
  • CTA: Gold standard for diagnosing Pulmonary Embolism (PE).
  • Characterizing pulmonary nodules (size, density, margins, calcification, fat).

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)

Uses
  • Superior soft tissue contrast compared to CT.
  • Evaluating chest wall, pleural, and mediastinal invasion by tumors (esp. Pancoast tumors).
  • Assessing mediastinal/hilar masses.
  • Evaluating vascular structures (MRA) without contrast or radiation (e.g., aortic dissection, congenital anomalies).
  • Cardiac MRI for function and morphology.
  • Limited use for lung parenchyma due to motion artifact and lower spatial resolution for fine detail.

US (Ultrasound)

Uses
  • Evaluating pleural effusions (detecting small effusions, characterizing complexity/loculations).
  • Guiding procedures: thoracentesis, chest tube placement, pleural/peripheral lung biopsies.
  • Assessing diaphragmatic function.
  • Point-of-care evaluation for pneumothorax (lung sliding), consolidation, effusions, pulmonary edema (B-lines).
  • Evaluating pericardial effusions.

OVERVIEW: 5 (+1) Basic Radiographic Densities

  1. Air (Gas): Most radiolucent (blackest). Found in lungs, trachea, stomach bubble, bowel gas. Pathologic air: pneumothorax, pneumomediastinum, subcutaneous emphysema.
  2. Fat: Gray, darker than soft tissue/fluid. Seen in subcutaneous layers, around organs.
  3. Fluid / Soft Tissue: Similar gray density. Includes water, blood, pus, muscle, organs (heart, liver), non-calcified masses/nodes.
    Specific Appearances
    • Consolidation (Pus/Fluid in Alveoli): Opacity that obscures underlying vessels, often with air bronchograms (air-filled bronchi visible against opaque alveoli). Typically space-occupying (no volume loss). Fluffy margins common. (e.g., Pneumonia).
    • Pulmonary Edema (Water): Increased interstitial markings, Kerley B lines, peribronchial cuffing, effusions. Can progress to alveolar edema appearing like consolidation, often bilateral/perihilar ("bat-wing").
    • Pleural Effusion (Water): Homogeneous opacity obscuring costophrenic angle (blunting) and diaphragm. Meniscus sign often seen on upright films. Layering on decubitus views.
    • Hemorrhage (Blood): Can appear as consolidation or diffuse GGO.
    • Mass/Nodule (Cells/Soft Tissue): Discrete opacity, usually well-defined.
  4. Bone (Calcium): Radiopaque (white). Ribs, clavicles, vertebrae, sternum. Calcified granulomas, nodules, or plaques are also this density.
  5. Metal: Most radiopaque (brightest white). Surgical clips, pacemakers, foreign bodies, contrast agents (though contrast is technically fluid density, it appears very bright).
  6. Atelectasis (Collapsed Lung): Increased opacity due to volume loss. Signs: displacement of fissures/hila TOWARDS collapse, elevation of hemidiaphragm, crowding of vessels/bronchi, compensatory hyperinflation of adjacent lung. Can be linear/plate-like, segmental, or lobar.

Discern Atelectasis from Pneumonia

Atelectasis
  • Key Feature: Volume Loss
  • Shift of mobile structures (trachea, heart, hila, fissures) TOWARDS the opacity.
  • Elevation of ipsilateral hemidiaphragm.
  • Crowding of ribs on affected side.
  • Compensatory hyperinflation of remaining lung.
  • Opacity may have sharp margins if abutting a fissure (e.g., RUL collapse and horizontal fissure elevation).
  • Linear or wedge-shaped opacities common.

Tuberculosis

General Info
  • Caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, an acid-fast bacillus (AFB).
  • Transmission: Inhalation of airborne droplet nuclei.
  • Pathogenesis involves cell-mediated immunity (Th1 response) leading to granuloma formation.
  • Primary TB: Initial infection, often asymptomatic or mild flu-like. Typically involves middle/lower lobes. Forms Ghon complex (peripheral lesion + hilar node involvement). Usually contained.
  • Latent TB Infection (LTBI): Immune system contains infection, but bacilli remain viable. Patient is asymptomatic, not infectious. Positive TST/IGRA, negative CXR/symptoms. Risk of reactivation.
  • Secondary (Reactivation) TB: Reactivation of LTBI, often due to weakened immunity. Typically affects apical and posterior segments of upper lobes (higher O2). Prone to cavitation. Symptomatic and infectious.
  • Risk factors for progression/reactivation: HIV/immunosuppression, recent infection (<2 yrs), diabetes, CKD/dialysis, silicosis, malnutrition, certain meds (TNF-alpha inhibitors), IVDU, smoking.
Symptoms of Active TB

Often insidious onset:

  • Pulmonary: Chronic cough (>3 weeks), sputum production (may be bloody - hemoptysis), pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea (late).
  • Constitutional: Fever (often low-grade, diurnal variation), night sweats, weight loss, anorexia, malaise, fatigue.
  • Other: Anemia, leukocytosis (variable), elevated ESR/CRP, clubbing (chronic disease).

Extra pulmonary TB (EPTB)

Common Sites & Features
  • Can affect almost any organ system, more common in HIV/immunosuppressed. Diagnosis often requires biopsy/fluid analysis from affected site.
  • Lymph node (TB lymphadenitis / Scrofula): Most common site (~35%). Painless swelling of lymph nodes (often cervical). May suppurate and form draining sinus tracts.
  • Pleural TB: (~20%) Pleural effusion (usually exudative, lymphocytic predominant). Diagnosis via pleural fluid ADA (adenosine deaminase) levels, PCR, or pleural biopsy (granulomas).
  • Genitourinary (GU) TB: (~10-15%) Can affect kidneys, bladder, genital tract. Often presents with sterile pyuria (WBCs in urine, no bacteria on standard culture), hematuria, flank pain, infertility. Requires 3+ morning urine samples for AFB culture.
  • Skeletal TB (Bone/Joint): (~10%) Affects spine (Pott's disease - vertebral destruction, kyphosis, abscess), large weight-bearing joints (hip, knee). Pain, swelling, decreased range of motion.
  • TB Meningitis: (~5%) Headache, fever, neck stiffness, altered mental status. Often subacute onset. CSF: high protein, low glucose, lymphocytic pleocytosis (initially may be neutrophilic). AFB smear often negative, culture/PCR needed. High morbidity/mortality. Common in children, HIV patients.
  • Miliary (Disseminated) TB: Hematogenous spread. Diffuse small (1-2 mm, "millet seed") nodules throughout lungs on CXR/CT. Can involve liver, spleen, bone marrow, other organs. Often presents with non-specific constitutional symptoms, organomegaly.
  • GI TB: Abdominal pain, diarrhea, weight loss. Can mimic Crohn's disease, malignancy. Often affects ileocecal region.
  • Other sites: Pericardial, cutaneous, ocular, adrenal.

TB Screening & Diagnosis

TB Skin Test (TST / PPD)

Intradermal injection of purified protein derivative (PPD). Read 48-72 hours later. Measures diameter of induration (palpable swelling), not erythema.

Interpretation (Cutoff for Positive):

  • ≥ 5 mm: HIV+, recent TB contacts, fibrotic changes on CXR consistent with old TB, organ transplant recipients, immunosuppressed (e.g., ≥15mg/day prednisone for >1 month, TNF-alpha inhibitors).
  • ≥ 10 mm: Recent immigrants (<5 yrs) from high-prevalence countries, IV drug users, residents/employees of high-risk settings (prisons, nursing homes, hospitals, homeless shelters), mycobacteriology lab personnel, persons with high-risk clinical conditions (silicosis, diabetes, CKD, certain malignancies), children < 4 yrs, infants/children/adolescents exposed to high-risk adults.
  • ≥ 15 mm: Persons with no known risk factors for TB.

Limitations: False positives (BCG vaccination, infection with non-tuberculous mycobacteria). False negatives (anergy due to immunosuppression, recent TB infection, overwhelming TB disease, very young age, improper technique/reading).

Interferon Gamma Release Assay (IGRA)

Blood test measuring T-cell release of interferon-gamma in response to specific M. tuberculosis antigens (not present in BCG or most NTM).

  • Examples: QuantiFERON-TB Gold Plus, T-SPOT.TB.
  • Advantages over TST: Requires single visit, results in 24h, not affected by BCG vaccination, less subjective reading.
  • Preferred for: People with history of BCG vaccination, those unlikely to return for TST reading.
  • Limitations: More expensive, requires lab processing, may have lower sensitivity in young children/severely immunocompromised, potential for indeterminate results.
  • Interpretation: Positive result indicates TB infection (latent or active). Does not distinguish between LTBI and active disease. Requires clinical evaluation and CXR to rule out active TB.
Diagnosing Active TB Disease

Requires microbiologic confirmation whenever possible.

  • Sputum Smear Microscopy: Acid-fast bacilli (AFB) staining (e.g., Ziehl-Neelsen, Kinyoun, fluorochrome). Rapid, inexpensive, but low sensitivity (requires high bacillary load). Specificity issues with NTM.
  • Nucleic Acid Amplification Tests (NAAT): e.g., Xpert MTB/RIF assay. Detects M. tuberculosis DNA directly from sputum. Rapid (hours), high sensitivity/specificity. Can also detect Rifampin resistance (Xpert). Recommended as initial test on at least one sputum sample.
  • Mycobacterial Culture: Gold standard. Most sensitive. Allows for species identification and drug susceptibility testing (DST). Takes weeks (liquid media faster than solid media). Obtain 3 sputum specimens (preferably early morning) 8-24 hours apart.
  • Chest X-ray (CXR): Essential. Findings vary: Primary TB (hilar adenopathy, middle/lower lobe infiltrate, effusion), Reactivation TB (apical/posterior upper lobe infiltrates, cavitation), Miliary TB (diffuse small nodules). Can be normal in HIV/immunosuppressed or EPTB.
  • Other samples for EPTB: Biopsy, CSF, urine, pleural fluid, etc. for AFB smear, culture, NAAT, histology.

Leprosy & Non-TB Mycobacteria (NTM)

Leprosy (Hansen's Disease)

Agent & Spectrum

Chronic infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium leprae, an obligate intracellular acid-fast bacillus. Primarily affects skin, peripheral nerves, upper respiratory tract, eyes, testes. Exists on a clinical and immunological spectrum based on host cell-mediated immunity (CMI):

  • Tuberculoid (TT): Strong CMI (Th1 response). Few lesions, well-defined borders, often anesthetic. Paucibacillary (few bacteria). Low infectivity.
  • Lepromatous (LL): Weak CMI (Th2 response). Numerous, widespread, poorly defined lesions (macules, papules, nodules). Symmetric nerve involvement, often late sensory loss. Multibacillary (many bacteria). Higher infectivity. Classic "leonine facies".
  • Borderline (BT, BB, BL): Intermediate features between TT and LL. Unstable, prone to reactions.

Leprosy Complications
  • Peripheral Neuropathy: Sensory loss (touch, pain, temp), motor weakness (claw hand - ulnar/median; wrist drop - radial; foot drop - peroneal), autonomic dysfunction (anhidrosis). Leads to injuries, ulcers (esp. plantar), infections, deformities, amputations.
  • Lepra Reactions: Acute inflammatory episodes.
    • Type 1 (Reversal Reaction): Upgrading (towards TT) or downgrading (towards LL) shift in CMI. Occurs in Borderline patients. Inflammation of existing skin lesions and nerves (neuritis - pain, swelling, loss of function). Requires prompt treatment with corticosteroids to prevent nerve damage.
    • Type 2 (Erythema Nodosum Leprosum - ENL): Immune complex mediated vasculitis. Occurs in BL/LL patients, often during/after treatment. Crops of tender subcutaneous nodules, fever, malaise, arthritis, uveitis, orchitis. Treated with corticosteroids or thalidomide.
  • Eye involvement: Lagophthalmos (inability to close eye), corneal ulcers, cataracts, glaucoma, blindness.
  • Other: Testicular atrophy, gynecomastia, amyloidosis, renal failure.

Non-TB Mycobacterial (NTM) Infections

General Info
  • Mycobacteria other than M. tuberculosis complex and M. leprae.
  • Ubiquitous environmental organisms (water, soil, dust).
  • Not typically transmitted person-to-person (exception: M. abscessus in CF patients).
  • Opportunistic pathogens, cause disease primarily in susceptible individuals.
  • Common species causing human disease:
    • M. avium complex (MAC): Most common cause of NTM lung disease. Also causes disseminated disease in advanced HIV, lymphadenitis in children.
    • M. kansasii: Causes lung disease similar to TB.
    • M. abscessus: Rapidly growing mycobacterium. Causes lung disease (esp. CF), skin/soft tissue infections, disseminated disease. Often highly drug-resistant.
    • Others: M. fortuitum, M. chelonae (rapid growers - skin/soft tissue); M. marinum ("fish tank granuloma" - skin); M. xenopi, M. malmoense, M. simiae (lung disease).
Predisposing Factors for Pulmonary NTM Disease
  • Underlying structural lung disease: Bronchiectasis (major risk factor), COPD, previous TB, cystic fibrosis (CF), silicosis/pneumoconiosis.
  • Impaired airway clearance: Primary ciliary dyskinesia (PCD).
  • Genetic factors: Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, specific immune defects (e.g., IFN-gamma pathway defects).
  • Host factors: Advanced age, low BMI, ?postmenopausal women ("Lady Windermere syndrome" - nodular bronchiectasis often in RML/lingula in thin, older women without prior lung disease).
  • Immunosuppression (less common for pulmonary NTM than TB, but major risk for disseminated MAC).
Clinical Manifestations
  • Pulmonary Disease: Most common form. Often insidious onset. Symptoms: chronic cough (often productive), fatigue, malaise, dyspnea, weight loss, low-grade fever, hemoptysis. Radiographic patterns:
    • Nodular bronchiectatic form: Multiple small nodules and cylindrical bronchiectasis, often involving right middle lobe and lingula. Common with MAC, esp. in older women.
    • Fibrocavitary form: Upper lobe cavities and fibrosis, resembling reactivation TB. More common in smokers with underlying COPD. Often caused by MAC or M. kansasii.
    • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis-like pattern ("Hot tub lung"): Diffuse GGOs/nodules after exposure to contaminated aerosols (often MAC).
  • Disseminated Disease: Primarily MAC in patients with severe immunosuppression (e.g., HIV with CD4 < 50). Widespread infection involving lymph nodes, liver, spleen, bone marrow. Presents with fever, night sweats, weight loss, abdominal pain, diarrhea, anemia, organomegaly.
  • Lymphadenitis: Usually cervical lymphadenitis in young children (1-5 yrs). Typically caused by MAC. Presents as unilateral, firm, non-tender node(s).
  • Skin and Soft Tissue Infection:
    • M. marinum: Granulomatous lesions ("fish tank granuloma") on extremities after exposure to contaminated water (pools, aquariums).
    • Rapid growers (M. fortuitum, M. chelonae, M. abscessus): Infections related to trauma, surgery, cosmetic procedures, contaminated injections. Can cause abscesses, cellulitis, osteomyelitis.
  • Other: Skeletal infections, catheter-related infections.
Diagnosis

Diagnosis of NTM lung disease requires a combination of clinical, radiographic, and microbiologic criteria (ATS/IDSA guidelines):

  • Clinical Criteria: Compatible symptoms (cough, fatigue, weight loss, etc.) AND exclusion of other diagnoses (like TB).
  • Radiographic Criteria: Compatible findings on CXR or HRCT (nodules, cavities, bronchiectasis, infiltrates).
  • Microbiologic Criteria:
    • Positive culture from ≥ 2 separate expectorated sputum samples OR
    • Positive culture from ≥ 1 bronchial wash or lavage (BAL) sample OR
    • Transbronchial or lung biopsy yielding NTM OR biopsy showing granulomatous inflammation/AFB + positive NTM culture from sputum/bronchial sample.
  • Note: Isolation of NTM from a single sputum sample may represent colonization or contamination, hence the need for multiple positive samples or invasive sampling.
  • AFB smear and culture are essential. Culture identifies species and allows for drug susceptibility testing (DST), which is crucial for guiding treatment (esp. for MAC and M. abscessus).
Treatment
  • Treatment decisions are complex, considering species, drug susceptibility, extent of disease, patient comorbidities, and potential drug toxicity. Not all patients with NTM isolation require treatment.
  • Requires multi-drug regimens for prolonged periods (often 12 months AFTER culture conversion).
  • MAC Lung Disease: Typically 3 drugs: Macrolide (Azithromycin or Clarithromycin) + Ethambutol + Rifamycin (Rifampin or Rifabutin).
  • M. kansasii Lung Disease: Usually susceptible to standard TB drugs: Rifampin + Isoniazid + Ethambutol.
  • M. abscessus Lung Disease: Very difficult to treat due to drug resistance. Requires combination therapy based on DST, often including IV agents (e.g., amikacin, tigecycline, imipenem) plus oral agents (macrolide - if susceptible, clofazimine, linezolid, bedaquiline). Treatment often prolonged and may involve surgery.
  • Surgical resection may be considered for localized disease (e.g., cavities, severe bronchiectasis) failing medical therapy.

Pediatric Pulmonology

Embryology

Key Points
  • Lung development occurs in stages: Embryonic, Pseudoglandular, Canalicular, Saccular, Alveolar.
  • Trachea and esophagus develop from foregut; errors in separation lead to Tracheoesophageal Fistula (TEF).
  • Surfactant production by Type II pneumocytes begins in the Canalicular stage (around 20-24 weeks) and increases significantly in the Saccular stage.
  • First major viability milestone (potential for survival outside womb with significant support) is around 23-25 weeks gestation, largely dependent on sufficient surfactant production and capillary development.
  • Alveolar development continues significantly after birth.

Congenital Malformations

Pulmonary hypoplasia

Incomplete development of lung tissue (fewer airways, alveoli, vessels). Often secondary to conditions limiting thoracic space (Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia - CDH, severe skeletal dysplasia) or reducing fetal breathing movements/lung fluid (Oligohydramnios - e.g., Potter sequence due to renal agenesis). Leads to respiratory insufficiency at birth.

Bronchogenic cysts

Abnormal budding of the ventral foregut/tracheobronchial tree during development. Fluid or mucus-filled cysts, usually in mediastinum or lung parenchyma. Do not communicate with airway typically. Risk of infection, compression of adjacent structures (airway, esophagus). Tx: Surgical resection.

Congenital lobar emphysema (CLE)

Marked hyperinflation (overdistension) of one or more lung lobes, usually due to bronchial cartilage abnormality causing a "ball-valve" effect (air trapping on expiration). Most commonly affects LUL, RML, RUL. Can cause respiratory distress due to compression of adjacent lung and mediastinal shift. Tx: Observation if mild, lobectomy if severe respiratory compromise.

Congenital pulmonary airway malformations (CPAM)

Formerly Congenital Cystic Adenomatoid Malformation (CCAM). Hamartomatous lesion of lung with cystic and solid components; abnormal bronchiolar structures. Does not function in gas exchange. Classified into types based on cyst size/histology. Most common primary congenital lung malformation. Risk of infection, pneumothorax, rarely malignant transformation (pleuropulmonary blastoma). Tx: Surgical resection, often electively in infancy (e.g., around 6 months).

Pulmonary sequestration

Area of non-functioning lung tissue that lacks normal connection to the tracheobronchial tree and receives anomalous systemic arterial blood supply (usually from aorta or branches).

  • Intralobar (ILS): Within normal lung lobe (usually posterior basal segment of lower lobe), shares visceral pleura. Presents later in childhood/adulthood with recurrent infections or incidentally.
  • Extralobar (ELS): Has its own separate pleura, located outside normal lung (often between lower lobe and diaphragm). Usually presents in infancy with respiratory distress, cyanosis, or associated with other congenital anomalies (esp. CDH).

Risk of infection, hemorrhage. Tx: Surgical resection.

Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (CDH)

Defect in diaphragm allowing abdominal contents (stomach, bowel, liver, spleen) to herniate into chest cavity. Most common type is posterolateral Bochdalek hernia (usually left side). Causes pulmonary hypoplasia and pulmonary hypertension due to lung compression in utero. Presents at birth with severe respiratory distress, scaphoid abdomen, bowel sounds in chest. Tx: Immediate intubation (avoid bag-mask ventilation), NGT for gastric decompression, stabilization (manage pulmonary HTN), delayed surgical repair.

Laryngomalacia

Most common cause of congenital stridor. Immature/floppy supraglottic structures (epiglottis, arytenoids, aryepiglottic folds) collapse inward during inspiration, causing intermittent inspiratory stridor. Worse when supine, crying, feeding, or agitated. Usually presents in first few weeks of life, peaks around 4-8 months. Dx: Flexible nasopharyngoscopy shows dynamic collapse. Tx: Usually benign and resolves spontaneously by 1-2 years. Surgery (supraglottoplasty) reserved for severe cases with failure to thrive, significant respiratory distress, apnea, or feeding difficulties. Albuterol is ineffective or may worsen symptoms.

Choanal atresia

Congenital blockage of posterior nasal passage(s) by bone or soft tissue. Bilateral atresia presents at birth with severe respiratory distress and cyanosis, as infants are obligate nasal breathers. Cyanosis often improves with crying (mouth breathing). Unilateral atresia may present later with chronic nasal discharge/obstruction. Dx: Inability to pass small catheter through nose into pharynx; confirmed with CT scan. Tx: Secure airway immediately (oral airway, intubation if needed), surgical repair.

TE fistula (Tracheoesophageal Fistula)

Abnormal connection between trachea and esophagus. Most common type (Type C, ~85%) involves Esophageal Atresia (EA) with a distal TEF (lower esophagus connects to trachea). Presents shortly after birth with excessive secretions, coughing/choking/cyanosis during feeding, respiratory distress, abdominal distension (air enters stomach via fistula). Dx: Inability to pass NGT into stomach (coils in upper esophageal pouch on CXR); air in stomach/bowel on CXR confirms distal fistula. Tx: NGT to suction upper pouch, elevate head of bed, surgical repair.

Pectus Deformities
  • Excavatum ("Funnel Chest"): Concave depression of sternum and costal cartilages. Most common chest wall deformity. Usually cosmetic concern, but severe cases can cause restrictive lung physiology, decreased exercise tolerance, cardiac compression/displacement. Tx: Surgical repair (Nuss procedure or Ravitch procedure) for significant symptoms or severe cosmetic concerns.
  • Carinatum ("Pigeon Chest"): Protrusion of sternum and costal cartilages. Less common. Usually asymptomatic, primary concern is cosmetic. Tx: Bracing (in younger patients) or surgical repair for cosmetic reasons.

Problems with Neonatal Transition

Transient tachypnea of newborn (TTN)

Delayed clearance/resorption of fetal lung fluid after birth, leading to mild respiratory distress ("wet lung"). Risk factors: Cesarean section delivery (especially without labor), maternal diabetes, precipitous delivery, male gender. Presents within hours of birth with tachypnea (RR > 60), mild retractions, grunting, possible cyanosis. CXR: Increased interstitial markings, perihilar streaking, fluid in fissures, mild hyperinflation, mild cardiomegaly. Tx: Supportive care, supplemental O2 (usually low requirement), typically resolves within 24-72 hours.

Meconium aspiration syndrome (MAS)

Aspiration of meconium-stained amniotic fluid into the lungs before, during, or immediately after birth. Occurs primarily in term or post-term infants experiencing fetal distress. Meconium causes airway obstruction (ball-valve effect, air trapping), chemical pneumonitis, surfactant inactivation, and pulmonary hypertension. Presents with respiratory distress (tachypnea, retractions, grunting, cyanosis) in infant born through meconium-stained fluid. Barrel chest, rales/rhonchi common. CXR: Patchy infiltrates, hyperinflation, atelectasis, possible pneumothorax/pneumomediastinum. Tx: Supportive care (O2, mechanical ventilation), surfactant therapy, inhaled nitric oxide for pulmonary HTN, antibiotics often given empirically. Routine endotracheal suctioning at birth only for non-vigorous infants.

Persistent pulmonary HTN of newborn (PPHN)

Failure of the normal postnatal drop in pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR), leading to continued high PVR and right-to-left shunting of deoxygenated blood across the ductus arteriosus and/or foramen ovale into systemic circulation. Causes severe hypoxemia. Can be primary (idiopathic) or secondary to other conditions (MAS, CDH, RDS, sepsis, pneumonia). Presents with significant respiratory distress and cyanosis, often labile (oxygenation changes rapidly), typically within first 12-24 hours. Pre-ductal vs. post-ductal SpO2 difference (>5-10%) suggests R-to-L shunting. Dx: Echocardiogram confirms elevated pulmonary artery pressures, R-to-L shunting, rules out structural heart disease. Tx: Treat underlying cause. Supportive care (maintain temp, glucose, electrolytes), minimal stimulation, sedation, supplemental O2, mechanical ventilation (gentle settings), maintain systemic BP, inhaled nitric oxide (iNO - selective pulmonary vasodilator), ECMO if refractory severe hypoxemia.

Problems with Premature Delivery

Neonatal RDS (Respiratory Distress Syndrome)

Caused by surfactant deficiency in premature infants (lungs not mature enough to produce adequate amounts). Surfactant reduces surface tension, preventing alveolar collapse (atelectasis). Deficiency leads to atelectasis, decreased lung compliance, increased work of breathing, V/Q mismatch, hypoxemia, hypercapnia. Presents shortly after birth with tachypnea, grunting, nasal flaring, retractions, cyanosis. CXR: Classic findings include low lung volumes, diffuse reticulogranular ("ground glass") opacities, and air bronchograms. Prevention: Antenatal corticosteroids given to mother at risk of preterm delivery. Tx: Exogenous surfactant administration via endotracheal tube, respiratory support (CPAP or mechanical ventilation), supportive care (thermoregulation, nutrition, fluid management). Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA) is a common comorbidity, may require treatment (indomethacin/ibuprofen or surgical ligation).

Chronic lung disease of prematurity/Bronchopulmonary Dysplasia (BPD)

Lung injury in premature infants leading to requirement for supplemental oxygen at 36 weeks post-menstrual age (or at 28 days of life if born >32 weeks). Multifactorial pathogenesis: lung immaturity, O2 toxicity, ventilator-induced lung injury (volutrauma/barotrauma), inflammation, infection, poor nutrition. Characterized by impaired alveolarization and vascular development. CXR: Variable; "New BPD" often shows diffuse hazy opacities and decreased lung volumes, while "Old BPD" (less common now) had cystic changes and hyperinflation. Long-term consequences: increased risk of respiratory infections, asthma-like symptoms, pulmonary hypertension, neurodevelopmental impairment. Tx: Minimize lung injury (gentle ventilation strategies, judicious O2 use), nutritional support, diuretics (for fluid overload), bronchodilators (limited role), inhaled steroids (controversial), caffeine (for apnea), possible home O2.

Apnea of prematurity

Developmental disorder in premature infants (<34 weeks gestation) characterized by cessation of breathing for > 20 seconds OR shorter pauses associated with bradycardia (HR < 100 bpm) or oxygen desaturation (SpO2 < 85%). Due to immature respiratory control centers in brainstem and weak respiratory muscle responses. Can be central (no effort), obstructive (effort but no airflow), or mixed (most common). Distinct from periodic breathing (short pauses <10 sec followed by rapid breaths, normal). Tx: Tactile stimulation, supplemental O2 (use cautiously), CPAP, methylxanthine therapy (Caffeine citrate is drug of choice - stimulates respiratory drive).

Respiratory Issues Found in Peds

Pneumothorax

Air in the pleural space, causing lung collapse.

  • Primary Spontaneous Pneumothorax (PSP): Occurs without underlying lung disease. Most common in tall, thin adolescent males. Often due to rupture of subpleural apical blebs.
  • Secondary Spontaneous Pneumothorax (SSP): Occurs due to underlying lung disease (e.g., Cystic Fibrosis, asthma, BPD, infection, Marfan syndrome).
  • Traumatic Pneumothorax: Due to penetrating or blunt chest trauma.
  • Iatrogenic Pneumothorax: Complication of medical procedures (e.g., central line placement, mechanical ventilation, thoracentesis).
  • Tension Pneumothorax: Life-threatening emergency. Air enters pleural space but cannot exit, causing progressive lung collapse, mediastinal shift away from affected side, impaired venous return, cardiovascular collapse. Requires immediate needle decompression followed by chest tube.

Tx: Small/stable PSP may be observed with supplemental O2. Larger or symptomatic pneumothorax requires drainage: needle aspiration or tube thoracostomy (chest tube). Recurrent PSP or persistent air leak may require VATS with blebectomy and pleurodesis (chemical or mechanical).

Asthma Mimics

Conditions presenting with wheezing or cough that can be mistaken for asthma:

  • Foreign body aspiration: Sudden onset cough, choking, wheezing, or stridor, often in toddlers (peak 1-3 yrs). May have unilateral wheeze or decreased breath sounds. CXR may show object (if radiopaque), unilateral hyperinflation (air trapping), atelectasis, or be normal. Requires rigid bronchoscopy for diagnosis and removal.
  • Vascular ring/sling: Congenital anomalies of aortic arch/pulmonary artery compressing trachea and/or esophagus. Presents in infancy/early childhood with stridor (often biphasic), persistent wheezing unresponsive to bronchodilators, cough, feeding difficulties (dysphagia). Dx: Barium swallow, CT/MRI Angiography, Echocardiogram. Tx: Surgical division of ring/sling.
  • Vocal cord dysfunction (VCD) / Paradoxical Vocal Fold Motion (PVFM): Abnormal adduction (closing) of vocal cords during inspiration (sometimes expiration). Presents with episodic shortness of breath, throat tightness, inspiratory stridor or wheeze, often triggered by exercise, stress, irritants. PFTs may show inspiratory loop flattening during episode. Misdiagnosed as refractory asthma. Dx: Direct visualization via laryngoscopy during symptoms is gold standard. Tx: Speech therapy (breathing techniques), managing triggers, counseling.
  • Others: Bronchomalacia/Tracheomalacia, GERD with aspiration, Cystic Fibrosis, PCD, Congenital heart disease, Interstitial lung disease, Bronchiolitis Obliterans.
Cystic Fibrosis (CF)
  • Autosomal recessive disorder caused by mutations in the CFTR (Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator) gene on Chromosome 7. Defective CFTR protein leads to abnormal chloride and water transport across epithelial surfaces.
  • Results in thick, sticky mucus in multiple organs.
  • Dx: Newborn screening (includes immunoreactive trypsinogen - IRT, +/- DNA testing). Confirmed by Sweat Chloride Test (pilocarpine iontophoresis, chloride > 60 mmol/L is diagnostic). Genetic testing identifies specific CFTR mutations.
  • Clinical Manifestations:
    • Pulmonary: Chronic cough, recurrent pulmonary infections (S. aureus, H. influenzae early; P. aeruginosa later; also Burkholderia cepacia, S. maltophilia, NTM), bronchiectasis, wheezing, digital clubbing, eventual respiratory failure. Nasal polyps, chronic sinusitis common.
    • Gastrointestinal: Pancreatic insufficiency (in ~85%) leading to malabsorption (fat, protein), steatorrhea (fatty, foul stools), failure to thrive. Meconium ileus at birth (~15-20%). Distal intestinal obstruction syndrome (DIOS) later in life. Rectal prolapse.
    • Hepatobiliary: Focal biliary cirrhosis, portal hypertension.
    • Endocrine: CF-related diabetes (CFRD).
    • Reproductive: Infertility (azoospermia due to congenital bilateral absence of the vas deferens - CBAVD in >95% males; thickened cervical mucus in females).
    • Sweat Glands: Salty sweat (basis for sweat test).
  • Tx: Multidisciplinary approach. Airway clearance techniques (chest physiotherapy, vest therapy), inhaled therapies (dornase alfa/Pulmozyme - mucolytic; hypertonic saline), chronic antibiotics (inhaled tobramycin/aztreonam for Pseudomonas), treatment of exacerbations. Pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT), fat-soluble vitamin supplementation (A, D, E, K), nutritional support. **CFTR Modulator therapies** (e.g., ivacaftor, lumacaftor, tezacaftor, elexacaftor) targeted to specific mutations, significantly improve outcomes. Lung transplantation for end-stage disease.
Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia (PCD)
  • Group of inherited disorders (mostly autosomal recessive) affecting structure and/or function of motile cilia. Impaired mucociliary clearance leads to chronic sinopulmonary disease.
  • Clinical Manifestations:
    • Neonatal respiratory distress (in ~80% term infants).
    • Chronic, wet cough starting in infancy.
    • Chronic rhinosinusitis, nasal polyps.
    • Chronic otitis media with effusion, conductive hearing loss.
    • Recurrent pneumonia, leading to bronchiectasis.
    • Situs Inversus Totalis (in ~50% - Kartagener Syndrome = PCD + Situs Inversus + Chronic Sinusitis + Bronchiectasis). Situs ambiguus (heterotaxy) also possible.
    • Infertility (immotile sperm in males, impaired fallopian tube function in females).
  • Dx: Often delayed. Suggestive clinical features + Low nasal nitric oxide (nNO) level (screening test) + Definitive test: Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) of ciliary biopsy showing ultrastructural defects (e.g., absent dynein arms) OR Genetic testing identifying biallelic pathogenic mutations in known PCD genes.
  • Tx: Similar to CF airway management: regular airway clearance, prompt treatment of infections, surveillance cultures, hearing/sinus management (tympanostomy tubes, sinus surgery). No specific therapy to correct ciliary defect currently.
SIDS (Sudden Infant Death Syndrome)
  • Sudden, unexpected death of an infant < 1 year of age that remains unexplained after thorough case investigation (autopsy, death scene investigation, review of clinical history).
  • Leading cause of post-neonatal infant mortality. Peak age 2-4 months.
  • Exact cause unknown, likely multifactorial (Triple Risk Model: vulnerable infant + critical developmental period + exogenous stressor).
  • Risk Factors: Prone or side sleeping position, soft bedding/co-sleeping in unsafe manner, overheating, maternal smoking during pregnancy, prenatal substance exposure, prematurity/low birth weight, male gender, lack of prenatal care, young maternal age, poverty, recent illness.
  • Protective Factors: Supine sleeping ("Back to Sleep"), firm sleep surface, room-sharing (without bed-sharing), breastfeeding, pacifier use at sleep time, immunizations.
  • Prevention: Focuses on safe sleep practices.
Brief Resolved Unexplained Event (BRUE)
  • Formerly Apparent Life-Threatening Event (ALTE).
  • Event occurring in infant < 1 year old reported by observer involving ≥1 of: cyanosis or pallor; absent, decreased, or irregular breathing; marked change in muscle tone (hyper- or hypotonia); altered level of responsiveness.
  • Duration typically < 1 minute (often 20-30 sec), followed by return to baseline state.
  • Diagnosis of exclusion after H&P rule out identifiable cause.
  • Categorized as Lower-Risk or Higher-Risk based on specific criteria (age >60 days, gestational age ≥32 wks and PMA ≥45 wks, first event, duration <1 min, no CPR required, no concerning Hx/PE findings).
  • Lower-risk BRUE: Minimal workup, education, shared decision-making about discharge.
  • Higher-risk BRUE: Requires admission and further investigation based on suspected underlying cause (e.g., GERD, seizure, infection, arrhythmia, airway anomaly, abuse).
  • BRUE itself is NOT predictive of SIDS, but underlying cause might be.
Apnea
  • Pathologic Apnea: Cessation of breathing > 20 seconds, OR shorter pause associated with bradycardia, cyanosis, or pallor. Needs evaluation.
  • Apnea of Prematurity: See above.
  • Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) in Children: Repetitive partial or complete upper airway obstruction during sleep. Most common cause is adenotonsillar hypertrophy. Symptoms: snoring, restless sleep, witnessed apneas/gasping, daytime neurobehavioral issues (hyperactivity, inattention, moodiness), mouth breathing, failure to thrive (severe cases). Dx: Polysomnography (sleep study). Tx: Adenotonsillectomy is first-line. CPAP if surgery not effective/indicated.
  • Central Apnea Syndromes:
    • Congenital Central Hypoventilation Syndrome (CCHS): Rare genetic disorder (PHOX2B mutation). Failure of automatic respiratory control, especially during sleep (absent/blunted response to hypercapnia/hypoxia). Requires lifelong ventilatory support (tracheostomy/ventilator or BiPAP).
    • Secondary central apneas (e.g., due to CNS injury, drugs, neuromuscular disease).
  • Breath-Holding Spells: Common benign phenomenon in toddlers (6 mo - 6 yr). Triggered by anger, frustration, pain, or startle. Types:
    • Cyanotic: Crying stops in expiration → breath hold → cyanosis → brief LOC/limpness/stiffening → spontaneous breathing resumes.
    • Pallid: Minor injury/startle → vagal response → pallor, bradycardia, LOC/limpness → rapid recovery.
    Usually outgrown. Differentiate from seizures. Reassurance is key. Iron deficiency may be associated.

Silly Teenagers (Inhalational Injuries)

E-cig vaping associated lung injury (EVALI)

Acute respiratory illness characterized by pneumonitis +/- ARDS. Strongly linked to vaping THC-containing products, particularly those with Vitamin E acetate as a diluent/thickening agent. Presents with respiratory symptoms (cough, SOB, chest pain), constitutional symptoms (fever, chills, weight loss), +/- GI symptoms. CXR/CT show bilateral opacities. Dx of exclusion. Tx: Supportive care (O2, ventilation if needed), corticosteroids often used.

Hydrocarbon inhalation

"Huffing" or inhalation abuse of volatile hydrocarbons (gasoline, solvents, aerosols, paint thinner). Causes acute lung injury (chemical pneumonitis, hemorrhagic pulmonary edema, ARDS) due to direct toxicity and surfactant destruction. Also significant systemic toxicity: CNS depression/excitation, seizures, cardiac arrhythmias ("sudden sniffing death"), hepatic/renal damage. Tx: Supportive care, decontamination (remove contaminated clothing), cardiac monitoring, manage systemic effects.

Respiratory Infections in Pediatrics

Acute otitis media (AOM)
  • Infection of the middle ear space. Peak age 6-18 months. Often follows viral URI causing Eustachian tube dysfunction.
  • Common bacteria: Strep pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae (non-typeable), Moraxella catarrhalis. Viruses also play role.
  • Dx Criteria: 1) Acute onset of symptoms (ear pain/otalgia, irritability, fever); 2) Middle ear effusion (bulging TM, limited TM mobility on pneumatic otoscopy, air-fluid level, otorrhea); 3) Signs/symptoms of middle ear inflammation (distinct TM erythema, otalgia).
  • Tx: Pain management (acetaminophen/ibuprofen). Antibiotics vs. observation depends on age/severity/certainty.
    • Immediate Abx for: Age <6 mo; Age 6 mo - 2 yr with bilateral or severe AOM; Any age with severe symptoms (mod-severe otalgia, otalgia >48h, temp ≥39°C); Otorrhea.
    • Observation option for: Age 6 mo - 2 yr unilateral non-severe; Age ≥2 yr non-severe.
    First-line Abx: High-dose Amoxicillin (80-90 mg/kg/day). Use Amoxicillin-clavulanate if amoxicillin in last 30 days, concurrent purulent conjunctivitis ("otitis-conjunctivitis syndrome" - often H. flu), or history of recurrent AOM unresponsive to amoxicillin. PCN allergy: Cefdinir, Cefuroxime, Ceftriaxone (IM); Azithromycin/Clarithromycin (less effective); Clindamycin (if concern for resistant Strep pneumo).
  • Complications: Mastoiditis, meningitis, facial nerve palsy, hearing loss, TM perforation, chronic suppurative OM, cholesteatoma.
  • Recurrent AOM (≥3 episodes in 6 mo or ≥4 in 12 mo): Consider tympanostomy tubes.
Acute otitis externa (AOE – “swimmers ear")
  • Inflammation/infection of the external auditory canal (EAC). Common in swimmers, humid climates, trauma (Q-tips).
  • Common bacteria: Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Staphylococcus aureus. Fungi (Aspergillus, Candida) less common.
  • Sx: Ear pain (otalgia), itching (pruritus), discharge (otorrhea), feeling of fullness, hearing loss. Pain often exacerbated by manipulating tragus or auricle. Exam: EAC edema, erythema, debris/discharge. TM may be hard to visualize but should be mobile if seen (unless concurrent AOM).
  • Tx: Clean EAC (aural toilet). Topical therapy is mainstay.
    • Mild: Acetic acid +/- hydrocortisone drops.
    • Moderate/Severe: Topical antibiotic +/- steroid drops (e.g., Ciprofloxacin/dexamethasone; Neomycin/polymyxin B/hydrocortisone - avoid if TM perforation suspected due to neomycin ototoxicity; Ofloxacin).
    Wick placement if canal severely swollen. Oral antibiotics generally not needed unless extension beyond canal (cellulitis) or immunocompromise. Pain control. Keep ear dry.
Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)
  • Viral infection causing inflammation and narrowing of the larynx and subglottic airway. Most common cause of infectious upper airway obstruction in children 6 months - 3 years.
  • Common virus: Parainfluenza virus (types 1, 2, 3). Others: RSV, influenza, adenovirus, rhinovirus, measles.
  • Sx: Prodrome of URI symptoms (runny nose, low fever) followed by characteristic triad: inspiratory stridor, barking cough ("seal bark"), and hoarseness. Symptoms often worse at night, may improve with cool air.
  • Dx: Clinical. Severity assessed using scoring systems (e.g., Westley Croup Score). Lateral neck X-ray (optional, if diagnosis uncertain) may show subglottic narrowing ("steeple sign").
  • Tx (Based on severity):
    • Mild (no stridor at rest): Supportive care (cool mist, fluids), single dose oral dexamethasone often given.
    • Moderate (stridor at rest, mild/mod retractions): Single dose Dexamethasone (oral, IM, or IV) + Nebulized Epinephrine (racemic or L-epi). Observe for ≥3-4 hours after epi for rebound.
    • Severe (significant stridor/retractions at rest, agitation, decreased air entry): Dexamethasone + Nebulized Epinephrine + Supplemental O2. Admit to hospital. Consider Heliox if severe.
Epiglottitis
  • Bacterial infection causing inflammation and massive swelling of the epiglottis and supraglottic structures. Life-threatening airway emergency. Incidence dramatically decreased due to Hib vaccine, but still occurs (un/under-immunized, other bacteria, adults).
  • Common bacteria: Historically Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib). Now more often Group A Strep, Strep pneumo, Staph aureus.
  • Sx: Rapid onset (hours) of high fever, severe sore throat, dysphagia, drooling, muffled voice ("hot potato voice"), anxiety, preference for tripod position (sitting up, leaning forward, neck extended). Inspiratory stridor is a LATE sign indicating critical airway narrowing.
  • Dx: Clinical suspicion is key. **DO NOT attempt to visualize pharynx/epiglottis with tongue depressor or any instrument outside a controlled setting (OR)** - can precipitate complete airway obstruction. If stable enough for imaging, lateral neck X-ray may show enlarged epiglottis ("thumb sign"). Definitive diagnosis by direct visualization (laryngoscopy) during intubation.
  • Tx: Immediate airway management is priority! Secure airway via endotracheal intubation (usually in OR by experienced personnel). IV antibiotics (e.g., Ceftriaxone or Cefotaxime +/- Vancomycin if MRSA suspected). Keep child calm, avoid agitation.
Strep throat (Group A Strep Pharyngitis)
  • Bacterial pharyngitis caused by Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A Beta-Hemolytic Streptococcus - GABHS or GAS). Peak age 5-15 years.
  • Sx: Sudden onset sore throat, fever, headache, nausea/vomiting/abdominal pain (esp. younger kids). Exam: pharyngeal erythema, tonsillar exudates, palatal petechiae, tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy. Often **lacks** cough, rhinorrhea, conjunctivitis, diarrhea (helps distinguish from viral).
  • Dx: Clinical suspicion using Centor criteria (or modified McIsaac criteria including age). Confirmation required:
    • Rapid Antigen Detection Test (RADT): High specificity, variable sensitivity.
    • Throat Culture: Gold standard. Recommended if RADT negative in children/adolescents (due to risk of rheumatic fever). Not needed if RADT negative in adults.
  • Tx: Antibiotics primarily to prevent acute rheumatic fever (ARF), also shorten symptom duration slightly and reduce transmission.
    • First-line: Penicillin V (oral) or Amoxicillin (oral, often preferred for taste/dosing) x 10 days. Benzathine Penicillin G (IM single dose) option if adherence concern.
    • PCN Allergy: Cephalexin/Cefadroxil (if non-anaphylactic allergy). Clindamycin or Macrolides (Azithromycin x 5 days, Clarithromycin x 10 days) if severe/anaphylactic PCN allergy (check local resistance patterns for macrolides).
    Return to school/daycare after 24h of antibiotics.
  • Complications: Suppurative (peritonsillar abscess, retropharyngeal abscess, otitis media, sinusitis). Non-suppurative (Acute Rheumatic Fever - preventable with timely abx; Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis - not preventable with abx). Scarlet fever (rash associated with GAS toxin). PANDAS (Pediatric Autoimmune Neuropsychiatric Disorders Associated with Streptococcal infections).
Sinusitis (Pediatric)
  • Inflammation of paranasal sinuses. Often follows viral URI. Ethmoid/maxillary sinuses present early, sphenoid/frontal develop later.
  • Dx of Acute Bacterial Rhinosinusitis (ABRS) based on clinical course (see criteria under Hoarseness/Sinusitis section: persistent ≥10d, severe onset ≥3d, or worsening/"double sickening").
  • Symptoms: Persistent nasal discharge (any quality), persistent daytime cough (often worse at night), +/- fever, facial pain/pressure (less reliable in young children), headache, halitosis.
  • Tx: Similar to adults. First-line: Amoxicillin or Amoxicillin-clavulanate. High-dose recommended if risk factors for resistance. Duration 10-14 days.
  • Complications (rare but serious): Orbital cellulitis (preseptal/postseptal), subperiosteal abscess, cavernous sinus thrombosis, meningitis, epidural/subdural abscess, brain abscess, Pott's puffy tumor (frontal osteomyelitis).
Pneumonia (Pediatric)
  • Lower respiratory tract infection involving lung parenchyma.
  • Etiology varies by age:
    • Neonates (<1 mo): Group B Strep, E. coli, Listeria, Klebsiella, Chlamydia trachomatis (afebrile, staccato cough), viruses (RSV, HSV).
    • Infants/Young Children (1 mo - 5 yr): Viruses most common (RSV, influenza, parainfluenza, adenovirus, metapneumovirus). Bacterial: Strep pneumoniae, H. influenzae (NTHi), Staph aureus.
    • Older Children/Adolescents (>5 yr): Mycoplasma pneumoniae and Chlamydia pneumoniae ("atypicals") become more common, along with Strep pneumoniae. Viruses still common.
  • Sx: Fever, cough, tachypnea (key sign), increased work of breathing (retractions, nasal flaring, grunting), abnormal lung sounds (crackles/rales, rhonchi, decreased breath sounds), chest pain, abdominal pain.
  • Dx: Clinical. CXR often obtained for hospitalized patients or if diagnosis uncertain/severe: Lobar consolidation suggests typical bacterial (esp. S. pneumo); Bilateral interstitial infiltrates suggest viral or atypical. Blood cultures rarely positive unless severe/bacteremic. Sputum culture difficult in young children.
  • Tx:
    • Viral: Supportive care (O2, fluids, antipyretics). Oseltamivir if influenza suspected/confirmed within 48h.
    • Bacterial (suspected outpatient): Amoxicillin (high dose) first-line for typical bacterial CAP. Macrolide (e.g., Azithromycin) if atypical pneumonia suspected (e.g., >5 yrs old, less acute onset, interstitial pattern).
    • Bacterial (suspected inpatient): Ampicillin or Penicillin G (if susceptible S. pneumo). Ceftriaxone or Cefotaxime often used empirically. Add Macrolide if atypical suspected. Add Vancomycin or Clindamycin if MRSA suspected (e.g., severe necrotizing pneumonia, post-influenza).
  • Acute Chest Syndrome (in Sickle Cell Disease): Medical emergency. New pulmonary infiltrate on CXR + fever and/or respiratory symptoms (cough, tachypnea, chest pain, hypoxia). Causes: Vaso-occlusion, infection, fat embolism. Tx: Supportive care (O2, fluids, pain control), broad-spectrum antibiotics (covering typicals + atypicals, e.g., Ceftriaxone + Azithromycin), simple or exchange transfusion.
Bronchiolitis
  • Viral lower respiratory tract infection causing inflammation and obstruction of small airways (bronchioles). Typically affects infants and children < 2 years old, peak age 2-6 months. Seasonal (winter/spring).
  • Common virus: Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV) (~50-80%). Others: Rhinovirus, metapneumovirus, influenza, parainfluenza, adenovirus, coronavirus.
  • Sx: Starts with URI prodrome (runny nose, cough, low fever) for 1-3 days, then progresses to lower respiratory symptoms: tachypnea, prominent wheezing (often diffuse, expiratory > inspiratory), cough, increased work of breathing (retractions, nasal flaring), irritability, feeding difficulties. Apnea can occur, especially in young infants/preemies.
  • Dx: Clinical, based on age and typical symptoms/signs during appropriate season. Viral testing (e.g., rapid RSV antigen) often done but doesn't usually change management. CXR (if done) typically shows hyperinflation, peribronchial thickening/cuffing, atelectasis (variable).
  • Tx: Primarily supportive care. Key is maintaining hydration and oxygenation.
    • Nasal suctioning (bulb syringe or deeper suction) before feeds/sleep to clear secretions.
    • Supplemental oxygen if SpO2 < 90-92%. High-flow nasal cannula (HFNC) may be used for moderate/severe distress.
    • IV fluids if poor oral intake.
    • Routine use of bronchodilators (Albuterol) is NOT recommended (lack of proven benefit). Trial may be considered in select infants with history suggesting underlying asthma, discontinued if no response.
    • Corticosteroids NOT recommended. Antibiotics only if bacterial co-infection suspected.
    Hospitalization if: respiratory distress, hypoxia, apnea, inability to maintain hydration.
  • Prevention: Handwashing. Palivizumab (Synagis) - monoclonal antibody against RSV, given monthly during RSV season to high-risk infants (severe prematurity, chronic lung disease, congenital heart disease) to reduce severity/hospitalization.
Pertussis (Whooping Cough)
  • Highly contagious respiratory infection caused by bacterium Bordetella pertussis. Preventable by DTaP/Tdap vaccines, but immunity wanes. Infants < 6 months are at highest risk for severe disease/death.
  • Transmission via respiratory droplets.
  • Classic Stages:
    • Catarrhal Stage (1-2 weeks): Insidious onset, resembles common cold (rhinorrhea, mild cough, low fever). **Most contagious period**.
    • Paroxysmal Stage (2-8 weeks): Cough becomes severe and occurs in bursts (paroxysms). Multiple rapid coughs followed by forceful inspiratory effort against a narrowed glottis, causing characteristic "whoop" (may be absent in infants/adults). Post-tussive emesis and exhaustion common. Apnea is common and dangerous in infants.
    • Convalescent Stage (weeks-months): Gradual resolution of cough, though paroxysms may recur with subsequent URIs.
  • Dx: Clinical suspicion based on cough characteristics. Confirmation via PCR or culture of nasopharyngeal swab/aspirate (highest yield in first 2-3 weeks). Marked lymphocytosis often present but not specific.
  • Tx: Supportive care (O2, hydration, nutrition). **Antibiotics** primarily to decrease infectivity/transmission (may lessen severity only if started early in catarrhal stage).
    • Drug of choice: Macrolides (Azithromycin preferred, especially infants <1mo; Clarithromycin; Erythromycin).
    • Alternative: TMP-SMX (if >2 mo and macrolide intolerant/allergic).
    Hospitalization often required for infants < 3-6 months due to risk of apnea, cyanosis, feeding difficulties. Droplet precautions. **Post-exposure prophylaxis** with antibiotics recommended for close contacts (household, childcare).
Abscess (Pediatric Head/Neck)
  • Retropharyngeal Abscess (RPA): Infection/pus collection in the potential space behind the pharynx, anterior to prevertebral fascia. Usually occurs in children < 6 years (retropharyngeal nodes regress with age). Often follows URI, pharyngitis, otitis media, or penetrating trauma. Polymicrobial (GAS, S. aureus, anaerobes, H. flu).
    • Sx: Fever, sore throat, dysphagia, odynophagia, neck stiffness/torticollis, muffled voice ("hot potato"), drooling, stridor/respiratory distress if airway compressed.
    • Dx: Lateral neck X-ray may show widened prevertebral space (> half vertebral body width at C2, > full width at C6). CT scan with contrast is preferred imaging to confirm abscess and extent.
    • Tx: Airway management crucial. IV antibiotics (e.g., Ampicillin-sulbactam or Clindamycin +/- Vancomycin). Surgical drainage (incision & drainage - I&D) usually required.
  • Peritonsillar Abscess (PTA) / Quinsy: Collection of pus between tonsillar capsule and superior constrictor muscle. Most common deep neck infection, typically occurs in adolescents/young adults (rare < 8-10 yrs). Often complication of tonsillitis. Polymicrobial (GAS, Fusobacterium, other anaerobes).
    • Sx: Severe unilateral sore throat, fever, muffled "hot potato" voice, trismus (difficulty opening mouth), drooling, ipsilateral ear pain. Exam: Unilateral tonsillar swelling/bulging, uvular deviation *away* from affected side.
    • Dx: Clinical diagnosis usually sufficient. Ultrasound or CT can confirm if needed.
    • Tx: Drainage (needle aspiration or I&D) + Antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin-clavulanate or Clindamycin). IV hydration/pain control often needed initially. Tonsillectomy may be considered for recurrent PTA.

Pediatric Case Studies

Case 1 (Peds):

26 yo G3P2 woman undergoes standard anatomy US at 20 weeks gestation. Cystic lesion noted to be connected to the lung.

What does this lesion most likely represent?
  • Bronchogenic cyst: Usually NOT connected to airway, fluid-filled.
  • Congenital lobar emphysema (CLE): Hyperinflated lobe, not typically described as cystic lesion connected to lung on prenatal US.
  • Congenital pulmonary airway malformation (CPAM): Most common congenital lung lesion, can be cystic, solid, or mixed, arises from airway.
  • Cystic fibrosis: Systemic disease, not a focal lesion.
  • Pulmonary sequestration: Lung tissue NOT connected to airway, has systemic blood supply.

Case 2 (Peds):

7 yo previously healthy child with cough for the last week. Low-grade temp. hoarse cough. Chest x-ray shows air bronchograms.

What is the most likely etiology?
  • Chlamydia trachomatis (Neonatal pneumonia - afebrile, staccato cough).
  • Mycoplasma pneumoniae: Common cause of atypical/"walking" pneumonia in school-aged children (>5 yrs). Gradual onset, low fever, persistent cough, wheezing possible. CXR often shows interstitial or patchy infiltrates (air bronchograms can be seen but less classic than lobar).
  • RSV (Bronchiolitis, usually younger kids <2, prominent wheeze).
  • Staph aureus (More severe, consolidation/abscess/pneumatoceles).
  • Strep pneumoniae (Typical lobar pneumonia, higher fever, more acute onset).
Best treatment?

Macrolide: Azithromycin (or Doxycycline/Levofloxacin in older children/adolescents).

Case 3 (Peds):

4 yo with cough, congestion, fever up to 103.5 F (39.7 C) x 3 days, labored fast breathing with rales, crackles over right mid-lung field.

What is the most likely etiology of this child's presentation?
  • Chlamydia trachomatis (Neonatal).
  • Mycoplasma pneumoniae (Usually older kids, less acute/high fever).
  • RSV (Bronchiolitis picture, wheezing, younger usually).
  • Staph aureus (Possible, but S. pneumo more common for lobar CAP).
  • Strep pneumoniae: Classic presentation for typical bacterial lobar pneumonia (high fever, focal lung findings - crackles, tachypnea, labored breathing). Most common bacterial cause in this age group.

Tx: High-dose Amoxicillin outpatient, or Ampicillin/Ceftriaxone inpatient.

Case 4 (Peds):

3 yo unimmunized child with 3-day history of high fever of 105.6 F (40.9 C), one day history of refusal to eat or drink. Refuses to lay down, mom thought was teething due to drool, but now has excessive drooling.

What finding would you expect to find on x-ray?

Presentation highly suspicious for Epiglottitis (rapid onset, high fever, drooling, refusal to lie down - tripod posture implied).

  • Air crescent sign (Aspergilloma).
  • Lightbulb sign (Posterior shoulder dislocation).
  • Sail sign (Normal thymus in infant CXR).
  • Steeple sign (Croup - subglottic narrowing).
  • Thumb sign: Enlarged, swollen epiglottis seen on lateral neck X-ray. Likely Hib in unimmunized child.
Best next step in management?

Airway Emergency!

  • CT scan (Delay, unnecessary).
  • High-flow O2 (Supportive, but doesn't secure airway).
  • I&D (Not relevant).

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)

OSAHS (Obstructive Sleep Apnea-Hypopnea Syndrome)

Definition Criteria (AASM)

Diagnosis requires either A or B:

A. Presence of ≥ 1 of the following:

  • Complaint of sleepiness, nonrestorative sleep, fatigue, or insomnia symptoms.
  • Waking up with breath holding, gasping, or choking.
  • Habitual snoring, breathing interruptions, or both reported by bed partner or observer.
  • Patient has been diagnosed with hypertension, mood disorder, cognitive dysfunction, coronary artery disease, stroke, congestive heart failure, atrial fibrillation, or type 2 diabetes mellitus.

AND Polysomnography (PSG) or Home Sleep Apnea Test (HSAT) shows:

  • Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI) or Respiratory Event Index (REI) ≥ 5 events per hour.

B. PSG or HSAT shows:

  • AHI or REI ≥ 15 events per hour (regardless of associated symptoms).

Note: Hypopnea definition often includes ≥3% O2 desaturation OR an arousal from sleep.

Physiology of Obstruction
  • During sleep, especially REM sleep, muscle tone decreases throughout the body, including the pharyngeal dilator muscles (e.g., genioglossus) that keep the upper airway patent.
  • In susceptible individuals (due to anatomy, obesity), the airway narrows or collapses during inspiration when negative pressure is generated.
  • Collapse leads to apnea (complete cessation of airflow ≥10s) or hypopnea (partial reduction in airflow ≥10s with desaturation/arousal).
  • Resulting hypoxia and hypercapnia stimulate chemoreceptors, triggering a brief arousal from sleep.
  • Arousal restores muscle tone, airway opens, breathing resumes (often with gasping/snorting).
  • Patient falls back asleep, muscle tone decreases again, and the cycle repeats throughout the night, leading to fragmented sleep and intermittent hypoxia.
Epidemiology & Risk Factors
  • Prevalence increases with age, higher in males until menopause, then similar rates.
  • Obesity (BMI > 30) is the strongest risk factor (present in ~60-70% of OSA patients). Increased neck circumference (>17 inches men, >16 inches women) is a strong predictor.
  • Craniofacial abnormalities: Retrognathia (recessed jaw), micrognathia (small jaw), macroglossia (large tongue), tonsillar/adenoidal hypertrophy (esp. children).
  • Other factors: Smoking, alcohol/sedative use (worsens muscle relaxation), nasal obstruction, family history, certain medical conditions (hypothyroidism, acromegaly, neuromuscular disorders).
Features/Symptoms
  • Excessive Daytime Sleepiness (EDS): Most common presenting complaint. Feeling tired despite adequate hours in bed.
  • Loud, Habitual Snoring: Often disruptive to bed partner.
  • Witnessed Apneas / Gasping / Choking: Reported by bed partner.
  • Non-restorative sleep ("Waking up tired").
  • Morning headaches.
  • Difficulty concentrating, memory problems, irritability, mood changes (depression/anxiety).
  • Nocturia (frequent nighttime urination).
  • Decreased libido / erectile dysfunction.
Health Consequences & Comorbidities

Untreated OSA is associated with significant health risks:

  • Cardiovascular: Systemic Hypertension (difficult to control), Coronary Artery Disease, Heart Failure, Arrhythmias (especially Atrial Fibrillation, nocturnal brady/tachyarrhythmias), Stroke, Pulmonary Hypertension. Mechanisms involve intermittent hypoxia, sympathetic activation, inflammation, endothelial dysfunction.
  • Metabolic: Insulin resistance, Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus, Metabolic Syndrome, Dyslipidemia.
  • Neurocognitive: Impaired attention, vigilance, memory, executive function. Increased risk of motor vehicle accidents and workplace errors due to sleepiness.
  • Mental Health: Depression, anxiety.
  • Perioperative complications: Increased risk during surgery/sedation.
Diagnosis
  • History and Physical Exam: Assess symptoms (Epworth Sleepiness Scale), risk factors, comorbidities. Examine airway (Mallampati score, tonsil size, neck circumference).
  • Polysomnogram (PSG): Gold standard. In-laboratory, overnight study monitoring multiple physiological signals: EEG (sleep stages), EOG (eye movements), EMG (muscle tone), ECG (heart rhythm), airflow (nasal/oral), respiratory effort (chest/abdomen belts), O2 saturation, body position, snoring sounds. Quantifies AHI, RDI, oxygen desaturation, sleep architecture.
  • Home Sleep Apnea Testing (HSAT): Portable monitor used at home. Records fewer signals (typically airflow, effort, O2 sat, heart rate). Suitable for patients with high pre-test probability of moderate-to-severe OSA without significant comorbidities (e.g., severe COPD, CHF, neuromuscular disease). Cannot definitively rule out OSA if negative (may need PSG).
  • Differential Diagnosis: Insufficient sleep, poor sleep hygiene, other sleep disorders (narcolepsy, restless legs syndrome, circadian rhythm disorders), medical conditions (hypothyroidism, HF), psychiatric conditions (depression), medication side effects.
OSAHS Severity Scale (based on AHI)
  • Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI): Average number of apneas + hypopneas per hour of sleep.
  • Respiratory Disturbance Index (RDI): Includes AHI + Respiratory Effort-Related Arousals (RERAs - flow limitation leading to arousal without full apnea/hypopnea).
  • Normal: AHI < 5 events/hour
  • Mild OSA: AHI 5 to 14 events/hour
  • Moderate OSA: AHI 15 to 29 events/hour
  • Severe OSA: AHI ≥ 30 events/hour
  • Severity also considers degree of oxygen desaturation and symptom severity.
Treatment
  • Positive Airway Pressure (PAP) Therapy: First-line treatment for moderate-severe OSA, and mild OSA with significant symptoms/comorbidities.
    • CPAP (Continuous PAP): Delivers constant pressure to splint airway open. Most common.
    • APAP (Auto-titrating PAP): Adjusts pressure automatically based on detected events.
    • BiPAP (Bilevel PAP): Delivers higher pressure on inspiration, lower on expiration. Used if high CPAP pressures needed, intolerance, or concurrent hypoventilation.
    Requires mask interface (nasal, nasal pillows, full face), adherence is key.
  • Behavioral Modifications:
    • Weight loss (if overweight/obese) - can significantly improve or resolve OSA.
    • Avoid alcohol and sedatives, especially before sleep.
    • Positional therapy (avoiding supine position, e.g., tennis ball technique, specialized pillows/devices) - for patients with primarily supine OSA.
  • Oral Appliances (Mandibular Advancement Devices - MAD): Custom-made devices by dentists trained in sleep medicine. Advance mandible forward, opening airway. Option for mild-moderate OSA, or severe OSA intolerant/refusing PAP. Less effective than PAP for severe OSA.
  • Surgery: Considered if PAP/OA fail or intolerance, or specific anatomical obstruction identified.
    • Uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP): Resects uvula, soft palate, tonsils. Variable success rates, less common now.
    • Maxillomandibular Advancement (MMA): More complex, advances upper/lower jaws. Higher success rate for severe OSA but more invasive.
    • Genioglossus Advancement, Hyoid Suspension: Target tongue base collapse.
    • Tonsillectomy/Adenoidectomy: Primary treatment for pediatric OSA if hypertrophy present.
    • Tracheostomy: Bypasses upper airway. Historically definitive treatment, now reserved for rare, life-threatening cases refractory to all other therapies.
  • Upper Airway Stimulation (Hypoglossal Nerve Stimulator): Implanted device (e.g., Inspire) that stimulates genioglossus muscle synchronously with inspiration to protrude tongue and open airway. Option for moderate-severe OSA intolerant/failing PAP, meeting specific criteria (BMI limits, certain collapse patterns on sleep endoscopy).

What is ARDS and what is the initiation factor?
  • ARDS = Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome. Clinical syndrome of acute, diffuse, inflammatory lung injury leading to increased pulmonary vascular permeability, increased lung weight, and loss of aerated lung tissue. Results in severe hypoxemia and bilateral radiographic opacities.
  • Initiation factor: Diffuse Alveolar Damage (DAD), which is the histologic hallmark. DAD results from injury to the alveolar capillary barrier, primarily involving Endothelial cell injury/activation AND/OR Type I Pneumocyte injury. This leads to leakage of protein-rich edema fluid into interstitium and alveoli.

  • Central caseation surrounded by histocytes, giant cells, and fibrous tissue (Granuloma - TB, fungal)
  • Eosinophilic hyaline membranes lining alveoli (Classic finding in the acute/exudative phase of DAD/ARDS - composed of fibrin, cellular debris, plasma proteins). Also see interstitial edema, inflammation, Type II pneumocyte hyperplasia (in later organizing phase).
  • Gray hepatization (Stage of lobar pneumonia)
  • Red hepatization (Stage of lobar pneumonia)

  • Prone positioning: Improves V/Q matching by recruiting dependent (dorsal) lung regions that are often atelectatic/edematous in supine position, improves drainage of secretions, reduces ventilator-induced lung injury. Standard therapy for moderate-severe ARDS.
  • Right lateral recumbent
  • Supine (Often worsens V/Q mismatch in ARDS).
  • Trendelenburg (Head down - generally not used for V/Q, may worsen ICP).

Lung biopsy: necrotizing lesion with pale center and hemorrhagic border invading blood vessels. Organism had 5-10 uM thick branching septate hyphae. Most likely agent?

Necrotizing, angioinvasive fungal infection with septate hyphae branching at acute angles in an immunocompromised host.

Foamy/eosinophilic alveolar exudate containing cysts/trophozoites (often seen with Silver stain - GMS or Giemsa).

  • Blastomyces dermatitidis

What if biopsy showed intense interstitial lymphocytic infiltration and large cells with an "owls eye" cytopathology – single, dark, basophilic nuclear inclusion with a peripheral halo?

Large cells (cytomegaly) with large intranuclear inclusion surrounded by a clear halo ("Owl's eye").

Case 5 (Path):

69 yo man born in Russia immigrated to US at age 40. Ohio. Cc chronic morning cough. Smoker has had chronic cough for many years, but this morning he noticed streak of blood in handkerchief when coughing. Does light hiking with his wife and is asplenic due to sledding accident as a child. Vitals and PE are normal. Sputum sample: AFB smear: patient has TB. CT: opacity in posterior segment right lower lobe.

What is the characteristic lesion of mycobacterium infection and pathogenesis?
  • Characteristic Lesion: Granuloma, typically with central caseous necrosis (cheese-like appearance grossly).
  • Pathogenesis: Inhaled M. tuberculosis bacilli are phagocytosed by alveolar macrophages but resist killing. Infected macrophages present antigens, activating T-cells (Th1 response). Activated T-cells release cytokines (like IFN-gamma) that activate macrophages (forming epithelioid histiocytes) and recruit other immune cells. Macrophages fuse to form multinucleated giant cells (Langhans type). Immune cells aggregate to wall off the infection, forming a granuloma. Central necrosis occurs due to host immune response and bacterial factors.
What is the difference between primary, secondary, and miliary TB?
  • Primary TB: Initial infection, usually in childhood or non-immune host. Often asymptomatic or mild flu-like. Typically involves middle or lower lung zones. Forms Ghon focus (peripheral parenchymal granuloma) + involvement of draining hilar/mediastinal lymph nodes = Ghon Complex. Usually contained by CMI, leading to LTBI (lesions may heal/calcify - Ranke complex).
  • Secondary (Reactivation) TB: Reactivation of latent TB infection, often years later when immunity wanes. Typically involves lung apices (upper lobes, superior segments of lower lobes) due to higher oxygen tension favoring aerobic Mtb growth. Characterized by tissue destruction, fibrosis, and cavitation. More symptomatic (cough, fever, night sweats, weight loss) and source of transmission.
  • Miliary TB: Hematogenous or lymphatic dissemination of Mtb from primary or secondary site. Results in numerous, tiny (1-2 mm, "millet seed"-sized) granulomas widely distributed throughout lungs and potentially other organs (liver, spleen, bone marrow, kidneys, brain, etc.). Can occur with primary infection (esp. infants/immunocompromised) or reactivation.
What if the patient's smear and culture indicated they were negative for TB? What might then be on differential (given Ohio location, granulomas)?

Granulomatous lung disease in Ohio River Valley region, TB negative:

  • Histoplasmosis: Caused by Histoplasma capsulatum. Endemic fungus in Ohio/Mississippi River Valleys, associated with bird/bat droppings. Inhaled spores → yeast form in macrophages. Can cause acute pulmonary infection, chronic pulmonary histoplasmosis (resembling TB, often in smokers with COPD), or disseminated disease (immunocompromised). Forms granulomas (often caseating, can calcify). Microscopy: Small, oval yeasts, often intracellular within macrophages.
  • Blastomycosis: Caused by Blastomyces dermatitidis. Also endemic in Ohio/Mississippi River Valleys, Great Lakes region. Can cause pulmonary disease (acute/chronic), skin lesions, bone involvement. Forms granulomas (often suppurative). Microscopy: Large, round yeasts with thick walls and characteristic broad-based budding.
  • Sarcoidosis: Systemic inflammatory disease of unknown cause characterized by non-caseating granulomas. Lungs and intrathoracic lymph nodes commonly involved (bilateral hilar adenopathy classic). Diagnosis of exclusion.
  • NTM infection (less likely to be primary consideration without positive AFB smear/culture, but possible).

Case 6 (Path):

32 yo AFAB with well-controlled asthma with cc dyspnea, fever, rhinorrhea, cough, myalgias x 3 days. Taking acetaminophen and nebulizer but feeling worse and produces yellow-green colored sputum. Daughter ill with influenza – dx with rapid test. PE: fever, 150 HR, BP 112/64, RR 20, O2 sat 98%. Appears ill but no significant respiratory distress. Crackles in lower left lung.

What type of pneumonia?
  • Aspiration pneumonia
  • Chronic pneumonia
  • Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP): Acquired outside hospital/healthcare setting. Likely triggered by influenza.
  • Health care-associated
  • Pneumonia of immunocompromised
What type of pneumonia pattern is characteristic of viral infections?
  • Bronchopneumonia (Patchy consolidation centered on airways - can be bacterial or viral).
  • Intra-alveolar pneumonitis (Alveoli filled with exudate - more typical of bacterial).
  • Interstitial pneumonia: Inflammation primarily involving the alveolar walls (interstitium) and adjacent airways. Common pattern for viral pneumonias (Influenza, RSV, Adenovirus, CMV, COVID-19) and atypical bacterial pneumonias (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia). Radiographically appears as reticular opacities, GGOs.
  • Lobar pneumonia (Consolidation of entire lobe - classic bacterial, esp. S. pneumo).
Describe pathogenesis of viral pneumonia

Viruses typically infect airway epithelial cells and/or alveolar pneumocytes (often Type I). Viral replication leads to cell damage/lysis and triggers an inflammatory response, primarily involving lymphocytes and macrophages infiltrating the alveolar walls (interstitium) and airways. This leads to interstitial edema and inflammation (interstitial pneumonitis). Damage to Type I cells prompts proliferation of Type II pneumocytes for repair. In severe cases (like influenza, COVID-19), can progress to Diffuse Alveolar Damage (DAD) with hyaline membrane formation.

If this were a primary bacterial pneumonia, describe bronchopneumonia and lobar pneumonia, and its stages?
  • Bronchopneumonia: Infection spreads via airways, resulting in patchy foci of consolidation centered around bronchioles, often bilateral and involving lower lobes. Grossly: multiple small, raised, consolidated areas. Histology: Neutrophilic exudate filling bronchioles and adjacent alveoli.
  • Lobar pneumonia: Infection rapidly spreads through alveolar pores (of Kohn), leading to consolidation of a large portion or entire lobe. Classic example: Streptococcus pneumoniae. Pathologic Stages (less distinct with antibiotics):
    1. Congestion (Day 1-2): Vascular engorgement, proteinaceous fluid, numerous bacteria, few neutrophils in alveoli. Lung is heavy, boggy, red.
    2. Red Hepatization (Day 2-4): Massive exudation with neutrophils, red blood cells, and fibrin filling alveoli. Lobe is red, firm, airless (resembles liver).
    3. Gray Hepatization (Day 4-8): Red cells disintegrate, fibrinosuppurative exudate persists. Lobe is grayish-brown, firm.
    4. Resolution (Day 8+): Exudate enzymatically digested and cleared by macrophages or coughed up. Lung architecture restored (usually).
Patient sent home, condition deteriorates over 7 days. ED with fever. PE: tachycardia, BP 95/52, tachypnea, O2 sat 85%. Ill-appearing. X-ray: homogenous left mid-lung infiltrate with possible cavitation. Lab: pancytopenia, Left shift. Sputum is blood-streaked and purulent. What is an important concern?

Deterioration after initial viral illness (influenza) with signs of severe pneumonia (hypoxia, shock, cavitation, purulent sputum) strongly suggests Secondary Bacterial Pneumonia.

  • Staphylococcus aureus (including MRSA) is a classic and often severe cause of post-influenza bacterial pneumonia. Frequently associated with necrosis, abscess formation, cavitation, and empyema.
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae is also a common cause of secondary bacterial pneumonia post-influenza.
  • Other possibilities: H. influenzae, Group A Strep.

The cavitation, sepsis picture, and history of influenza make S. aureus pneumonia a primary concern.

Pharmacology: Drugs for Rhinitis/Allergy

Allergen avoidance is the first step, but often insufficient. Pharmacotherapy targets symptoms and underlying inflammation.

Oral Antihistamines (H1 Receptor Antagonists)

General Info

Block histamine H1 receptors, reducing sneezing, itching (pruritus), rhinorrhea, and ocular symptoms (conjunctivitis). Less effective for nasal congestion compared to decongestants or nasal steroids.

First Generation
  • Examples: Diphenhydramine (Benadryl), Hydroxyzine (Atarax), Chlorpheniramine (Chlor-Trimeton), Brompheniramine.
  • Characteristics: Lipophilic, readily cross BBB causing significant sedation and cognitive impairment. Potent anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, urinary retention, blurred vision, constipation, confusion - esp. elderly).
  • Use: Generally avoided for routine allergic rhinitis due to side effects. Used for acute allergic reactions, hives, motion sickness, sleep aid (diphenhydramine).
Second Generation
  • Examples: Loratadine (Claritin), Desloratadine (Clarinex), Cetirizine (Zyrtec), Levocetirizine (Xyzal), Fexofenadine (Allegra).
  • Characteristics: Less lipophilic, minimal BBB penetration = less/non-sedating (Cetirizine/Levocetirizine may cause mild sedation in some). Minimal anticholinergic effects. Longer duration of action (usually once daily).
  • Use: Preferred oral agents for allergic rhinitis and chronic urticaria. Best taken regularly during allergy season for optimal effect.

Topical Antihistamines (Nasal Sprays, Eye Drops)

Azelastine (Astelin, Astepro - Nasal Spray)
  • H1 receptor antagonist nasal spray.
  • PK: Rapid onset (~15-30 minutes). Some systemic absorption (~40%).
  • A/E: Bitter taste (dysgeusia), headache, somnolence (can occur due to systemic absorption), nasal irritation.
  • Use: Allergic rhinitis (seasonal/perennial). Effective for nasal congestion, sneezing, itching, rhinorrhea. Often considered second-line after or add-on to nasal steroids.
Olopatadine (Patanase - Nasal Spray; Pataday/Pazeo - Eye Drops)
  • H1 receptor antagonist with mast cell stabilizing properties.
  • PK: Nasal spray onset ~30 min. Ocular drops onset within minutes. Lower systemic absorption than azelastine.
  • A/E: Nasal spray: Epistaxis (nosebleed), headache, altered taste. Eye drops: Local irritation, dry eye, headache.
  • Use: Nasal spray for allergic rhinitis. Eye drops highly effective for allergic conjunctivitis.

Glucocorticoids (Steroids)

MOA & Warnings
  • MOA: Broad anti-inflammatory effects. Bind intracellular glucocorticoid receptors → translocate to nucleus → alter gene transcription. Suppress synthesis of cytokines, chemokines, adhesion molecules. Inhibit phospholipase A2 (via lipocortin), reducing production of prostaglandins and leukotrienes. Decrease inflammatory cell recruitment (eosinophils, lymphocytes). Reduce vascular permeability.
  • Warnings (Systemic effects - dose/duration dependent): HPA axis suppression, Cushing's syndrome, growth suppression (children), osteoporosis, cataracts, glaucoma, hyperglycemia, immunosuppression, mood changes, skin thinning, impaired wound healing.
Topical (Intranasal) Steroids (INS)
  • Examples: Fluticasone propionate (Flonase), Fluticasone furoate (Flonase Sensimist), Mometasone (Nasonex), Budesonide (Rhinocort Aqua), Ciclesonide (Omnaris, Zetonna), Beclomethasone (Beconase AQ, Qnasl), Triamcinolone (Nasacort AQ).
  • Most effective single maintenance therapy for allergic rhinitis, particularly for nasal congestion.
  • Mechanism: Reduce local inflammation, eosinophils, mast cells, mediator release.
  • Onset: May take hours to days for initial effect, full effect after 1-2 weeks of regular use.
  • Bioavailability varies (lower with fluticasone furoate, mometasone, ciclesonide = potentially fewer systemic effects).
  • A/E (Local): Nasal dryness, burning, stinging, epistaxis (most common), headache. Rare: nasal septal perforation (improper technique). Systemic effects minimal at standard doses but possible with high doses/long term.
  • Use: First-line for moderate-to-severe or persistent allergic rhinitis. Also used for non-allergic rhinitis, nasal polyps.
Systemic Steroids

Oral or parenteral steroids (e.g., Prednisone) generally NOT used for routine allergic rhinitis due to systemic side effects. May be considered for short bursts (e.g., 5-7 days) for severe, refractory symptoms or severe nasal polyposis, often as bridge to other therapies.

Leukotriene Modifiers

Montelukast (Singulair)
  • MOA: Selective cysteinyl leukotriene receptor (CysLT1) antagonist. Blocks effects of LTC4, LTD4, LTE4.
  • Use: Allergic rhinitis (seasonal/perennial), asthma prophylaxis/maintenance. Oral tablet, once daily. Less effective than INS for rhinitis, especially congestion. May be useful add-on or alternative, particularly if comorbid asthma.
  • A/E: Generally well-tolerated. Headache, GI upset. **Neuropsychiatric events** (agitation, aggression, anxiety, depression, sleep disturbances, suicidal ideation) - **FDA Black Box Warning**. Rare: Churg-Strauss syndrome (eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis - potentially related to steroid withdrawal).

Mast Cell Stabilizers (Chromones)

Cromolyn Sodium (NasalCrom - Nasal Spray; Opticrom - Eye Drops)
  • MOA: Stabilizes mast cells, preventing degranulation and release of histamine/other mediators upon allergen exposure. May also inhibit neuronal reflexes.
  • PK: Poorly absorbed systemically.
  • Use: Prophylactic treatment of allergic rhinitis/conjunctivitis. Must be used regularly *before* exposure. Less effective than INS or antihistamines. Requires frequent dosing (3-4 times daily). Excellent safety profile.
  • A/E: Local irritation (nasal stinging, sneezing; eye burning).

Anticholinergic Agents

Ipratropium Bromide Nasal Spray (Atrovent Nasal)
  • MOA: Non-selective muscarinic antagonist. Blocks acetylcholine effects on nasal glands, reducing watery secretions.
  • Quaternary amine = poorly absorbed, minimal systemic effects, does not cross BBB.
  • Use: Primarily effective for reducing rhinorrhea (runny nose) associated with allergic or non-allergic (vasomotor) rhinitis. Little effect on sneezing, itching, or congestion.
  • Dosing: Usually 2 sprays per nostril 2-3 times daily.
  • A/E: Nasal dryness, epistaxis (common). Rare systemic anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention - use caution in glaucoma, BPH).

Decongestants (Alpha-Adrenergic Agonists)

Topical (Nasal Sprays/Drops)
  • MOA: Alpha-1 agonists cause vasoconstriction of nasal mucosal blood vessels, reducing swelling and congestion.
  • Examples: Oxymetazoline (Afrin), Phenylephrine (Neo-Synephrine), Naphazoline (Privine), Xylometazoline.
  • Characteristics: Rapid onset (seconds to minutes), highly effective for temporary congestion relief.
  • A/E: Local stinging/burning/dryness. **Rhinitis Medicamentosa (Rebound Congestion):** Occurs with use for > 3-5 consecutive days. Downregulation of alpha receptors leads to worsening congestion when spray stopped, prompting further use (vicious cycle).
  • Use: Short-term relief (≤ 3 days) of severe congestion (e.g., during URI, before air travel). Not for chronic rhinitis.
Systemic (Oral)
  • Examples: Pseudoephedrine (Sudafed - behind-the-counter), Phenylephrine (Sudafed PE - OTC).
  • Characteristics: Less intense, slower onset, longer duration than topical. Less rebound congestion.
  • A/E (Systemic): CNS stimulation (insomnia, anxiety, tremor), cardiovascular effects (tachycardia, palpitations, increased blood pressure), urinary retention (esp. BPH).
  • Contraindications/Cautions: Uncontrolled HTN, severe CAD, MAOI use within 14 days. Use caution in controlled HTN, heart disease, hyperthyroidism, diabetes, glaucoma, BPH. Phenylephrine generally less effective than pseudoephedrine orally due to high first-pass metabolism.
  • Use: Temporary relief of nasal congestion associated with URI or allergic rhinitis. Often found in combination cold/allergy products.

Immunotherapy

Omalizumab (Xolair - Anti-IgE)
  • MOA: Recombinant humanized monoclonal antibody that binds to free IgE, preventing it from binding to mast cells/basophils, reducing allergic response.
  • Use: Primarily approved for moderate-to-severe persistent allergic asthma inadequately controlled on ICS. Also approved for chronic idiopathic urticaria and nasal polyps. Sometimes used off-label for severe refractory allergic rhinitis.
  • Administration: Subcutaneous injection every 2-4 weeks (dose based on IgE level/weight). Expensive.
  • A/E: Injection site reactions. **Anaphylaxis** (Black Box Warning - can occur anytime). Potential small increased risk of cardiovascular events, malignancy (controversial).
Allergen Immunotherapy (AIT)

Process of administering gradually increasing doses of specific allergens to induce immune tolerance.

  • Types:
    • Subcutaneous Immunotherapy (SCIT): "Allergy shots". Administered in physician's office. Build-up phase then maintenance phase (3-5 years). Most established form.
    • Sublingual Immunotherapy (SLIT): Tablets or drops placed under tongue daily at home. Available for specific allergens (grass pollens, ragweed, dust mites).
  • Use: Moderate-severe allergic rhinitis/conjunctivitis and/or allergic asthma confirmed by IgE testing (skin or serum specific IgE) when symptoms poorly controlled by meds/avoidance, or patient prefers potential for long-term remission.
  • Mechanism: Shifts immune response from Th2 (allergic) towards Th1/Treg (tolerance), increases IgG4 blocking antibodies.
  • A/E: Local reactions common (itching/swelling at injection site or mouth). Systemic allergic reactions possible (esp. SCIT, requires observation period post-injection, patient must carry epinephrine auto-injector). Anaphylaxis rare but possible.

Surgery

Indications

Considered for structural issues or complications refractory to medical therapy:

  • Functional Endoscopic Sinus Surgery (FESS): For chronic rhinosinusitis (CRS) unresponsive to medical therapy, especially with nasal polyps or fungal sinusitis. Aims to enlarge sinus ostia, remove diseased tissue/polyps, improve drainage.
  • Septoplasty: Corrects deviated nasal septum causing significant obstruction.
  • Turbinate Reduction (Turbinoplasty): Reduces size of hypertrophied inferior turbinates causing obstruction refractory to medical therapy (e.g., INS).

Acute Rhinosinusitis (ARS) - Treatment Focused

Definition & Causes
  • Symptomatic inflammation of nasal cavity and paranasal sinuses lasting < 4 weeks.
  • Vast majority (~90-98%) caused by viruses (rhinovirus, influenza, parainfluenza, adenovirus).
  • Acute Bacterial Rhinosinusitis (ABRS) develops as complication in small percentage (~0.5-2%).
  • Common bacterial pathogens in ABRS:
    • Streptococcus pneumoniae (~30-40%)
    • Haemophilus influenzae (non-typeable) (~30-40%)
    • Moraxella catarrhalis (~10-20%)
    • Staphylococcus aureus, other Strep species, anaerobes (less common, more in chronic/odontogenic source).
Antibiotic Selection (for ABRS)

Antibiotics indicated only if criteria for ABRS met (persistent ≥10d, severe onset ≥3d, or worsening/"double sickening"). Observation ("watchful waiting") is an option for non-severe cases.

  • First-line Therapy (Standard Dose): Amoxicillin-clavulanate (Augmentin). Preferred over amoxicillin alone due to H. flu/M. cat resistance (beta-lactamase production).
  • High-Dose Amoxicillin-clavulanate (2g BID or 90mg/kg/day peds): Recommended if: Geographic region with high rates (>10%) of invasive penicillin-non-susceptible S. pneumoniae, severe infection (fever >39C, systemic illness), age >65, recent hospitalization, antibiotic use within past month, immunocompromise, daycare attendance (peds).
  • Penicillin Allergy:
    • Non-anaphylactic allergy: Oral 3rd gen cephalosporin (Cefixime, Cefpodoxime) + Clindamycin OR Doxycycline (adults/older children) OR Levofloxacin/Moxifloxacin (adults).
    • Anaphylactic allergy: Doxycycline OR Levofloxacin/Moxifloxacin. Clindamycin alone has poor G- coverage.
  • Duration: 5-7 days for uncomplicated ABRS in adults. 10-14 days for children or complicated cases.
Adjunctive Therapies
  • Intranasal Saline Irrigation: Recommended, helps clear secretions.
  • Intranasal Corticosteroids: Recommended, particularly if underlying allergic rhinitis. Reduces inflammation, may shorten duration/severity.
  • Analgesics/Antipyretics (Acetaminophen, Ibuprofen): For pain/fever relief.
  • Not Generally Recommended: Oral/Topical Decongestants (limited evidence, side effects), Antihistamines (unless underlying allergy, may thicken secretions). Antifungals only if fungal sinusitis confirmed.
Therapy for Non-Responders (Failure after 3-5 days)
  • Reassess diagnosis (could it be non-bacterial? Complication?).
  • Broaden antibiotic coverage / Switch class (e.g., switch from Augmentin to Respiratory Fluoroquinolone or vice versa). Consider resistant pathogens.
  • Obtain imaging (CT scan) to evaluate for complications (orbital/intracranial extension, abscess, mucocele) or anatomical issues.
  • Consider referral to ENT for sinus culture via endoscopy/puncture if refractory.

Acute Pharyngitis (Strep Throat) - Treatment Focused

General Info & Diagnosis
  • Most cases (~80-90% adults, ~70% children) are viral (adenovirus, rhinovirus, coronavirus, influenza, EBV, HSV, etc.).
  • Bacterial cause: Primarily Group A Beta-Hemolytic Streptococcus (GABHS / GAS / S. pyogenes). Others less common (Groups C/G Strep, Fusobacterium necrophorum - Lemierre's, N. gonorrhoeae, C. diphtheriae, Mycoplasma, Chlamydia).
  • Distinguishing Viral vs. GAS: Clinical criteria (Centor/McIsaac) help assess probability but are not definitive.
    • Centor Criteria: Fever (>38C), Absence of Cough, Tender Anterior Cervical Lymphadenopathy, Tonsillar Exudates. (Score 0-1 low risk, 2-3 intermediate, 4 high risk).
    • McIsaac adds Age: 3-14 yrs (+1), 15-44 yrs (0), ≥45 yrs (-1).
  • Testing: Recommended for scores ≥ 2-3.
    • Rapid Antigen Detection Test (RADT): High specificity (~95%), variable sensitivity (~70-90%).
    • Throat Culture: Gold standard. Perform if RADT negative in children/adolescents (due to ARF risk). Not needed if RADT negative in adults.
Complications of GABHS
  • Suppurative (Direct extension/spread): Peritonsillar abscess (PTA), Retropharyngeal abscess (RPA), Otitis Media, Sinusitis, Mastoiditis, Cervical Lymphadenitis, Bacteremia (rare).
  • Non-suppurative (Immune-mediated, delayed):
    • Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF): Occurs 2-4 weeks after untreated/inadequately treated GAS pharyngitis. Inflammatory disease affecting heart (carditis, valvular damage), joints (migratory polyarthritis), skin (erythema marginatum, subcutaneous nodules), CNS (Sydenham chorea). **Preventable with timely antibiotic treatment (within 9 days of onset)**.
    • Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN): Occurs 1-3 weeks after GAS pharyngitis OR skin infection (impetigo). Immune complex deposition in glomeruli → hematuria, edema, hypertension, renal insufficiency. **Not preventable with antibiotics**.
    • Scarlet Fever: Rash (sandpaper texture, spares palms/soles, Pastia's lines) + strawberry tongue, associated with GAS toxin.
    • PANDAS (Pediatric Autoimmune Neuropsychiatric Disorders Associated with Streptococcal infections): Controversial association with OCD/tics.
Treatment
  • Viral Pharyngitis: Symptomatic relief (analgesics, fluids, lozenges, rest).
  • GAS Pharyngitis: Antibiotics recommended to prevent ARF, shorten duration slightly, reduce transmission.
    • First-line: Penicillin V (oral, 10 days) or Amoxicillin (oral, 10 days - often preferred peds). Benzathine Penicillin G (IM, single dose) if adherence is a concern.
    • Penicillin Allergy:
      • Non-anaphylactic: Cephalexin or Cefadroxil (1st gen Ceph, 10 days).
      • Anaphylactic: Clindamycin (10 days) OR Macrolides (Azithromycin 5 days, Clarithromycin 10 days) - check local resistance.
  • Symptomatic relief alongside antibiotics.

Pharmacology: Drugs for Respiratory Infections

General Antibiotic Classes

Beta-lactams (Penicillins, Cephalosporins, Carbapenems, Monobactams)
  • MOA: Inhibit bacterial cell wall synthesis by binding to Penicillin-Binding Proteins (PBPs, transpeptidases), preventing peptidoglycan cross-linking. Generally Bactericidal. Time-dependent killing.
  • Resistance: Beta-lactamase enzymes (hydrolyze beta-lactam ring - overcome by adding inhibitor like clavulanate, sulbactam, tazobactam), altered PBPs (e.g., MRSA), decreased permeability.
  • A/E: Hypersensitivity reactions (most common - rash to anaphylaxis), GI upset, diarrhea, C. difficile colitis, seizures (esp. imipenem at high doses/renal failure), interstitial nephritis, hematologic effects (rare).
  • Spectrum varies widely:
    • Natural PCNs (Pen G, Pen V): Strep, some anaerobes.
    • Aminopenicillins (Amoxicillin, Ampicillin): Strep, Enterococcus, some GNRs (H. flu, E. coli - resistance common).
    • Anti-staphylococcal PCNs (Nafcillin, Oxacillin, Dicloxacillin): MSSA, Strep.
    • Extended-spectrum (Anti-pseudomonal) PCNs (Piperacillin): Broad GNR coverage including Pseudomonas, GPC, anaerobes. Usually combined with Tazobactam.
    • Cephalosporins (Generations 1-5): Increasing GNR coverage, decreasing GPC coverage (gen 1-3); Gen 4 (Cefepime) broad GPC/GNR including Pseudomonas; Gen 5 (Ceftaroline) covers MRSA.
    • Carbapenems (Imipenem, Meropenem, Ertapenem, Doripenem): Very broad spectrum (GPC, GNR including Pseudomonas [except Ertapenem], ESBL producers, anaerobes). Reserved for resistant infections.
    • Monobactams (Aztreonam): Gram-negative aerobic coverage ONLY (including Pseudomonas). No cross-reactivity with other beta-lactams (except ceftazidime) - useful in severe PCN allergy.
Macrolides
  • Examples: Azithromycin, Clarithromycin, Erythromycin.
  • MOA: Inhibit protein synthesis by binding reversibly to the 50S ribosomal subunit, blocking translocation. Generally Bacteriostatic.
  • Spectrum: Good coverage of "Atypical" pathogens (Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Chlamydia pneumoniae, Legionella pneumophila). Also covers some GPC (Strep pneumo - resistance increasing, GAS) and some GNRs (H. flu, M. catarrhalis). Azithromycin has best GNR activity.
  • A/E: GI distress (N/V/D - esp. Erythromycin due to motilin agonism), metallic taste (Clarithro), cholestatic hepatitis (rare), QT prolongation (risk of Torsades de Pointes), hypersensitivity.
  • Interactions: Erythromycin and Clarithromycin are potent CYP3A4 inhibitors (increase levels of warfarin, statins, etc.). Azithromycin has fewer interactions.
  • Use: CAP (esp. outpatient, or combination inpatient), Strep throat (PCN allergy), Pertussis, Chlamydia infections, MAC prophylaxis/treatment.
Clindamycin
  • MOA: Lincosamide class. Inhibits protein synthesis by binding to 50S ribosomal subunit (overlaps with macrolide site). Bacteriostatic.
  • Spectrum: Excellent activity against Gram-positive cocci (Strep, Staph including many CA-MRSA strains) and most Anaerobes (including above-diaphragm anaerobes like Peptostreptococcus, Fusobacterium; also B. fragilis). Poor GNR coverage.
  • A/E: High risk of Clostridioides difficile colitis (pseudomembranous colitis) - Black Box Warning. Diarrhea, rash, nausea.
  • Use: Skin/soft tissue infections (esp. if MRSA suspected), infections of oral cavity/head/neck, aspiration pneumonia, lung abscess, alternative for GPC infections in PCN-allergic patients, adjunct therapy for toxic shock syndrome (suppresses toxin production).
Sulfonamides & Trimethoprim (TMP-SMX)
  • MOA: Sequential blockade of folic acid synthesis. Sulfamethoxazole (SMX) inhibits dihydropteroate synthase (competes with PABA). Trimethoprim (TMP) inhibits dihydrofolate reductase. Bacteriostatic alone, Bactericidal in combination (TMP-SMX).
  • Spectrum: Broad coverage including many GPC (Staph including many MRSA, Strep pneumo - resistance varies), many GNRs (Enterobacterales - resistance increasing, H. flu, M. cat), *Nocardia*, *Stenotrophomonas maltophilia*, *Toxoplasma gondii*, *Pneumocystis jirovecii*. Poor anaerobic coverage.
  • A/E: Hypersensitivity reactions (rash, urticaria, photosensitivity, Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis - SJS/TEN), GI upset, bone marrow suppression (anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia - esp. with prolonged use/folate deficiency), hyperkalemia (TMP effect), crystalluria/renal damage (SMX - maintain hydration), hemolytic anemia (in G6PD deficiency).
  • Use: UTIs, PJP treatment and prophylaxis, Nocardiosis, Stenotrophomonas infections, skin/soft tissue infections (CA-MRSA), traveler's diarrhea, sinusitis/otitis (less common now due to resistance).
  • Contraindications: Sulfa allergy, G6PD deficiency, pregnancy (esp. 1st/3rd trimester - kernicterus risk), infants <2 months, megaloblastic anemia due to folate deficiency.
Tetracyclines
  • Examples: Doxycycline, Minocycline, Tetracycline. Tigecycline (glycylcycline derivative).
  • MOA: Inhibit protein synthesis by binding reversibly to the 30S ribosomal subunit, preventing tRNA binding. Bacteriostatic.
  • Spectrum: Broad, including "Atypicals" (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Rickettsia, Ehrlichia), Spirochetes (Borrelia - Lyme, Treponema - Syphilis), some GPC (Staph including some MRSA, Strep pneumo - resistance varies), some GNRs (H. flu, M. cat). Good activity against Plasmodium (malaria prophylaxis/tx).
  • A/E: GI upset (N/V/D, esophagitis - take with water, remain upright), photosensitivity, deposition in developing bones and teeth causing permanent tooth discoloration (contraindicated in pregnancy and children < 8 years), hepatotoxicity (rare), dizziness/vertigo (Minocycline).
  • Use: CAP (outpatient alternative/combo), skin/soft tissue infections (CA-MRSA, acne), tick-borne diseases (Lyme, RMSF, Ehrlichiosis), STIs (Chlamydia), malaria prophylaxis/treatment.
Fluoroquinolones (FQs)
  • Examples: Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin, Gemifloxacin, Delafloxacin.
  • MOA: Inhibit bacterial DNA replication by targeting DNA gyrase (topoisomerase II) and topoisomerase IV. Bactericidal. Concentration-dependent killing.
  • Spectrum:
    • Ciprofloxacin: Excellent GNR coverage including Pseudomonas. Weak GPC and atypical coverage.
    • "Respiratory Quinolones" (Levo, Moxi, Gemi): Enhanced GPC (esp. Strep pneumo) and atypical coverage compared to Cipro. Levo retains Pseudomonas activity; Moxi has better anaerobic activity but loses Pseudomonas. Gemi primarily respiratory pathogens.
    • Delafloxacin: Broadest; covers GPC (including MRSA), GNRs (including Pseudomonas), atypicals, anaerobes.
  • A/E: GI upset, CNS effects (dizziness, headache, confusion, hallucinations, seizures), tendinitis/tendon rupture (Achilles most common - Black Box Warning), peripheral neuropathy (can be permanent - BBW), QT prolongation (risk of Torsades - esp. Moxi), photosensitivity, dysglycemia (hyper/hypo), aortic aneurysm/dissection risk (BBW), C. difficile colitis, mental health side effects (BBW). Use should be reserved for situations where benefits outweigh risks.
  • Use: CAP (Resp FQs), HAP/VAP, complicated UTIs/pyelonephritis (Cipro/Levo), prostatitis, intra-abdominal infections (Moxi or others + Metronidazole), bone/joint infections, traveler's diarrhea (Cipro).
Aminoglycosides
  • Examples: Gentamicin, Tobramycin, Amikacin, Streptomycin, Plazomicin.
  • MOA: Inhibit protein synthesis by irreversibly binding to 30S ribosomal subunit, causing mRNA misreading and premature termination. Bactericidal. Concentration-dependent killing with post-antibiotic effect (PAE).
  • Spectrum: Excellent aerobic Gram-negative coverage (including Pseudomonas). Used synergistically with cell wall agents (beta-lactams or vancomycin) for serious Gram-positive infections (e.g., Staph/Enterococcal endocarditis). Streptomycin used for TB, Tularemia, Plague.
  • A/E: Nephrotoxicity (ATN, reversible), Ototoxicity (irreversible auditory/vestibular damage), neuromuscular blockade (rare, risk with rapid infusion or myasthenia gravis). Require therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM - peak/trough or AUC monitoring with extended-interval dosing).
  • Use: Serious GNR infections (sepsis, pneumonia - HAP/VAP, complicated UTI), often in combination therapy. Inhaled Tobramycin for CF.
Vancomycin
  • MOA: Glycopeptide. Inhibits cell wall synthesis by binding tightly to D-Alanyl-D-Alanine terminus of peptidoglycan precursors, blocking transglycosylation and transpeptidation. Bactericidal (slowly).
  • Spectrum: Gram-positive organisms ONLY, including MRSA, Methicillin-resistant S. epidermidis (MRSE), Enterococcus (not VRE), Penicillin-resistant Strep pneumo. Oral formulation (not absorbed) used ONLY for C. difficile colitis.
  • A/E: Nephrotoxicity (risk increases with higher troughs, duration, concomitant nephrotoxins), Ototoxicity (rare), Red Man Syndrome (infusion-related histamine release - flushing, itching, rash on face/neck/upper torso; slow infusion rate, premedicate with antihistamines), thrombocytopenia, neutropenia (rare). Requires TDM (trough levels typically targeted).
  • Use: Serious MRSA infections (bacteremia, endocarditis, pneumonia, osteomyelitis), empiric coverage for severe infections where MRSA suspected (e.g., severe CAP, HAP/VAP, sepsis), treatment of serious GPC infections in patients with severe beta-lactam allergy.
Linezolid
  • MOA: Oxazolidinone class. Unique mechanism - inhibits protein synthesis by binding to 50S ribosomal subunit near interface with 30S, preventing formation of the 70S initiation complex. Bacteriostatic (bactericidal vs Strep).
  • Spectrum: Gram-positive organisms ONLY, including MRSA, VRE (both E. faecalis and E. faecium), Penicillin-resistant Strep pneumo. Also covers Nocardia, some Mycobacteria.
  • A/E: Bone marrow suppression (thrombocytopenia, anemia, leukopenia - esp. with use > 2 weeks, requires monitoring), peripheral neuropathy and optic neuropathy (with prolonged use > 28 days, can be irreversible), lactic acidosis (rare), nausea, diarrhea. Weak, reversible, non-selective MAOI activity - risk of serotonin syndrome if co-administered with SSRIs, SNRIs, other serotonergic agents (requires careful monitoring/avoidance).
  • Use: Pneumonia (HAP/VAP) caused by MRSA, complicated skin/soft tissue infections (MRSA), VRE infections. Good oral bioavailability (IV to PO switch easy).

Specific Infection Management Notes

Diphtheria
  • Agent: Corynebacterium diphtheriae (toxigenic strains).
  • Key feature: Pharyngeal pseudomembranes (gray, adherent), sore throat, fever, potential for airway obstruction, myocarditis, neuropathy due to exotoxin.
  • Tx: Diphtheria Antitoxin (equine) to neutralize unbound toxin + Antibiotics (Penicillin G IV/IM or Erythromycin PO/IV x 14 days) to eradicate bacteria. Respiratory isolation.
Acute Bronchitis
  • Inflammation of large airways, usually viral (>90%). Presents with cough (+/- sputum, often follows URI), lasts 1-3 weeks. Wheezing may occur.
  • Tx: Primarily supportive (rest, fluids, antitussives if cough severe/disruptive - limited evidence). Bronchodilators (e.g., albuterol) if wheezing present.
  • Antibiotics are NOT recommended for uncomplicated acute bronchitis as cause is usually viral. Consider antibiotics only if pertussis suspected or high suspicion of bacterial pneumonia develops.
  • Antivirals (Oseltamivir, Zanamivir, Baloxavir) only if Influenza confirmed/suspected within 48h of onset during flu season.
Pneumonia - General Tx Principles

See Microbiology Table 2 for specific pathogen/syndrome treatments.

  • Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP):
    • Severity assessment (CURB-65, PSI) guides site of care (outpatient vs inpatient vs ICU).
    • Empiric therapy targets most likely pathogens (S. pneumo, atypicals, H. flu, viruses).
    • Duration typically 5-7 days if clinically improving.
  • Hospital-acquired pneumonia (HAP) / Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP):
    • Higher risk of resistant pathogens (MRSA, Pseudomonas, ESBLs, Acinetobacter).
    • Empiric therapy based on patient risk factors for MDR organisms and local antibiogram. Often requires broad-spectrum coverage initially.
    • De-escalate therapy based on culture results. Duration typically 7 days.
  • CURB-65 Score (1 point each):
    • Confusion (New onset)
    • Urea > 7 mmol/L (BUN > 19 mg/dL)
    • Respiratory rate ≥ 30 breaths/min
    • Blood pressure (Systolic < 90 mmHg or Diastolic ≤ 60 mmHg)
    • Age ≥ 65 years
    • Score 0-1: Outpatient; Score 2: Consider Inpatient; Score ≥ 3: Inpatient (often ICU).
Chronic Bronchitis Exacerbation (AECOPD)
  • Acute worsening of respiratory symptoms (dyspnea, cough, sputum) beyond normal day-to-day variation. Often triggered by infection (viral or bacterial) or environmental factors.
  • Tx: Optimize bronchodilators (SABA +/- SAMA). Systemic corticosteroids (e.g., Prednisone 40mg x 5 days). Supplemental O2. Consider non-invasive ventilation if respiratory failure.
  • Antibiotics: Recommended if patient has increased sputum purulence PLUS either increased dyspnea or increased sputum volume (2 of 3 Cardinal Symptoms - Anthonisen criteria), OR if requiring mechanical ventilation.
    • Target likely pathogens (H. flu, S. pneumo, M. cat).
    • Choice depends on local resistance, severity, risk factors (e.g., Amox/Clav, Doxycycline, Azithromycin, Levofloxacin). Duration 5-7 days.
Pertussis (Whooping Cough)
  • Agent: Bordetella pertussis.
  • Tx: Primarily supportive. Antibiotics decrease transmission and may shorten duration if started early (catarrhal stage).
    • First-line: Macrolides (Azithromycin preferred, esp. infants <1mo; Clarithromycin; Erythromycin).
    • Alternative (>2mo): TMP-SMX.
  • Treat patient AND provide post-exposure prophylaxis to close contacts.
Adjunctive Therapies for Cough
  • Antitussives (cough suppressants):
    • Centrally acting: Dextromethorphan (OTC), Codeine/Hydrocodone (opioids, prescription). Limited evidence for acute cough, potential side effects (sedation, constipation). May help chronic cough or sleep disruption.
    • Peripherally acting: Benzonatate (Tessalon Perles).
  • Expectorants (e.g., Guaifenesin): Questionable efficacy for acute cough.
  • Honey (for children > 1 year): Some evidence for nighttime cough relief.
  • Bronchodilators: Only if associated wheezing/bronchospasm.

Antifungal / Anti-TB / Anti-Parasitic / Other

Pulmonary TB
  • Standard initial phase (2 months): RIPE (Rifampin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol).
  • Continuation phase (4 months): INH + RIF.
  • Monitor for toxicity: Hepatotoxicity (INH, RIF, PZA), Optic neuritis (EMB - check vision/color), Peripheral neuropathy (INH - give Pyridoxine/B6 concurrently), Hyperuricemia (PZA), Drug interactions (esp. RIF - potent CYP inducer).
Nocardiosis
  • Caused by Nocardia species (Gram-positive, branching, filamentous, weakly acid-fast bacteria). Opportunistic infection, often pulmonary, can disseminate (brain abscess common).
  • Tx: TMP-SMX is cornerstone. Duration is long (months). Severe/disseminated disease often requires combination therapy initially (e.g., TMP-SMX + Amikacin + Imipenem/Ceftriaxone). Tailor based on species/susceptibility.
Pneumocystis jirovecii Pneumonia (PJP)
  • Fungal pneumonia, primarily in immunocompromised (HIV with CD4 < 200, chronic steroids, transplant, chemotherapy).
  • Sx: Subacute onset fever, dry cough, progressive dyspnea. Hypoxia often out of proportion to CXR findings (diffuse bilateral interstitial/ground-glass opacities). Elevated LDH common.
  • Dx: Organism identification in sputum (induced), BAL fluid, or biopsy (GMS or Giemsa stain, immunofluorescence).
  • Tx/Prophylaxis: TMP-SMX is first-line (high dose for treatment, lower dose for prophylaxis).
    • Alternatives (Treatment): Clindamycin + Primaquine (check G6PD); Pentamidine IV (toxic); Atovaquone (mild-mod disease). Add corticosteroids if moderate-severe hypoxemia (PaO2 < 70 or A-a gradient > 35).
    • Alternatives (Prophylaxis): Dapsone, Atovaquone, Pentamidine inhaled.
Histoplasmosis
  • Dimorphic fungus (H. capsulatum), endemic to Ohio/Mississippi River Valleys. Spores inhaled from soil contaminated with bird/bat droppings.
  • Spectrum: Asymptomatic (~90%), Acute pulmonary (flu-like), Chronic cavitary pulmonary (resembles TB), Disseminated (immunocompromised). Mediastinal granuloma/fibrosis.
  • Tx: Mild/Moderate acute/chronic pulmonary: Itraconazole (PO, months). Severe pulmonary / Disseminated: Lipid formulation Amphotericin B (IV) initially, followed by long course of Itraconazole.
Coccidioidomycosis
  • Dimorphic fungus (C. immitis/posadasii), endemic to SW US ("Valley Fever"). Spores (arthroconidia) inhaled from soil.
  • Spectrum: Asymptomatic (~60%), Acute pulmonary ("Valley Fever" - flu-like, rash, arthralgias), Persistent pulmonary nodules/cavities, Disseminated (skin, bone, CNS - meningitis).
  • Tx: Mild/Moderate pulmonary: Often no treatment needed in immunocompetent. Severe/progressive pulmonary or Disseminated (non-meningeal): Fluconazole or Itraconazole (PO, long duration). Meningitis: Fluconazole (high dose, lifelong). Very severe/refractory: Amphotericin B.
Blastomycosis
  • Dimorphic fungus (B. dermatitidis), endemic to Ohio/Mississippi River Valleys, Great Lakes, SE US. Spores inhaled from soil/decaying wood.
  • Spectrum: Pulmonary (acute/chronic, can mimic pneumonia/mass), Skin (verrucous/ulcerated lesions), Bone, GU, CNS.
  • Tx: Mild/Moderate pulmonary/non-CNS disseminated: Itraconazole (PO, 6-12 months). Severe pulmonary / CNS / Immunocompromised: Lipid formulation Amphotericin B (IV) initially, followed by step-down to Itraconazole.
Cryptococcosis
  • Encapsulated yeast (Cryptococcus neoformans/gattii). Found in soil, pigeon droppings. Inhalation leads to pulmonary infection +/- dissemination (predilection for CNS - meningitis). Primarily affects immunocompromised (esp. HIV).
  • Tx: Pulmonary (mild/mod, immunocompetent): Fluconazole (PO, 6-12 months). Severe pulmonary or CNS disease (Meningitis): Induction therapy with Lipid formulation Amphotericin B + Flucytosine (IV, 2+ weeks), followed by consolidation (Fluconazole high dose, 8+ weeks), then maintenance (Fluconazole lower dose, ≥1 year or until immune reconstitution). Requires lumbar punctures to manage ICP.
Mucormycosis
  • Invasive fungal infection caused by molds in order Mucorales (e.g., Rhizopus, Mucor, Lichtheimia). Angioinvasive, rapidly progressive, high mortality. Risk factors: Diabetes (esp. DKA), immunosuppression (neutropenia, transplant), iron overload, trauma/burns.
  • Forms: Rhino-orbital-cerebral (most common), Pulmonary, Cutaneous, GI, Disseminated.
  • Tx: Requires aggressive approach: Surgical debridement of necrotic tissue + High-dose lipid formulation Amphotericin B (IV). Step-down/salvage therapy: Posaconazole or Isavuconazole. Control underlying condition (e.g., hyperglycemia, neutropenia).
Aspergillosis
  • Caused by Aspergillus species (ubiquitous mold). Spectrum depends on host immunity/lung status.
    • Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA): Hypersensitivity reaction in asthma/CF patients. Wheezing, eosinophilia, high IgE, infiltrates. Tx: Corticosteroids + Itraconazole.
    • Aspergilloma (Fungus Ball): Colonization of pre-existing lung cavity (e.g., old TB cavity). Often asymptomatic or hemoptysis. Tx: Observation, surgery if severe hemoptysis (antifungals ineffective).
    • Chronic Pulmonary Aspergillosis (CPA): Subacute/chronic infection in patients with underlying lung disease. Cavities, fibrosis, nodules. Tx: Long-term oral azoles (Voriconazole, Itraconazole).
    • Invasive Pulmonary Aspergillosis (IPA): Life-threatening infection in severely immunocompromised (esp. neutropenia, transplant). Angioinvasion, necrosis, hemorrhage. Halo sign/air crescent on CT. Tx: Voriconazole (drug of choice). Alternatives: Isavuconazole, Lipid Ampho B, Echinocandins (salvage).
Candidiasis
  • Caused by Candida species (yeast, normal flora). Pulmonary infection rare, usually represents colonization or hematogenous spread (invasive candidiasis) in severely ill/immunocompromised.
  • Tx (Invasive/Pneumonia): Echinocandins (e.g., Caspofungin, Micafungin) or Fluconazole (if susceptible species like C. albicans and patient stable). Amphotericin B for severe/resistant cases.
Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome (SARS) / Avian Influenza
  • SARS (SARS-CoV-1): Coronavirus outbreak (2002-2003). Severe viral pneumonia/ARDS. No specific proven antiviral therapy. Supportive care crucial.
  • Avian Influenza (e.g., H5N1, H7N9): Sporadic human infections from poultry contact. High mortality rate. Supportive care. Neuraminidase inhibitors (Oseltamivir) recommended early, but efficacy uncertain/resistance possible.
Misc Pharm Facts
  • Tetracyclines: Contraindicated in pregnancy (Category D) and children < 8 years old due to permanent tooth discoloration and potential bone deposition.
  • Severe IgE-mediated beta-lactam allergy (anaphylaxis): Avoid all penicillins, cephalosporins (cross-reactivity ~1-10%), carbapenems (except maybe meropenem in specific cases). Safe alternative for Gram-negative coverage: Aztreonam (monobactam - structurally different, very low cross-reactivity). Use other classes (FQ, AG, Vanc, Linezolid, Clinda, TMP-SMX, etc.) based on suspected pathogen.

Pharmacology: Drugs Used in Asthma and/or COPD

General Asthma Concepts

  • Chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways characterized by reversible bronchoconstriction, airway hyperresponsiveness (AHR), and airway inflammation (esp. eosinophilic).
  • Pathophysiology involves complex interplay of genetics, environment, immune cells (mast cells, eosinophils, Th2 lymphocytes), mediators (histamine, leukotrienes, cytokines like IL-4, IL-5, IL-13), and structural changes (airway remodeling).
  • Goals of therapy: Achieve and maintain control of symptoms, prevent exacerbations, maintain normal activity levels, maintain normal (or near-normal) lung function, minimize adverse effects of treatment.

Sympathomimetic Agents (Bronchodilators)

MOA

Activate Beta-2 adrenergic receptors on airway smooth muscle. This activates adenylyl cyclase via Gs protein coupling, increasing intracellular cyclic AMP (cAMP). Increased cAMP leads to protein kinase A activation, phosphorylation of target proteins, decreased intracellular calcium, and ultimately smooth muscle relaxation (bronchodilation). Beta-2 agonists may also inhibit mediator release from mast cells and decrease microvascular leakage.

Non-Selective / Older Agents
  • Epinephrine: Potent, rapid-acting non-selective agonist (α1, α2, β1, β2). Causes bronchodilation (β2), but also significant cardiac stimulation (β1 - tachycardia, arrhythmias) and vasoconstriction (α1). Use now limited to anaphylaxis treatment (IM injection). Inhaled forms (Primatene Mist) previously OTC but removed/reformulated (less effective).
  • Ephedrine: Mixed-acting (direct/indirect agonist). Longer duration, oral bioavailability, more CNS effects than epinephrine. Obsolete for respiratory use.
  • Isoproterenol: Potent non-selective β-agonist (β1=β2). Effective bronchodilator but significant cardiac side effects (tachycardia, arrhythmias). Largely replaced by selective β2 agonists.

Selective Beta-2 Agonists

Short-Acting Beta Agonists (SABA)
  • "Reliever" or "Rescue" medications. Rapid onset (minutes), duration 4-6 hours. Used for acute symptom relief in asthma and COPD.
  • Albuterol (Salbutamol outside US) (Ventolin, ProAir, Proventil): Most common SABA. Available as MDI, DPI, nebulizer solution, oral syrup/tablets (oral less preferred).
  • Levalbuterol (Xopenex): R-enantiomer of albuterol. Claimed fewer side effects (less β1 activity), but clinical significance debated, more expensive. MDI, nebulizer solution.
  • Terbutaline (Brethine): Available as tablet, injection (SubQ for severe exacerbations). Inhaler less common.
  • Metaproterenol / Pirbuterol: Older SABAs, less β2-selective, largely replaced.
  • A/E (Dose-related): Tremor (most common, β2 effect on skeletal muscle), tachycardia/palpitations (some β1 activity, reflex tachycardia), nervousness, headache, hypokalemia (β2 drives K+ into cells), hyperglycemia. Tolerance (tachyphylaxis) can develop with frequent use. Over-reliance indicates poor underlying control.
Long-Acting Beta Agonists (LABA)
  • "Controller" medications. Duration ≥ 12 hours (Formoterol, Salmeterol, Vilanterol, Olodaterol) or 24 hours (Indacaterol). Provide sustained bronchodilation.
  • Asthma Use: **NEVER use as monotherapy** due to increased risk of asthma-related death (masked inflammation). **MUST be combined with an Inhaled Corticosteroid (ICS)** in a combination inhaler or as separate inhalers. Used for step 3+ therapy in persistent asthma.
  • COPD Use: Can be used as monotherapy or in combination with LAMA and/or ICS for maintenance treatment.
  • Examples:
    • Salmeterol (Serevent): Slow onset. DPI. Combo w/ Fluticasone (Advair).
    • Formoterol (Foradil, Perforomist): Faster onset than Salmeterol (can be used in some reliever strategies with ICS). DPI, Nebulizer. Combo w/ Budesonide (Symbicort), Mometasone (Dulera).
    • Arformoterol (Brovana): R-enantiomer of formoterol. Nebulizer solution for COPD.
    • Indacaterol (Arcapta): Ultra-LABA (24h). DPI for COPD. Combo w/ Glycopyrrolate (Utibron).
    • Olodaterol (Striverdi): Ultra-LABA (24h). Respimat inhaler for COPD. Combo w/ Tiotropium (Stiolto).
    • Vilanterol: Ultra-LABA (24h). Only available in combination inhalers. DPI. Combos: w/ Fluticasone furoate (Breo - Asthma/COPD), Umeclidinium (Anoro - COPD), Umeclidinium + Fluticasone (Trelegy - Asthma/COPD).
  • A/E: Similar to SABAs (tremor, tachycardia, hypokalemia), but risk minimized with inhaled route. QT prolongation possible. Risk in asthma monotherapy noted above.

Corticosteroids

MOA & General Effects

Potent anti-inflammatory agents. Do NOT directly relax airway smooth muscle (not bronchodilators).

  • MOA: Bind intracellular glucocorticoid receptors → complex translocates to nucleus → alters gene transcription:
    • ↑ Synthesis of anti-inflammatory proteins (e.g., lipocortin-1, annexin A1).
    • ↓ Synthesis of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, TNF-α, IL-4, IL-5, IL-13), chemokines, adhesion molecules.
    • Inhibit phospholipase A2 → ↓ production of prostaglandins and leukotrienes.
    • Reduce inflammatory cell infiltration/activation (eosinophils, T-lymphocytes, mast cells, macrophages).
    • Reduce microvascular permeability.
    • Over time, reduce airway hyperresponsiveness.
  • Use in Asthma: Cornerstone of long-term control for persistent asthma.
  • Use in COPD: Reduce frequency/severity of exacerbations in patients with history of exacerbations, often used in combination with LABA +/- LAMA. Do not slow long-term decline in FEV1.
Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS)
  • Deliver drug directly to airways, minimizing systemic exposure/side effects. Preferred route for long-term asthma/COPD control.
  • Examples: Beclomethasone (Qvar), Budesonide (Pulmicort), Ciclesonide (Alvesco), Flunisolide (Aerospan), Fluticasone propionate (Flovent), Fluticasone furoate (Arnuity, in Breo, Trelegy), Mometasone (Asmanex, in Dulera).
  • Available as MDI, DPI, Nebulizer solution (Budesonide).
  • Dosed based on potency (low, medium, high dose).
  • A/E (Local): Oropharyngeal candidiasis (thrush) - prevent by rinsing mouth/gargling after use, using spacer device with MDI. Dysphonia (hoarseness). Cough.
  • A/E (Systemic - higher doses/long term): Potential for adrenal suppression, decreased bone mineral density, skin thinning/bruising, cataracts, glaucoma, decreased growth velocity in children (small effect, generally final adult height unaffected). Risk lower than with oral steroids.
  • Ciclesonide: Prodrug activated by esterases in lung, potentially fewer local/systemic effects.
Systemic Corticosteroids (Oral/IV)
  • Examples: Prednisone, Prednisolone, Methylprednisolone, Dexamethasone.
  • Use:
    • Acute Exacerbations (Asthma/COPD): Short course ("burst") typically 5-10 days (e.g., Prednisone 40-60mg/day adults) to rapidly reduce inflammation. Tapering usually not needed for courses < 2 weeks.
    • Severe Persistent Asthma/COPD: Long-term daily or alternate-day therapy for patients refractory to maximal inhaled therapy (use lowest effective dose).
  • A/E (Systemic): Numerous and significant, especially with long-term use. See MOA/Warnings section above (Cushingoid features, hyperglycemia, osteoporosis, mood changes, immunosuppression, etc.).

Leukotriene Modifiers

Receptor Antagonists (LTRAs) & Synthesis Inhibitors

Target the effects of cysteinyl leukotrienes (LTC4, LTD4, LTE4), which cause bronchoconstriction, mucus secretion, and airway inflammation.

  • Use: Primarily as alternative or add-on therapy for mild-to-moderate persistent asthma, especially useful in patients with concurrent allergic rhinitis or exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB). Less effective than ICS as monotherapy for asthma control. Oral administration.
  • Montelukast (Singulair): CysLT1 receptor antagonist. Once daily dosing. Generally well-tolerated but carries **FDA Black Box Warning** for neuropsychiatric events (agitation, depression, suicidality).
  • Zafirlukast (Accolate): CysLT1 receptor antagonist. BID dosing. Associated with rare but potentially fatal hepatotoxicity (requires monitoring). Drug interactions (CYP2C9 inhibitor). Rarely used.
  • Zileuton (Zyflo): Inhibits 5-lipoxygenase enzyme, blocking synthesis of LTB4 and cysteinyl LTs. Requires QID or BID (ER) dosing. Potential for hepatotoxicity (requires LFT monitoring). Drug interactions (CYP1A2, 3A4 inhibitor). Rarely used due to inconvenience and toxicity profile.

Phosphodiesterase Inhibitors

Theophylline (Methylxanthine)
  • Older bronchodilator, largely replaced by safer/more effective agents.
  • MOA: Non-selective phosphodiesterase (PDE) inhibitor → ↑cAMP → bronchodilation. Also adenosine receptor antagonism, histone deacetylase (HDAC) activation (anti-inflammatory effects).
  • Effects: Bronchodilation, increased diaphragmatic contractility, mild CNS stimulation, cardiac stimulation (tachycardia, arrhythmias), increased gastric acid secretion, weak diuretic.
  • Use: Historically used for asthma/COPD maintenance (oral) and acute exacerbations (IV aminophylline). Now considered alternative/add-on therapy only.
  • PK: Narrow therapeutic index (target 5-15 mcg/mL). Metabolism varies widely (CYP1A2, 3A4), affected by age, smoking, diseases, many drug interactions. Requires TDM.
  • A/E (Toxicity - dose related): N/V/D, headache, insomnia, anxiety (at therapeutic levels). Seizures and fatal arrhythmias (at levels > 20-25 mcg/mL).
Roflumilast (Daliresp) (PDE-4 Inhibitor)
  • MOA: Selective inhibitor of PDE4 (predominant PDE in inflammatory cells like neutrophils, eosinophils, macrophages). Increases intracellular cAMP in these cells, reducing inflammatory mediator release. Primarily anti-inflammatory, minimal bronchodilation.
  • Use: Oral agent approved to reduce risk of COPD exacerbations in patients with severe COPD associated with chronic bronchitis and a history of frequent exacerbations. Not for acute relief.
  • A/E: Common: Diarrhea, nausea, headache, weight loss, insomnia. Neuropsychiatric effects (anxiety, depression, suicidality) reported.

Antimuscarinic Agents (Anticholinergics)

MOA & General Effects

Block the effects of acetylcholine on muscarinic receptors (primarily M3) on airway smooth muscle and submucosal glands. This inhibits vagally-mediated bronchoconstriction and reduces mucus secretion.

More potent bronchodilators in COPD (where vagal tone is a major component of bronchoconstriction) than in asthma.

Administered via inhalation (MDI, DPI, nebulizer) to minimize systemic effects.

A/E (Inhaled): Usually minimal. Most common is dry mouth. Less common: metallic taste, urinary retention (caution in BPH), blurred vision/precipitation of glaucoma (if sprayed in eyes), constipation, tachycardia (rare).

Short-Acting Muscarinic Antagonist (SAMA)
  • Ipratropium Bromide (Atrovent):
    • Non-selective muscarinic antagonist. Quaternary amine (poor absorption).
    • Duration ~4-8 hours. Dosed QID usually.
    • Use: COPD maintenance (less common now with LAMAs available). Preferred anticholinergic for acute asthma exacerbations (added to SABA in ED, usually for first few doses). Also used for rhinitis (nasal spray). Available as MDI, nebulizer solution, nasal spray. Combination with Albuterol (Combivent MDI, DuoNeb nebulizer).
Long-Acting Muscarinic Antagonist (LAMA)
  • "Controller" medication, usually once or twice daily dosing. Cornerstone of COPD maintenance therapy. Also used as add-on therapy in severe asthma.
  • Tiotropium (Spiriva):
    • First LAMA, long duration (24h). Kinetically selective for M1/M3 over M2.
    • Use: COPD maintenance (reduces symptoms and exacerbations). Add-on therapy for moderate-severe Asthma (age 6+) inadequately controlled on ICS/LABA.
    • Available as HandiHaler (DPI - capsule) or Respimat (soft mist inhaler).
  • Aclidinium (Tudorza Pressair): BID dosing DPI for COPD.
  • Umeclidinium (Incruse Ellipta): Once-daily DPI for COPD. Available in combo with Vilanterol (Anoro) or Fluticasone/Vilanterol (Trelegy).
  • Glycopyrrolate (Seebri Neohaler, Lonhala Magnair): BID DPI or nebulizer solution for COPD. Available in combo with Indacaterol (Utibron) or Formoterol (Bevespi). (Note: Glycopyrrolate is also used systemically as antisialagogue).
  • Revefenacin (Yupelri): Once-daily nebulized LAMA for COPD.

Monoclonal Antibodies (Biologics) - Primarily for Severe Asthma

Anti-IgE Monoclonal Antibody
  • Omalizumab (Xolair):
    • MOA: Binds free IgE, prevents binding to FcεR1 on mast cells/basophils.
    • Use: Moderate-to-severe persistent allergic asthma (age 6+) with elevated IgE levels and sensitivity to perennial aeroallergen, inadequately controlled on ICS. Also for chronic idiopathic urticaria, nasal polyps.
    • Admin: SubQ injection every 2-4 weeks.
    • A/E: Injection site reactions, **Anaphylaxis** (BBW), possible small increased risk CV events/malignancy (controversial).
Anti-IL-5 / Anti-IL-5 Receptor Monoclonals

Target Interleukin-5 (key cytokine for eosinophil development/survival/activation).

  • Use: Add-on maintenance for severe eosinophilic asthma (age varies by agent, typically ≥6 or ≥12 yrs) with elevated blood eosinophil counts.
  • Mepolizumab (Nucala): Binds IL-5. SubQ every 4 weeks.
  • Reslizumab (Cinqair): Binds IL-5. IV infusion every 4 weeks. Higher risk of anaphylaxis.
  • Benralizumab (Fasenra): Binds IL-5 Receptor Alpha (IL-5Rα) on eosinophils → ADCC causes eosinophil depletion. SubQ every 4 weeks x3, then every 8 weeks.
  • A/E: Headache, injection site reactions, fatigue. Anaphylaxis rare. Possible increased herpes zoster risk (Mepo).
Anti-IL-4 Receptor Alpha Monoclonal
  • Dupilumab (Dupixent):
    • MOA: Blocks IL-4 Receptor Alpha subunit, inhibiting signaling of both IL-4 and IL-13 (key Th2 cytokines involved in IgE production, eosinophil recruitment, mucus production, AHR).
    • Use: Add-on maintenance for moderate-to-severe eosinophilic asthma OR oral steroid-dependent asthma (age 6+). Also for atopic dermatitis, chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps, eosinophilic esophagitis.
    • Admin: SubQ injection every 2 weeks.
    • A/E: Injection site reactions, conjunctivitis, oral herpes, eosinophilia (transient).
Anti-TSLP Monoclonal
  • Tezepelumab (Tezspire):
    • MOA: Blocks Thymic Stromal Lymphopoietin (TSLP), an upstream epithelial cytokine ("alarmin") that initiates multiple downstream inflammatory cascades (including Th2/eosinophilic and non-eosinophilic pathways).
    • Use: Add-on maintenance for severe asthma (age 12+) regardless of eosinophil count or allergic status.
    • Admin: SubQ injection every 4 weeks.
    • A/E: Pharyngitis, arthralgia, back pain, injection site reactions.

Mast Cell Stabilizers

Cromolyn Sodium & Nedocromil
  • MOA: Poorly understood, thought to stabilize mast cells by inhibiting chloride channels? Prevents release of histamine/other mediators. Anti-inflammatory, not a bronchodilator.
  • Use: Prophylactic therapy for mild persistent asthma or EIB. Requires regular use (QID) before exposure/exercise. Much less effective than ICS. Excellent safety profile.
  • Formulations: Nebulizer solution (Cromolyn), MDI (Nedocromil - discontinued in US). Also nasal spray/eye drops for allergy.
  • A/E: Throat irritation, cough, unpleasant taste.

Asthma Management Approach (Simplified Steps - GINA/NHLBI Influence)

Stepwise Therapy

Goal: Achieve control using lowest effective dose. Step up if uncontrolled, step down if well-controlled for ≥3 months.

  1. Step 1 (Infrequent symptoms <2x/month): As-needed low-dose ICS-Formoterol (preferred reliever) OR As-needed SABA + take low-dose ICS whenever SABA used.
  2. Step 2 (Symptoms ≥2x/month but not daily): Daily low-dose ICS OR As-needed low-dose ICS-Formoterol (preferred reliever). Alt: LTRA.
  3. Step 3 (Symptoms most days, or waking with asthma ≥1x/week): Low-dose ICS + LABA (combination inhaler preferred). Alt: Medium-dose ICS or Low-dose ICS + LTRA.
  4. Step 4 (Symptoms most days, waking ≥1x/week, low lung function): Medium-dose ICS + LABA. Alt: High-dose ICS + LABA, may add Tiotropium or LTRA.
  5. Step 5 (Symptoms uncontrolled on Step 4): High-dose ICS + LABA. Refer for specialist assessment +/- Add-on therapy (e.g., Tiotropium, Anti-IgE, Anti-IL5/5R, Anti-IL4Rα, Anti-TSLP). Consider low-dose oral corticosteroids (minimize use).

All steps include SABA for quick relief if not using ICS-Formoterol as reliever. Education, trigger avoidance, action plan are crucial at all steps.

Asthma Exacerbation Management (Status Asthmaticus)
  • Assess severity (PEFR, SpO2, RR, accessory muscle use, speech).
  • Oxygen: Titrate to maintain SpO2 93-95% (adults), 94-98% (children).
  • SABA: High doses via MDI+spacer or nebulizer, frequently (q20min x 3 doses then hourly or continuous).
  • SAMA (Ipratropium): Add to SABA for initial treatment in ED for moderate-severe exacerbations.
  • Systemic Corticosteroids: Oral (Prednisone/Prednisolone) or IV (Methylprednisolone) given early for all but mildest exacerbations. Continue for 5-7 days typically.
  • Adjuncts (Severe/Refractory): IV Magnesium Sulfate, Heliox, Non-invasive ventilation (BiPAP/CPAP), Intubation/Mechanical Ventilation if impending/actual respiratory failure.

COPD Management Approach (Simplified - GOLD 2023 Influence)

Assessment & Initial Therapy

Assessment considers: 1) Spirometry confirmation (FEV1/FVC < 0.7 post-bronchodilator); 2) Symptom burden (CAT score or mMRC dyspnea scale); 3) Exacerbation history.

Initial Pharmacotherapy based on GOLD Group (A, B, E):

  • Group A (Low risk, fewer symptoms: mMRC 0-1, CAT <10, 0-1 mod exacerbation not leading to hospital admission): A Bronchodilator (SABA or SAMA PRN, or LABA or LAMA scheduled).
  • Group B (Low risk, more symptoms: mMRC ≥2, CAT ≥10, 0-1 mod exacerbation not leading to hospital admission): Long-acting Bronchodilator (LAMA or LABA).
  • Group E (High risk: ≥2 mod exacerbations OR ≥1 leading to hospital admission): Start with LAMA + LABA combination therapy. (Consider LAMA+LABA+ICS if blood eosinophils ≥ 300 cells/μL).
Follow-up & Escalation

Adjust therapy based on predominant treatable trait (Dyspnea or Exacerbations):

  • Persistent Dyspnea:
    • On LABA or LAMA → Escalate to LAMA + LABA.
    • On LAMA + LABA → Consider switching device/molecules, investigate/treat other causes of dyspnea.
  • Persistent Exacerbations:
    • On LABA or LAMA → Escalate to LAMA + LABA.
    • On LAMA + LABA:
      • If Blood Eosinophils ≥ 300 → Add ICS (LAMA+LABA+ICS).
      • If Blood Eosinophils < 300 → Consider adding Roflumilast (if FEV1 <50% & chronic bronchitis) OR Azithromycin (long-term, esp. former smokers).
    • On LAMA+LABA+ICS: Consider adding Roflumilast or Azithromycin. Consider de-escalating ICS if no benefit/pneumonia risk (esp. if eos <100).

Other therapies: Smoking cessation, vaccinations (flu, pneumococcal, pertussis, COVID-19), pulmonary rehabilitation, oxygen therapy (if chronic hypoxemia PaO2 ≤ 55 or SpO2 ≤ 88%), surgical options (LVRS, transplant) in select patients.

Drugs for Rhinitis/Allergy CIL (Clinical Integration Lecture)

Case 1 (Rhinitis/Allergy CIL):

25 yo falling asleep during operation, constant rhinorrhea, frequent tearing of eyes, conjunctivitis. A few weeks ago, began an OTC antihistamine, he took more than usual (2 or 3). PMH: HTN rx: atenolol QD. Vitals normal. PE: prominent erythematous boggy nasal turbinates, 2+ enlarged tonsils, no lymphadenopathy, 2+ conjunctivitis.

What is the diagnosis?
  • Acute pharyngitis
  • Acute rhinosinusitis
  • Allergic Rhinitis: Constant rhinorrhea, tearing, conjunctivitis, boggy turbinates suggest allergy. Falling asleep suggests sedating antihistamine use.
  • Ludwig's angina
  • Vincent's angina
Review immunologic response to allergies:

Initial exposure (sensitization) → Allergen processed by APCs → Presented to Th2 cells → Th2 cells release IL-4, IL-13 → Stimulate B cells to class switch and produce allergen-specific IgE → IgE binds to high-affinity FcεR1 receptors on mast cells and basophils.
Re-exposure → Allergen cross-links IgE on mast cells → Degranulation → Release of preformed mediators (Histamine, proteases) = Early phase reaction (sneezing, itching, rhinorrhea, vasodilation). Synthesis of new mediators (Leukotrienes, Prostaglandins, Cytokines) → Late phase reaction (sustained inflammation, nasal congestion, eosinophil recruitment).

Which of the following initial therapies is CONTRAINDICATED for him?
  • A first-gen H1 antagonist (e.g., Diphenhydramine): CONTRAINDICATED due to severe sedation (already falling asleep) and anticholinergic effects.
  • A leukotriene receptor antagonist (Montelukast): Option, but not first-line, neuropsych warning.
  • Acupuncture: Alternative therapy.
  • A second-gen H1 antagonist (e.g., Loratadine): Appropriate initial option.
  • An intranasal corticosteroid (e.g., Fluticasone): Most effective long-term, appropriate initial option.
If he were not hypertensive, the adrenergic agonist that would be most appropriate targets which receptor?

For nasal decongestion:

  • Alpha-1: Causes vasoconstriction in nasal mucosa, reducing swelling. Target of decongestants (phenylephrine, pseudoephedrine, oxymetazoline).
  • Alpha-2: Primarily CNS effects.
  • Beta-1: Primarily heart.
  • Beta-2: Primarily lungs (bronchodilation).
  • Beta-3: Adipose tissue, bladder.
An adrenergic agonist (decongestant) is CONTRAINDICATED/used with caution because it elevates the risk of?
  • Acute stroke / Hypertensive crisis: Due to systemic vasoconstriction and increased BP, especially in patients with pre-existing HTN.
