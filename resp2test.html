Alveolar-arterial O2 gradient (A-a gradient)

Calculation [Click to reveal]

PAO2 = FiO2 x (PB – PH20) – PaCO2/R

Or simplified: PAO2 ≈ 150 - 1.25 x PaCO2 (at sea level, room air)

A-a O2 gradient = PAO2 – PaO2

Causes of Increased A-a Gradient (V/Q mismatch, Shunt) [Click to reveal]

V/Q mismatch occurs in:

  • COPD, bronchitis, asthma
  • Pulmonary emboli
  • Interstitial lung disease

Shunts occur in:

  • CHF (Congestive Heart Failure)
  • ARDS (Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome)
  • Lobar pneumonia

Acidosis and Alkalosis

Basic Compensation [Click to reveal]
  • Acidosis: Kidneys increase production/reabsorption of HCO3-
  • Alkalosis: Kidneys excrete HCO3-
  • If there is normal pH, but abnormal PCO2 or HCO3- → suspect mixed acid-base disorder!
Approach to the Patient (Anion Gap) [Click to reveal]

Calculate anion gap: AG = Na+ - (Cl- + HCO3-)

  • Normal range 6-12
Treatment Considerations [Click to reveal]

Respiratory Acidosis:

  • Tracheal intubation and assisted mechanical ventilation
  • Oxygen titrated carefully

Respiratory Alkalosis:

  • Rebreathing from a paper bag during attacks (use cautiously/if hyperventilation syndrome)
  • Treat underlying stress/cause

Imaging Studies & Procedures

  • Radiography: [Click to reveal]

    Pleural abnormalities.

  • US (Ultrasound): [Click to reveal]

    Pleural abnormalities, septations within loculated pleural collections.

  • Nuc Med (Nuclear Medicine - V/Q Scan): [Click to reveal]

    Airway stenosis (CT reconstruction).

  • PET scan (Positron Emission Tomography): [Click to reveal]

    Solitary pulmonary nodules and staging of lung cancer.

  • MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging): [Click to reveal]

    Distinguish vascular from nonvascular, pulmonary emboli (less common than CTA), define aortic lesions (aneurysms or dissection), Pancoast's tumor evaluation.

  • Pulmonary Angiography: [Click to reveal]

    Arterial system visualization (invasive, largely replaced by CTA for PE).

  • Sputum Analysis: [Click to reveal]

    Bacterial pathogens (Gram stain, culture).

  • Percutaneous Needle Aspiration: [Click to reveal]

    Biopsy of peripheral lung nodules/masses.

  • Thoracentesis: [Click to reveal]

    Diagnostic sampling of pleural effusion, or palliation of dyspnea.

  • Bronchoscopy: [Click to reveal]

    Retrieval of foreign body and suctioning of a massive hemorrhage.

  • Flexible Fiberoptic Bronchoscopy: [Click to reveal]

    Evaluation of endobronchial pathology like tumors, granulomas, bronchitis, foreign bodies, sites of bleeding. Can perform washings, brushings, biopsies.

  • Transbronchial Needle Aspiration (TBNA): [Click to reveal]

    Aspiration of cellular material from mass lesions or enlarged lymph nodes adjacent to airways. Samples from lung surrounding lymph nodes w/o need for surgery or general anesthesia.

  • Endobronchial Ultrasound (EBUS): [Click to reveal]

    Ultrasound-guided aspiration of mediastinal and hilar lymph nodes, harder to reach areas for staging.

  • Medical Thoracoscopy (Pleuroscopy): [Click to reveal]

    Evaluation and management of pleural-based problems (effusions, biopsies).

  • Mediastinoscopy and Mediastinotomy: [Click to reveal]

    Obtain specimen from masses or nodes in the mediastinum (surgical).

  • Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery (VATS): [Click to reveal]

    Diagnosis and management of pleural as well as parenchymal lung disease (minimally invasive surgery).

  • Thoracotomy: [Click to reveal]

    Open chest surgery for diagnostic sampling or resection of lung tissue.

Hoarseness & Sinusitis

Hoarseness

Case 1:

28 yo female with cc of sinus pressure and pain. Unable to work recently since she can't concentrate and is very tired. She is a receptionist, and her voice is hoarse to the point of being hard to understand. Feels pain in her cheeks every step she takes steps up the stairs to the third floor of her apartment. Pain started 10 days ago, getting worse. She has tried IBU and acetaminophen. Sleeping is hard due to bad cough. This has happened 3-4 times/year for last year. She's been treated with ABX 4 times this year. Three episodes of this in spring. She has felt warm but has not taken her temp. Her R ear is occasionally painful, "stuffy". She has been using the “neti pot" but hasn't helped. Has no allergies and isn't taking any meds. PE: BP 110/68, pulse 85, temp 38, RR 20 and unlabored. Hoarse hypo nasal voice with diminished volume, dark circles surround her eyes, mouth breathing during interview. R tympanic membrane is retracted and lacks a good cone of light. Erythematous, boggy inferior turbinates and thick clear nasal discharge, posterior oropharynx shows post-nasal drainage and cobblestoning. Indirect laryngoscopy with mirror shows edema of true and false vocal cords. High arched palate with crowding of maxillary teeth. No cervical lymphadenopathy is appreciated. Lungs show diffuse wheezing.

What is cobble stoning? [Click to reveal]

Minor salivary glands in posterior oropharynx are inflamed and looks like a cobble stone road, can be from post-nasal drip or acid reflux.

The most common causative bacteria in acute sinusitis is? [Click to reveal]
  • Staph aureus
  • Strep pneumoniae → Strep pneumoniae, H. influenzae, Moraxella are three most common causes of acute sinusitis
  • Pseudomonas sp.
  • Mycoplasma
  • Strep viridans
Timeframe for Sinusitis [Click to reveal]
  • Acute: 1-4 weeks
  • Subacute: 4-12 weeks
  • Chronic: greater than 12 weeks
Distinguishing Viral vs Bacterial Sinusitis [Click to reveal]
  • Yellow or green nasal discharge (can be viral or bacterial)
  • Daytime and nighttime cough (non-specific)
  • Symptoms lasting more than 10-14 days → suggests bacterial as viral usually resolves on its own.
  • Fever (can be present in both, high fever >39C for 3-4 days suggests bacterial)
  • Nasal Congestion (non-specific)
  • Facial pressure and pain → can distinguish sinusitis from simple URI.
Major and Minor Criteria for Sinusitis (Case 1 Patient) [Click to reveal]
  • Major Criteria Examples: facial pressure and fullness, nasal obstruction, purulent discharge, hyposmia/anosmia, fever.
  • Minor Criteria Examples: headache, fever (low grade), halitosis, fatigue, dental pain, cough, ear pain/pressure/fullness, postnasal drip.
  • Case 1 Patient:
    • Major: facial pain/pressure
    • Minor: postnasal drip, cough, ear pain, fever (subjective warmth, temp 38)
  • Diagnosis Criteria:
    • ≥2 major criteria OR
    • 1 major and ≥2 minor criteria
Pathophysiology of Bacterial Sinusitis [Click to reveal]

Bacterial infection leads to mucosal inflammation, closed ostium, reduced ventilation of closed air space and stasis of secretions, decreased pH, injury to cilia and epithelium, and retention of secretions. Air in mucosalized air space causes negative pressure, creates watery secretions and bacteria will grow.

Treatment Goal and Approach [Click to reveal]

Goal: open the ostia to equalize pressure.

  • Glucocorticoids (topical nasal steroids) decrease inflammation.
  • Systemic steroids can decrease inflammation (used less often).
  • Sudafed (pseudoephedrine): alpha-1 agonist → vasoconstriction (helps decongest).

Case 2:

28 yo female, cannot breathe through her nose, both ears plugged, every spring and early fall she has similar symptoms. She was asymptomatic for several years after giving her birth to her first child. Vital signs normal. BP: 140/94, RR 16. PE: b/l retracted tympanic membrane with air fluid levels in middle ear. Inferior turbinates are blue, boggy, thin rhinorrhea. High arched palate, cobblestoning of posterior oropharynx and visible postnasal drainage.

Discussion points [Click to reveal]

Patient is slightly HTN, high RR (Note: RR 16 is normal, maybe meant high BP?). Retracted tympanic membrane means closed air space, negative pressure. Blocks eustachian tube. Closed space within the middle ear, oxygen gets absorbed into mucosal membrane, leaves negative pressure, fluid occurs. Arched hard palate, crowding of teeth: suggests chronic mouth breathing, often since childhood.

Which med is indicated for nasal congestion due to allergic rhinitis? [Click to reveal]
  • Cromolyn sodium (mast cell stabilizer)
  • Ipratropium bromide nasal spray → given for patients for rhinitis (specifically rhinorrhea, less effective for congestion)
  • Fluticasone nasal spray → steroids are most effective
  • Azelastine nasal spray → second choice (antihistamine spray)
  • Diphenhydramine (oral 1st gen antihistamine, sedating, not ideal for congestion)
1st versus 2nd generation antihistamines [Click to reveal]
  • 1st gen: smaller, lipid soluble, easily penetrate BBB, anticholinergic activity (e.g., Benadryl impairs cognition).
  • 2nd gen: lipophilic but do not cross BBB as readily, less sedation, no significant anticholinergic activity (e.g., Loratadine, Cetirizine).
Which med would be CONTRAINDICATED given her classical presentation (HTN)? [Click to reveal]
  • Guaifenesin (expectorant)
  • Pseudoephedrine → patient has HTN (alpha-1 agonist, can increase BP)
  • Diphenhydramine (antihistamine)
  • Mometasone nasal → intranasal corticosteroid
  • Montelukast (leukotriene receptor antagonist)
Initial approach to treatment of her allergies? [Click to reveal]

Investigate further her environment and potential sources of allergen

  • Avoidance strategies
  • Pharmacotherapy (e.g., nasal steroids, antihistamines)
  • Immunotherapy (allergy shots)
Systemic steroids adverse effects [Click to reveal]
  • Short term: increased appetite, moodiness, insomnia
  • Long term: adrenal insufficiency, osteoporosis, cataracts, hyperglycemia, weight gain, etc.
Immunotherapy indications in allergic sinusitis [Click to reveal]
  • IgE mediated symptoms not readily controlled by pharmacotherapy and avoidance
  • Symptoms persisting > 1 season (perennial) or severe seasonal
  • Severe symptomatology
  • Symptoms caused by allergens not easily avoided

Case 3:

45 yo male with cc left ear pain. Noticed this three months ago after a trip to the lake. Seen by PCP who said nothing was wrong. It has progressively gotten worse over time, and has a dull burning quality. Modulating factors that precipitated include chewing and swallowing food. Hx of bad ear infections as child. Has been to loud concerts with loud ringing. Lost appetite and 10 lb in last month. No meds. Smoker 1ppd for 28 years, occasional alcohol, and marijuana. Vitals okay, slightly HTN. PE: ears show no sign of infection, good cone of light. Pretty good.

With no positive physician findings thus far, what additional physical exam should be performed to test your differential diagnosis? [Click to reveal]
  • Perform rigid rhinoscopy to eval nasopharynx (r/o tumor)
  • Have him open and close his mandible while palpating the TMJ → this is common cause with no sign of infection and negative PE
  • Watch pupillary reactions to light and accommodation (CN check)
  • Listen to the neck for carotid bruits (vascular referral?)
  • Palpate thyroid to feel for a mass (referred pain?)
What nerves provides sensation to the ear? [Click to reveal]
  • V (Trigeminal - auriculotemporal branch)
  • VII (Facial - small contribution)
  • IX (Glossopharyngeal - Jacobson's nerve)
  • X (Vagus - Arnold's nerve)
  • Cervical plexus (C2, C3 - great auricular, lesser occipital)
  • Note: Text lists V, VII, IX, X, Somatic afferents. Somatic afferents is a category, not a specific nerve.
What additional tests? [Click to reveal]
  • Facial nerve EMG stimulation → facial palsy that doesn't get better (Not indicated here)
  • Flexible fiberoptic laryngoscopy → reasonable, but would try to do mirror exam first (to eval pharynx/larynx for referred pain source)
  • MRI of brain (If suspecting central cause/tumor)
  • Referral to neurologist for full evaluation of migraine HA (Migraine can cause otalgia)
  • Dental referral for eval of teeth → most common source of referred otalgia
Ten T's of otalgia (Differential Diagnosis) [Click to reveal]
  • Cranial nerve "ten" (Vagus nerve irritation/lesion)
  • Tooth pain (Dental source)
  • TMJ dysfunction → probably #1 cause (esp. with chewing pain)
  • "Tube" dysfunction (Eustachian tube dysfunction)
  • Otitis media
  • Otitis externa
  • Trauma
  • Tonsillitis
  • Tumor (e.g., nasopharyngeal, base of tongue, larynx)
  • Thyroiditis

OVERVIEW: 5 Densities on Chest Radiograph

  1. Lung (Air): Normally radiolucent (dark).
    Collapsed/Atelectatic Density [Click to reveal]

    Increased opacity due to volume loss. Causes: Mucus plugging, foreign bodies, pleural parenchymal scarring, lung cancer obstructing airway. Termed 'Density' or 'Opacity' often synonymous with atelectasis in context.

  2. Pus (Fluid/Consolidation): Pneumonia.
    Characteristics [Click to reveal]

    Densities fill alveoli. Tend to occupy space (no volume loss or minimal). Very little atelectatic changes (unless post-obstructive). Ill-defined at the edges ("fluffy margins").

  3. Cells (Soft Tissue): Cancer (primary lung carcinoma or mets).
    Characteristics [Click to reveal]

    Often begin in bronchus and may lead to collapse (atelectasis) or appear as a well-defined mass.

  4. Water (Fluid): Pulmonary edema, pleural effusion.
    Characteristics [Click to reveal]
    • Edema: Can be asymmetrical, resembles focal density, classic "butterfly" or "bat-wing" pattern (perihilar).
    • Effusion: Sometimes obscures portions of chest cavity. Meniscal sign (blunting of costophrenic angle), uniformly dense, space neutral or slightly space occupying.
  5. Blood: Pulmonary hemorrhage.
    Characteristics [Click to reveal]

    Uncommon. Often seen as contusion or bruising of lung in trauma patient. Can appear as consolidation.

  6. (Implicit 6th density - Bone/Calcium: Ribs, clavicles, calcified nodules - very radiopaque/white)

Discern Atelectasis from Pneumonia

Atelectasis
  • Volume loss (key feature)
  • Increased linear markings as alveoli collapse
  • "Accordion" sign (crowding of vessels/bronchi)
  • Hilar/fissure/mediastinal shift TOWARDS the atelectasis
  • Straight edge to density (often along a fissure)
  • Minimal bowing of trachea visible on example image
  • Silhouette sign (loss of normal border, e.g., diaphragm)
  • Lowering of hilum (e.g., L hilum in example)
  • Opacity in anterior basal segment of LL in example (ATX = atelectasis)
Pneumonia
  • Little or no volume loss, space neutral
  • Alveolar pattern with fluffy margins
  • Lobar distribution common
  • NO significant SHIFT of mediastinum/fissures

Tuberculosis

General Info [Click to reveal]
  • Typically affects apical and posterior segments of upper lobes.
  • Causative agent: Mycobacterium tuberculosis (acid-fast bacillus).
  • Risk factors: immunocompromised, infants, elderly, diabetes, CKD, IVDU, alcohol, tobacco, malnutrition, malignancy, silicosis.
Symptoms of Active TB [Click to reveal]

Cough (often > 3 weeks), fever, night sweats, sputum production (may be bloody - hemoptysis), malaise, fatigue, weight loss, anemia, leukocytosis (variable), clubbing (chronic), elevated ESR.

Extra pulmonary TB

Common Sites & Features [Click to reveal]
  • Lymph node (scrofula or lymphadenitis): 35-40%, most common site.
  • Pleural involvement: 20%, could become an effusion, dx with elevated ADA (adenosine deaminase).
  • Upper airway: May need bx, sx are hoarseness, dysphonia, dysphagia.
  • GU TB: 10-15%, presents with negative pyuria in acidic urine (sterile pyuria), requires 3 morning urine cultures for AFB.
  • Skeletal TB: 10%, affects weight bearing joints, “Pott's disease” in the spine (vertebral osteomyelitis/discitis).
  • TB meningitis: 5%, HA or mental changes, common in children, HIV patients. Lumbar puncture with AFM smear and culture, hydrocephalus on CT/MRI.
  • Miliary or disseminated TB: Lungs appear covered in millet seeds (small diffuse nodules on CXR).
  • GI TB: Uncommon, fistulae or Crohn's disease-like appearance.

TB Skin test (TST / PPD)

Limitations & Interpretation [Click to reveal]

Limitations: Prednisone and other DMARDs can suppress the skin response. Will be positive in those who have received BCG vaccine (common outside US). False negatives in anergy.

Positive at induration 5mm or > in:

  • HIV infection
  • Household contact with person with active TB
  • Nursing home patient with fibrotic changes on x-ray suggestive of old TB
  • Organ transplants, immune suppressed (e.g., >15mg/day prednisone for >1 month)

Positive at induration 10mm or > in:

  • Residents or employees of high risk settings: jails, nursing homes, shelters, hospitals
  • Health care employees
  • Recent (within 5 yrs) immigrants from high prevalence country
  • IV drug user
  • Immunosuppressive conditions like diabetes, renal insufficiency, gastrectomy, silicosis
  • Children < 4 years old
  • Infants, children, adolescents exposed to high-risk adults

Positive at induration 15mm or > in:

  • Standard for low risk individuals (persons with no known risk factors for TB)

Interferon Gamma Release Assay (IGRA)

Use & Interpretation [Click to reveal]
  • Preferred for people with BCG vaccine history.
  • Useful for those who have a hard time making 2nd appointment (for TST reading).
  • Useful for patients on immunosuppressive therapy (may be more reliable than TST).
  • Positive = patient exposed and infected with TB (latent or active). Does not distinguish between latent and active.
  • Requires Work-up: history, PE, PA/Lat chest x-ray.

Leprosy (Hansen's Disease)

Agent & Spectrum [Click to reveal]

Caused by M. leprae: acid-fast bacillus. Exists on a spectrum from Tuberculoid (TT) to Lepromatous (LL), with Borderline (BT, BB, BL) in between.

Leprosy Complications [Click to reveal]

Neuropathy, insensitivity, and myopathy:

  • Loss of fine touch, pain, and heat receptors
  • Mostly ulnar nerve: Clawing of 4th and 5th digits, loss of DAB (dorsal interossei abduction) in hand, loss of sensation.
  • Median nerve: Thumb opposition and grasp weakness/loss.
  • Radial nerve: Wrist drop.
  • Peroneal nerve: Foot drop.
  • Plantar ulceration at metatarsal heads most common manifestation of leprous neuropathy.
  • Lepra reactions (Type 1 reversal, Type 2 ENL).

Non-TB Mycobacterial (NTM) Infections

Common Species & Transmission [Click to reveal]
  • Common species: M. avium complex (MAC), M. kansasii, M. abscessus. Others: M. simiae, M. fortuitum, M. immunogenum.
  • Found in environment (water, soil).
  • Majority due to MAC, M. kansasii, M. abscessus.
  • No known human to human transmission (except rare cases in CF patients).
Predisposing Factors [Click to reveal]
  • Impaired host defense (e.g., underlying lung disease like bronchiectasis, COPD, pneumoconiosis).
  • Primary ciliary dyskinesia.
  • Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency.
  • Cystic Fibrosis (CF).
  • Direct inoculation (surgery/trauma).
  • Immunosuppression (less common for pulmonary NTM than TB, but risk for disseminated).
Clinical Manifestations [Click to reveal]
  • Pulmonary: Most common in North America. Symptoms: throat clearing, nagging cough, slowly progressive fatigue. Often insidious onset. May see nodules, cavities, bronchiectasis on imaging. Bronchoscopy may be needed for diagnosis.
  • Disseminated: Malaise, fever, weight loss, organomegaly, lymphadenopathy, anemia, granuloma formation (esp. in severe immunosuppression like advanced HIV).
  • Cervical lymph nodes: Cervical lymphadenopathy, firm and painless node (scrofula-like).
  • Skin and soft tissue: Fish tank granuloma (M. marinum - papules or ulcers after exposure to contaminated water). Other species can cause post-traumatic or surgical site infections.
  • Skeletal infections, catheter-related bloodstream infections.
Diagnosis [Click to reveal]
  • Acid-fast or fluorochrome stain on sputum/tissue.
  • Culture: Biopsy/sputum takes 6-7 weeks for results (longer than TB). Need specific NTM culture.
  • CT Chest: "Tree-in-bud" patterns in bronchial inflammation, nodules, cavities, bronchiectasis (esp. RML and lingula).
  • Diagnostic Criteria (ATS/IDSA): Requires clinical, radiographic, AND microbiologic criteria met.
    • Clinical symptoms + Radiographic findings + EITHER:
    • Growth of NTM from ≥2 separate sputum samples OR
    • Positive bronchoscopic alveolar lavage (BAL) sample OR
    • Pulmonary parenchymal biopsy with granulomatous inflammation OR AFB AND positive NTM culture from sputum/bronchial sample.
    (Text simplification: Growth of NTM from 2/3 sputum samples OR positive bronchoscopic alveolar sample OR pulmonary parenchymal biopsy with granulomatous inflammation OR mycobacteria found)
Treatment [Click to reveal]
  • Requires multi-drug regimen based on species and susceptibility testing.
  • Commonly includes: Macrolide (e.g., azithromycin/clarithromycin), ethambutol, rifamycin (e.g., rifampin/rifabutin). Often for 12 months AFTER culture conversion.
  • Resection of cavitary lesions and bronchiectasis segments may be considered in some cases.

Pediatric Pulmonology

Embryology

Key Points [Click to reveal]
  • Errors in septum forming between esophagus and trachea leads to TE fistula (Tracheoesophageal Fistula).
  • First major viability milestone → 25 weeks gestation (surfactant production begins).

Congenital Malformations

Pulmonary hypoplasia [Click to reveal]

Incomplete lung development, usually secondary to another organ not developing properly (e.g., renal agenesis - Potter sequence) or conditions limiting thoracic space (e.g., diaphragmatic hernia).

Bronchogenic cysts [Click to reveal]

Derived from foregut, fluid-filled. Can get infected or compress airways. Tx: surgical resection.

Congenital lobar emphysema (CLE) [Click to reveal]

Hyperinflation of one or more lobes due to bronchial cartilage abnormality (ball-valve effect). Can cause respiratory distress. Tx: conservative management if mild, lobectomy in severe cases.

Congenital pulmonary airway malformations (CPAM) [Click to reveal]

Most common primary congenital lung malformation. Non-functioning cystic lung tissue. Formerly CCAM. Tx: surgical excision (usually around 6 months) due to infection and malignancy risk.

Pulmonary sequestration [Click to reveal]

Nonfunctional lung tissue not connected to the tracheobronchial tree, receives systemic arterial supply. Increased infection risk.

  • Intralobar: Most common, within normal lung lobe (usually L lower lobe), associated with Scimitar syndrome (anomalous pulmonary venous return). Presents later.
  • Extralobar: Has its own pleura, associated with other congenital anomalies (esp. diaphragmatic hernia), presents earlier in life with respiratory distress, CHF.

Tx: Surgical resection.

Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (CDH) [Click to reveal]

Defect in diaphragm allowing abdominal contents into chest. Usually left side (Bochdalek). Causes pulmonary hypoplasia. Tx: NGT for decompression, intubate immediately (avoid bag-mask), surgical repair.

Laryngomalacia [Click to reveal]

Exaggerated collapse of supraglottic structures (epiglottis, arytenoids) on inspiration → inspiratory stridor, worse when supine/agitated. Most common cause of stridor in infants. Dx: flexible nasopharyngoscopy. Tx: usually resolves spontaneously; surgery (supraglottoplasty) if severe (failure to thrive, severe resp distress, apnea). DO NOT GIVE ALBUTEROL (relaxes airways, can worsen collapse).

Choanal atresia [Click to reveal]

Nasal fossae closed off posteriorly by soft tissue/bone. Obligate nasal breathers, causes respiratory distress especially when feeding/sleeping (mouth closed). Cyanosis improves with crying. Dx: inability to pass small catheter through nose; confirmed with CT. Tx: secure airway (oral airway/intubation), surgical repair.

TE fistula (Tracheoesophageal Fistula) [Click to reveal]

Abnormal connection between trachea and esophagus. Often associated with esophageal atresia (EA). Presents with coughing/choking/cyanosis during feeding, excessive secretions, abdominal distension. Dx: inability to pass NGT into stomach, CXR shows coiled tube, air in stomach (if distal fistula). Tx: Surgical repair.

Pectus Deformities [Click to reveal]
  • Excavatum: Concave sternum ("funnel chest"). Usually uncomplicated but can cause Restrictive Lung Disease (RLD), decreased cardiac function, exercise intolerance. Repair (Nuss procedure) often for cosmesis or significant symptoms.
  • Carinatum: Bowed-out sternum ("pigeon chest"). Usually no functional deficit, diagnosis is clinical. Repair primarily cosmetic.

Problems with Neonatal Transition

Transient tachypnea of newborn (TTN) [Click to reveal]

Delayed clearance of fetal lung fluid ("wet lung"). Not enough air due to fluid. Associated with CS delivery and maternal diabetes. Presents with tachypnea, grunting, retractions shortly after birth. CXR: perihilar streaking, fluid in fissures. Tx: supportive care, minimal O2, usually resolves within 3 days.

Meconium aspiration syndrome (MAS) [Click to reveal]

Aspiration of meconium-stained amniotic fluid in utero or during delivery. Causes chemical pneumonitis, airway obstruction, surfactant inactivation. Presents with respiratory distress in term/post-term infant born through meconium-stained fluid. Rhonchi primarily. CXR: patchy infiltrates, hyperinflation, possible pneumothorax. Tx: supportive care, O2, mechanical ventilation, surfactant, inhaled nitric oxide if needed. Routine endotracheal suctioning no longer recommended for vigorous infants.

Persistent pulmonary HTN of newborn (PPHN) [Click to reveal]

Failure of normal circulatory transition at birth, leading to high pulmonary vascular resistance, R-to-L shunting across ductus arteriosus/foramen ovale. Presents with significant respiratory distress and cyanosis within 12 hours, often out of proportion to lung disease. Dx: ECHO → elevated PA pressures, R-to-L shunt. Tx: supplemental O2, sedation, gentle ventilation, correct acidosis, maintain BP, pulmonary vasodilators (inhaled nitric oxide, sildenafil), ECMO if severe.

Problems with Premature Delivery

Neonatal RDS (Respiratory Distress Syndrome) [Click to reveal]

Surfactant deficiency in premature infants. Leads to alveolar collapse, pulmonary shunt, decreased compliance, alveolar hypoventilation, hypercapnia. CXR: diffuse reticulogranular pattern ("ground glass"), air bronchograms, low lung volumes. Prevention: antenatal steroids to mother prior to preterm birth. Tx: exogenous surfactant delivery to neonate via ETT, CPAP/mechanical ventilation. PDA (Patent Ductus Arteriosus) is common comorbidity, may need indomethacin/ibuprofen or surgical ligation.

Chronic lung disease of prematurity/Bronchopulmonary Dysplasia (BPD) [Click to reveal]

Defined as O2-dependence at 36 weeks post-menstrual age (or 28 days of life). Due to lung injury from prematurity, O2 toxicity, barotrauma/volutrauma from ventilation. CXR: sponge-like appearance (alternating atelectasis and hyperinflation), cysts. Long term: pulm HTN, eventual R heart failure. Tx: minimize O2/ventilation injury, diuretics, bronchodilators, nutrition; may need mechanical ventilation or tracheostomy.

Apnea of prematurity [Click to reveal]

Pauses in breathing > 20 sec or shorter pauses with bradycardia/desaturation. Due to immature respiratory control center. Apnea vs increased RR (periodic breathing). Associated with low tone of laryngeal muscles. Tx: physical stimulation, O2, caffeine.

Respiratory Issues Found in Peds

Pneumothorax [Click to reveal]
  • Primary spontaneous: No underlying lung disease (tall thin males, rupture of apical blebs). CT may show blebs/bullae.
  • Secondary: Due to underlying lung disease (barotrauma from ventilation, asthma, CF) or trauma (penetrating/blunt).
  • Tx: 100% O2 (helps nitrogen resorption), observation if small/stable, fine needle decompression (if tension), tube thoracostomy (chest tube), VATS (Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery) with pleurodesis if recurrent/persistent.
Asthma Mimics [Click to reveal]
  • Foreign body aspiration: Usually < 3 years old, often R mainstem bronchus. Sudden onset cough/wheeze/choking. May have unilateral wheeze or decreased breath sounds. CXR may show air trapping or object. Requires bronchoscopy.
  • Vascular ring: Aortic arch anomalies wrap around trachea and esophagus. Presents with stridor, wheezing, feeding difficulties. Dx: Barium swallow, CT/MRI Angio. Tx: Surgical division.
  • Vocal cord dysfunction (VCD): Paradoxical vocal cord adduction during inspiration. Misdiagnosed as asthma. Inspiratory stridor/wheeze. PFTs may show inspiratory loop flattening. Often triggered by stress/exercise. Tx: diaphragmatic breathing, speech therapy, counseling.
Cystic Fibrosis (CF) [Click to reveal]
  • Autosomal recessive mutation in CFTR gene (Chr 7). Defective chloride transport.
  • Dx: Sweat chloride test (>60 mmol/L), genetic testing (newborn screen includes CF).
  • Sx: Chronic/recurrent pulmonary disease (cough, infection, bronchiectasis), failure to thrive, pancreatic insufficiency (malabsorption, steatorrhea), GI issues (meconium ileus at birth, distal intestinal obstruction syndrome - DIOS), infertility (absence of vas deferens in males, thick cervical mucus in females), azoospermia, secondary amenorrhea.
  • Pathophys: Thick and sticky secretions lead to impaired mucociliary clearance = recurrent bacterial infections (Staph aureus, Pseudomonas, Burkholderia).
  • Tx: CFTR modulators (e.g., ivacaftor, lumacaftor, tezacaftor, elexacaftor - based on genotype), airway clearance techniques (vest, CPT), inhaled therapies (hypertonic saline, dornase alfa), antibiotics for chronic infection/exacerbations, pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT), nutritional support. Meconium ileus requires surgery for disimpaction. Lung transplant for end-stage disease.
Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia (PCD) [Click to reveal]
  • Autosomal recessive disorder of ciliary structure/function. Impaired mucociliary clearance.
  • Sx: Neonatal respiratory distress, chronic cough (wet), nasal congestion, chronic otitis media/ear effusions, chronic sinusitis, situs inversus totalis (in ~50% - Kartagener syndrome), infertility (immotile sperm, impaired fallopian tube function), bronchiectasis.
  • Dx: Low nasal nitric oxide, ciliary biopsy for electron microscopy, genetic testing.
  • Tx: Airway clearance, antibiotics for infections, tympanostomy tubes, chest physiotherapy. Monitor lung function.
SIDS (Sudden Infant Death Syndrome) [Click to reveal]
  • Unexpected death < 1 yo, cause unknown after investigation. Peak age 2-4 months.
  • Higher risk: males, premature, low birth weight, impoverished mothers, maternal smoking/drug use, sibling that died of SIDS, winter months.
  • Prevention (Safe Sleep): Supine sleeping (Back to Sleep), no loose/soft bedding, separate but proximate sleep space (room-sharing, not bed-sharing), avoid overheating, smoking cessation.
  • Protection: Breast feeding, pacifier use, room-sharing.
Brief Resolved Unexplained Event (BRUE) [Click to reveal]
  • Event in infant < 1 year old reported by observer involving ≥1 of: cyanosis/pallor, irregular/absent/decreased breathing, marked change in tone (hyper/hypotonia), altered level of responsiveness. Duration < 1 minute, resolves spontaneously.
  • Low risk BRUE (specific criteria met) requires minimal workup. High risk requires further investigation.
  • Could be due to GERD, seizures, brain bleeds, infection, cardiac arrhythmia, airway issues.
  • Workup (if high risk): ECG, pertussis screen, consider others based on Hx/PE.
Apnea [Click to reveal]
  • > 20 sec pause in breathing, or shorter if associated with bradycardia/cyanosis/pallor. Abnormal.
  • Congenital central hypoventilation syndrome (CCHS): Loss of automatic respiratory control, especially during sleep. Autosomal dominant, PHOX2B gene mutation. Requires lifelong ventilatory support.
  • Breath holding spells: Common (6mo-6yr). Triggered by anger/pain. Sustained exhalation followed by cyanosis and LOC with spontaneous return of breath. Usually benign. Differentiate from seizure.
  • Obstructive upper-airway obstruction (OSA in kids): Restless sleep, snoring, behavior problems, inattention/hyperactivity. Often due to adenotonsillar hypertrophy. Dx with polysomnography. Tx: adenotonsillectomy.

Silly Teenagers (Inhalational Injuries)

E-cig vaping associated lung injury (EVALI) [Click to reveal]

Associated with THC-containing products and vitamin E acetate. Can cause severe pneumonitis/ARDS. Tx: Supportive care, Oxygen, Steroids often used.

Hydrocarbon inhalation [Click to reveal]

Huffing gasoline, solvents etc. Causes chemical pneumonitis with destruction of surfactant leading to ARDS, necrotizing pneumonitis, and hemorrhagic pulmonary edema. Also cardiac/CNS toxicity.

Respiratory Infections in Pediatrics

Acute otitis media (AOM) [Click to reveal]
  • Complication of viral URI or eustachian tube dysfunction. Peak 6-18 months. 90% have at least one episode by 2 yo.
  • Common bacteria: Strep pneumo, H. flu (non-typeable), M. catarrhalis.
  • Sx: Fever and ear pain (otalgia), irritability. Bulging tympanic membrane and poor TM mobility with bulb insufflation (pneumatic otoscopy).
  • Tx: High-dose amoxicillin (90mg/kg/day). Use amoxicillin-clavulanate if amoxicillin in last 30 days, concurrent conjunctivitis, or recurrent AOM. Alternatives if allergic: cefdinir, azithromycin, clindamycin.
  • If chronic (> 3 episodes in 6 months or > 4 in 12 months) → consider tympanostomy tubes.
Acute otitis externa (AOE – “swimmers ear") [Click to reveal]
  • Inflammation of external auditory canal. Most common 5-14 yo.
  • Common bacteria: Pseudomonas, S. aureus, S. epidermidis. Fungal also possible.
  • Sx: Ear pain (otalgia), itching, hearing loss, tenderness of tragus/auricle when manipulated. Canal edema/erythema/debris.
  • Tx: Mild → topical acetic acid and hydrocortisone drops. Moderate/Severe → topical antibiotic +/- steroid drops (e.g., ciprofloxacin/dexamethasone, neomycin/polymyxin B/hydrocortisone - avoid if TM perforated). Wick placement if canal swollen shut.
Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis) [Click to reveal]
  • Viral inflammation of larynx and subglottic airway → obstruction. Age 6m-3yr typically.
  • Common virus: Parainfluenza (types 1, 2, 3). Also RSV, influenza, adenovirus.
  • Sx: Prodrome of fever, URI sx, then develops inspiratory stridor, barky cough ("seal bark"), hoarse voice. Worse at night.
  • Dx: Clinical. Neck x-ray shows "steeple sign" (subglottic narrowing).
  • Tx: Mild (no stridor at rest): supportive (cool mist, hydration). Moderate/Severe (stridor at rest, retractions): single dose dexamethasone (oral or IM), nebulized racemic epinephrine (observe for rebound).
Epiglottitis [Click to reveal]
  • Bacterial infection causing inflammation/swelling of epiglottis and supraglottic structures. MEDICAL EMERGENCY. Age 2-6 yr historically, now seen in un/under-immunized and adults.
  • Common bacteria: Historically H. influenzae type b (Hib) - less common with vaccine. Now Strep species, Staph aureus.
  • Sx: Rapid onset high fever, sore throat, dysphagia, drooling, muffled voice ("hot potato"), tripod sitting position, inspiratory stridor, respiratory distress.
  • Dx: Clinical suspicion. Lateral neck x-ray shows "thumb sign" (swollen epiglottis). **DO NOT attempt to visualize pharynx with tongue depressor** - can precipitate airway obstruction. Secure airway first!
  • Tx: Secure airway (intubation, often in OR), IV antibiotics (e.g., ceftriaxone +/- vancomycin).
Strep throat (Group A Strep Pharyngitis) [Click to reveal]
  • Bacterial pharyngitis. Peak age 5-15 yo.
  • Bacteria: S. pyogenes (Group A Streptococcus - GAS).
  • Sx: Sudden onset fever, sore throat, abdominal pain, headache, anterior cervical lymphadenopathy, tonsillar exudates, palatal petechiae. Often lacks cough/rhinorrhea (helps distinguish from viral). Centor criteria used (Fever, Absence of cough, Tonsillar exudates, tender anterior Cervical lymphadenopathy).
  • Dx: Rapid strep antigen test (good specificity, variable sensitivity). Throat culture is gold standard (esp. if rapid test negative in children/adolescents).
  • Tx: Once daily amoxicillin x 10 days (or Penicillin VK). Return to school next day if tx prior to 5 pm (or after 24h of abx). Prevents rheumatic fever, may shorten illness duration slightly.
  • Complications: Scarlet fever, rheumatic fever, post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis (PSGN), peritonsillar abscess.
Sinusitis (Pediatric) [Click to reveal]
  • Often starts as viral URI.
  • Sx suggesting bacterial: Lack of improvement after 10 days OR worsening symptoms after initial improvement ("double sickening") OR severe onset (fever > 39°C + purulent discharge for ≥3 days). Cough worse at night, rhinitis.
  • Tx: Amoxicillin or Amox-clavulanate x 10 days.
  • Complications: Pott's puffy tumor (frontal sinus osteomyelitis with subperiosteal abscess), epidural abscess, orbital cellulitis.
Pneumonia (Pediatric) [Click to reveal]
  • Community-acquired (CAP): 80% viral (RSV, influenza, parainfluenza, adenovirus). Bacterial causes: S. pneumo (most common bacterial), H. flu, S. aureus, M. pneumo (older kids/adolescents - "walking pneumonia").
  • Sx: Fever, cough, tachypnea, hypoxia, nasal flare, grunting, chest pain, crackles.
  • Dx: Clinical. CXR helpful: lobar consolidation → suggests bacterial; bilateral diffuse infiltrate → suggests viral/mycoplasma.
  • Tx: Viral: supportive care. Bacterial (suspected): Amoxicillin (outpatient), Ampicillin (inpatient). Atypical (Mycoplasma/Chlamydia): Azithromycin.
  • Acute chest syndrome (in Sickle Cell Disease): Vaso-occlusive crisis in pulmonary vasculature + infection/infarction. Looks like lobar pneumonia. Requires broad spectrum abx (covering typicals + atypicals, e.g., ceftriaxone + azithromycin), supportive care, aggressive fluids, transfusion, pain control.
Bronchiolitis [Click to reveal]
  • Lower respiratory tract infection causing inflammation/obstruction of small airways (bronchioles). Usually < 2 years old, peak 2-6 months.
  • Common virus: RSV (Respiratory Syncytial Virus). Also rhinovirus, metapneumovirus, influenza.
  • Sx: URI prodrome then progresses to symmetric wheezing, cough, tachypnea, retractions, excessive mucous and necrotic epithelium clogging system.
  • Dx: Clinical. CXR often shows hyperinflation, peribronchial cuffing.
  • Tx: Primarily supportive → nasal suctioning, hydration (fluids), O2 if needed. Bronchodilators generally NOT effective. Palivizumab prophylaxis (monoclonal antibody against RSV) for high-risk infants (ex-premis <29 wks, chronic lung disease, congenital heart disease).
Pertussis (Whooping Cough) [Click to reveal]
  • Highly contagious bacterial infection. Primarily affects infants < 6 months (highest morbidity/mortality).
  • Bacteria: Bordetella pertussis.
  • Stages:
    • Catarrhal stage (1-2 weeks): Runny nose, low-grade fever, mild cough (most contagious).
    • Paroxysmal stage (2-6 weeks): Intense coughing fits (paroxysms), often ending with forceful inspiration ("whoop") accompanied by post-tussive emesis. Apnea common in infants.
    • Convalescent stage (weeks-months): Gradual resolution of sx, but residual cough for months possibly.
  • Dx: Clinical suspicion + PCR testing or culture on nasopharyngeal swab.
  • Tx: Macrolide antibiotics (e.g., azithromycin, erythromycin) - shortens contagious period, may lessen severity if started early (catarrhal stage). Supportive care. Droplet precautions. Treat close contacts prophylactically.
Abscess (Pediatric) [Click to reveal]
  • Retropharyngeal Abscess: Infection in retropharyngeal space. Usually < 6 years old. Organisms: S. aureus, GAS, anaerobes. Sx: Fever, sore throat, dysphagia, neck stiffness/torticollis, muffled voice, drooling, often no preceding URI sx. Imaging: Lateral neck x-ray shows widened prevertebral space; CT confirms. Tx: IV antibiotics (e.g., Ampicillin/Sulbactam or Clindamycin), I&D (Incision & Drainage) often required.
  • Peritonsillar Abscess (PTA): Collection of pus between tonsillar capsule and pharyngeal muscles. Usually > 8 years old / adolescents. Organisms: GAS, S. aureus, anaerobes. Sx: Severe sore throat (often unilateral), fever, trismus ("lockjaw"), "hot potato voice", drooling, uvular deviation away from abscess, asymmetric tonsil swelling. Tx: Needle aspiration or I&D, IV/oral antibiotics (e.g., Amox/Clav, Clindamycin).

Pediatric Case Studies

Case 1 (Peds):

26 yo G3P2 woman undergoes standard anatomy US at 20 weeks gestation. Cystic lesion noted to be connected to the lung.

What does this lesion most likely represent? [Click to reveal]
  • Bronchogenic cyst → looks like a cyst, usually NOT connected to lung airway, walled off, full of fluids, can increase chance of malignancy/infections later.
  • Congenital lobar emphysema → aka congenital lobar hyperinflation. Looks like a tension pneumothorax (hyperinflated lobe), kids end up w/ lobectomy if severe.
  • Congenital pulmonary airway malformation (CPAM) → most common, made up several types of tissues (cystic/solid components). Connected to the lung, spontaneous malformation, excision to prevent infection and malignancy (usually done around 6 months).
  • Cystic fibrosis (Systemic disease, not typically a single focal lesion like this)
  • Pulmonary sequestration → 2nd most common, actually lung tissue but not functional (no connection to airway, systemic blood supply). Intralobar → full lobectomy often needed.

Case 2 (Peds):

7 yo previously healthy child with cough for the last week. Low-grade temp. hoarse cough. Chest x-ray shows air bronchograms.

What is the most likely etiology? [Click to reveal]
  • Chlamydia trachomatis (Neonatal pneumonia)
  • Mycoplasma pneumoniae → patient is 7 years old (School-aged kid), common cause of atypical/"walking" pneumonia. Patchy infiltrate on CXR, wheezy on exam, often low grade or no fever. Treat with macrolide.
  • RSV → would see more atelectasis and mucous, usually younger kids (<2).
  • Staph aureus (More severe, consolidation/abscess)
  • Strep pneumoniae (Typical lobar pneumonia, higher fever)
Best treatment? [Click to reveal]

Macrolide: azithromycin

Case 3 (Peds):

4 yo with cough, congestion, fever up to 103.5 F (39.7 C) x 3 days, labored fast breathing with rales, crackles over right mid-lung field.

What is the most likely etiology of this child's presentation? [Click to reveal]
  • Chlamydia trachomatis (Neonatal)
  • Mycoplasma pneumoniae (Usually older kids, less acute/high fever)
  • RSV (Bronchiolitis picture, wheezing, younger usually)
  • Staph aureus → more likely to be necrotizing pneumonia and would see air pockets (pneumatoceles).
  • Strep pneumoniae → chest x-ray likely shows lobar consolidation. Classic presentation for typical bacterial pneumonia.
    • Lobar pneumonia, treat with amoxicillin (high dose) outpatient, or ampicillin if inpatient.
    • Fever, tachypnea = typical signs of lobar bacterial pneumonia.
    • CXR not always necessary for outpatient diagnosis but would show consolidation (loses heart border if RML/lingula = silhouette sign, more likely bacterial pneumonia).
    • Viral is most common overall, but high fever/lobar signs point more to bacterial.

Case 4 (Peds):

3 yo unimmunized child with 3-day history of high fever of 105.6 F (40.9 C), one day history of refusal to eat or drink. Refuses to lay down, mom thought was teething due to drool, but now has excessive drooling.

What finding would you expect to find on x-ray? [Click to reveal]
  • Air crescent sign (Aspergilloma)
  • Lightbulb sign (Posterior shoulder dislocation)
  • Sail sign (Normal thymus in infant CXR)
  • Steeple sign (Croup)
  • Thumb sign → epiglottis, likely due to H. influenzae (since unimmunized).
Best next step in management? [Click to reveal]
  • CT scan (Not primary)
  • High-flow O2 (Supportive, but airway is primary concern)
  • I&D (Only if abscess suspected later)
  • Intubation → will lose airway if you don't do that
    • Protect airway first! Then start IV antibiotics (e.g., ceftriaxone +/- vancomycin). Call anesthesia/ENT STAT.
  • IV antibiotics (Important, but AFTER airway secured)
If patient IS immunized → what else to worry about? [Click to reveal]

If immunized against Hib, epiglottitis less likely but still possible from other bacteria (Strep, Staph). Worry about an abscess (retropharyngeal, peritonsillar - though less common at 3yo). Consider Staph/Strep. Older kids presenting with drooling, consider GAS or S. aureus causing PTA or RPA.

Case 5 (Peds):

15-months with barky cough and noisy breathing, worse at night, overall worsening in last 2-3 days. Low grade fever of 100.5 F (38.1 C). Likely Croup.

Most likely etiology? [Click to reveal]

Parainfluenza virus

Management? [Click to reveal]

Steroids (dexamethasone), and cool air/mist. Nebulized racemic epi if stridor at rest/significant distress.

Clues? [Click to reveal]
  • Steeple sign on x-ray (though often clinical dx)
  • Age: 6 months - 3 years typically
  • Immunization status doesn't matter (viral)
  • Barky cough, stridor, worse at night
  • Gets better with cool air

Case 6 (Peds):

21-month-old previously healthy male, brought to clinic by his parents turning blue with crying and possible seizures. Posture of his body changes.

Best next step in his management? [Click to reveal]
  • Chest x-ray (Low yield initially)
  • Echocardiogram (Consider if cardiac cause suspected)
  • Pulse ox (Essential monitoring)
  • Reassurance → breath-holding spell (Classic description: trigger (crying), cyanosis, brief LOC/posturing, rapid recovery. Cyanotic type most common).
    • Cyanotic (decreased brain perfusion) vs pallid (vagal response, complete loss of tone).

Case 7 (Peds):

9-month-old female with 2-day history of worsening cough, congestion, “wheezing". Today, was breathing "really hard" so mom wanted to get her checked out. Lots of nasal discharge. Afebrile, P 160, O2 is 92%. All signs of respiratory distress and accessory muscle use. Attends day-care, fully immunized, worsening with decreased oral intake. T-max 100.4 F (38 C). Copious rhinorrhea.

Most likely underlying cause? [Click to reveal]
  • Aspirated foreign body (Possible, but less likely with diffuse sx/rhinorrhea)
  • Inappropriate smooth muscle contraction of the airways (Asthma - less common at this age, usually more focal wheeze)
  • Infection within lung parenchyma (Pneumonia - possible, but wheezing points more to airways)
  • Mucus and debris within the airways (Classic for Bronchiolitis)
    • Worse on day 3-5
    • Copious rhinorrhea
    • Symmetric wheeze (unlike asthma which is often focal)
Most likely agent? [Click to reveal]

RSV → supportive care, high flow O2, time

  • Under 92% sats → admit
  • DO NOT GIVE ALBUTEROL (generally not effective in bronchiolitis)

Babies with chronic lung disease, congenital heart disease will receive monoclonal antibodies (Palivizumab) for this prophylactically.

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)

OSAHS (Obstructive Sleep Apnea-Hypopnea Syndrome)

Definition Criteria [Click to reveal]
  • Unexplained excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS) with at least five obstructive breathing events (apnea or hypopnea) per hour of sleep.
  • OR AHI (Apnea/Hypopnea Index) of 15 or more in the absence of daytime symptoms (per older criteria, text says 10 or more).
  • Sleep study positive IF:
    • AHI ≥ 5 with associated symptoms (EDS, snoring, witnessed apneas, non-restorative sleep, fatigue etc.)
    • AHI ≥ 15 regardless of symptoms.
    • (Text notes AHI > 15 without symptoms + O2 sat drop > 3% - desaturation criteria often included in hypopnea definition).
Physiology of Obstruction [Click to reveal]
  • Inspiration: Intraluminal pharyngeal pressure becomes increasingly negative, creating a "suctioning" force that tends to collapse the upper airway.
  • Muscle Tone: Pharyngeal dilator muscles normally counteract this, but they relax/go weak during sleep (especially REM).
  • Arousal: Airway collapses → apnea/hypopnea → hypoxia/hypercapnia → triggers brief arousal from sleep → muscle tone restored, airway opens → ventilation resumes → fall back asleep → cycle repeats.
Epidemiology & Risk Factors [Click to reveal]
  • ~60% of cases due to obesity (BMI > 30 is major risk factor, 50% mentioned in text). Neck circumference is a strong predictor.
  • More common in Males, Middle age.
  • Anatomic factors: Shortening of mandible (retrognathia), macroglossia, tonsillar hypertrophy.
  • Other conditions: Acromegaly, hypothyroidism, myotonic dystrophy, Ehlers-Danlos.
  • Family history.
Features/Symptoms [Click to reveal]
  • Loud Snoring (but can be absent in some with OSA).
  • Witnessed apneas / Gasping/snorting during sleep.
  • Frequent awakening or sleep disruption (fragmented sleep).
  • Excessive Daytime Sleepiness (EDS) - most common presenting complaint.
  • Non-restorative sleep, morning headaches, difficulty concentrating, irritability.
Health Consequences & Comorbidities [Click to reveal]
  • Neurocognitive: Daytime sleepiness, impaired vigilance, cognition, difficulty concentrating, depressing mood alteration.
  • Cardiovascular: HTN (augmented SNS activation and alterations in RAAS, lead to MI risk), arrhythmias (esp. Afib), stroke, heart failure. Recurrent hypoxia and release of cortisol contribute.
  • Metabolic: Diabetes mellitus (insulin resistance independent of obesity), weight gain, metabolic syndrome.
  • Other: Nocturia, decreased libido.
  • Public health concern → Driving with lack of sleep (similar impairment to alcohol). Increased risk of accidents.
  • Disturbed sleep architecture, decrease in sleep quality = sleep deprivation.
Diagnosis [Click to reveal]
  • Differential includes: Insufficient sleep/poor sleep hygiene, shift work disorder, psychological/psychiatric causes (depression, anxiety), drugs (sedatives, alcohol), narcolepsy, phase alteration syndrome (delayed/advanced sleep phase).
  • Gold standard: Polysomnogram (PSG) - in-lab sleep study monitoring EEG, EOG, EMG, ECG, airflow, respiratory effort, O2 saturation.
  • Home Sleep Apnea Testing (HSAT) is an alternative for high-probability patients without significant comorbidities.
OSAHS Severity Scale (based on AHI) [Click to reveal]
  • Apnea-hypopnea index (AHI): Number of apneas plus hypopneas per hour of sleep.
  • Respiratory disturbance index (RDI): Number of apneas plus hypopneas plus RERAs (Respiratory Effort-Related Arousals) per hour of sleep.
  • Mild OSAHS: AHI of 5-14 events/h
  • Moderate OSAHS: AHI of 15-29 events/h
  • Severe OSAHS: AHI of ≥30 events/h
Treatment [Click to reveal]
  • Behavioral: Weight loss if obese, limit alcohol/sedatives (especially before bed), sleep position therapy (avoid supine).
  • Treat Reversible Causes: e.g., treat allergic rhinitis with intranasal spray if contributing to nasal obstruction.
  • CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure): Standard first-line therapy. Keeps airway open during sleep with pressurized air. APAP (auto-titrating) and BiPAP (bilevel) are variations.
  • Mandibular Repositioning Splint (MRS) / Oral Appliance: Specialist use only (dentist trained in sleep medicine). Advances mandible/tongue forward. Option for mild-moderate OSA or those intolerant of CPAP.
  • Surgery:
    • Tonsillectomy (and adenoidectomy) in children (primary treatment if hypertrophy is the cause).
    • Uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP): Resection of uvula, soft palate, tonsils. Not done as often, variable success.
    • Other surgeries: Genioglossus advancement, maxillomandibular advancement (MMA), tracheostomy (rare, severe cases).
  • Bariatric surgery: For significant weight loss in obese patients.
  • New therapies: Upper airway neurostimulation (hypoglossal nerve stimulator, e.g., Inspire) - stimulates tongue muscles during sleep (implanted device).

    • Neoplasia → work-up needed as 10-15% have underlying carcinoma (post-obstructive).
    • Usually involves dependent lung segments (posterior upper lobes, superior lower lobes if supine; basal lower lobes if upright). Text says right lower lobe, but can be found in other areas as well.
    • Complications: Sepsis, hemorrhage, brain abscess, meningitis.
  • Granulomas (Less typical for acute bacterial aspiration, more chronic/TB/fungal)
  • Vasculitis (Not typical)

Pharmacology: Drugs for Rhinitis/Allergy

Allergy avoidance first, but this is difficult to achieve and maintain.

Oral Antihistamines

General Info [Click to reveal]

Prevent and relieve most symptoms (sneezing, itching, rhinorrhea, ocular sx) BUT have only a modest effect on nasal congestion.

First Generation [Click to reveal]
  • Examples: diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine, chlorpheniramine
  • No longer popular for allergies due to sedative effect (cross BBB) and other antimuscarinic properties (dry mouth, urinary retention, blurred vision).
Second Generation [Click to reveal]
  • Examples: loratadine/desloratadine, cetirizine/levocetirizine, fexofenadine
  • Less sedative effect (less lipophilic, do not cross BBB as easily). Cetirizine/Levocetirizine can still cause some sedation.
  • Often taken prophylactically rather than after symptoms start for best effect.

Topical Antihistamines (Nasal Sprays, Eye Drops)

Azelastine (Nasal Spray) [Click to reveal]
  • PK: Onset of action ~15 minutes. ~40% systemically absorbed, hepatic metabolism.
  • A/E: Bitter taste, headache, somnolence, excessive sedation if taken with other sedatives (e.g., alcohol).
  • Use: Allergic rhinitis. Often second-line after nasal steroids.
Olopatadine (Nasal Spray, Eye Drops) [Click to reveal]
  • PK: ~58% bioavailability (nasal), renal excreted, half-life 8-12 hours.
  • A/E: Epistaxis (nosebleed), headache.
  • Use: Nasal spray for allergic rhinitis. Eyedrop for allergic conjunctivitis (works within 3 minutes).

Epinastine, Ketotifen, Levocabastine not indicated in the US for nasal use (Ketotifen available as eye drop).

Glucocorticoids (Steroids)

MOA & Warnings [Click to reveal]
  • MOA: Inhibition of phospholipase A2, liberating arachidonic acid, starting leukotriene pathway; inhibition of inflammatory cytokine production. Broad anti-inflammatory effects.
  • Warnings (Systemic effects, more relevant for oral/high-dose inhaled): Glaucoma, cataracts, impaired wound healing, immunosuppression, suppression of the HPA axis, reduced growth velocity (in children).
Topical (Intranasal) Steroids [Click to reveal]
  • Examples: Fluticasone, Mometasone, Budesonide, Ciclesonide, Beclomethasone.
  • Most effective single agent for allergic rhinitis, especially congestion.
  • Long onset of relief: takes at least 12 hours, sometimes longer (days) for full effect. Needs regular use.
  • Chronically, suppress all nasal symptoms and improve quality of life.
  • Some have limited bioavailability (fluticasone, ciclesonide) and fewer dose-related systemic A/E.
  • A/E: Epistaxis, sneezing, stinging, headache (local effects).
  • Use: First-line for moderate-to-severe or persistent allergic rhinitis.
Systemic Steroids [Click to reveal]

Neither prednisone nor parenteral steroids are used for routine allergic rhinitis as long-term risks outweigh any temporary benefit. May be used short-term for severe exacerbations or nasal polyps.

Leukotriene Antagonists

Montelukast [Click to reveal]
  • MOA: Blocks cysteinyl leukotriene receptor (CysLT1).
  • Use: Seasonal or perennial rhinitis in patients > 2 years (also used for asthma). Less effective than nasal steroids. Chronically, relieves both nasal and ocular symptoms by ~1/3.
  • A/E: Headache, GI upset, rash. **Neuropsychiatric effects** (agitation, aggression, depression, suicidality) - FDA Black Box Warning. Maybe Churg-Strauss syndrome (eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis - rare).
  • Zafirlukast (rarely used due to hepatotoxicity risk and BID dosing). Zileuton (inhibits 5-lipoxygenase, hepatotoxicity risk, QID dosing).

Chromones

Cromolyn and Nedocromil [Click to reveal]
  • MOA: Inhibit mast cell or eosinophil degranulation by inhibiting delayed chloride channels, and may also act on nasal nerves (stabilize mast cells).
  • PK: Low bioavailability.
  • Use: Nasal sprays or eyedrops. Less effective than antihistamines or nasal steroids. Requires frequent dosing (QID). Very safe profile. Used prophylactically.
  • A/E: Local irritation cough, dry mouth.

Anticholinergic Agents

Ipratropium nasal spray [Click to reveal]
  • Quaternary amine (doesn't cross BBB).
  • PK: Given TID, low bioavailability.
  • Use: Decreases rhinorrhea (runny nose) in people > 5 years (allergic or non-allergic/vasomotor rhinitis). Not effective for itching, sneezing, congestion.
  • A/E: Epistaxis, dry nose/mouth, urinary retention, glaucoma (if sprayed in eyes), other anticholinergic effects (rare due to low absorption).

Decongestants

Topical (Nasal Sprays/Drops) [Click to reveal]
  • Alpha-1 adrenergic agonists used for temporary relief.
  • MOA: Vasoconstriction within seconds → reduces swelling/congestion.
  • Examples: Phenylephrine, Oxymetazoline (Afrin, Dristan nasal sprays), Naphazoline. (Ephedrine → banned). (Pseudoephedrine → oral, "behind-the-counter"). (Oral Sudafed OM likely contains phenylephrine, Vicks Sinex often oxymetazoline).
  • A/E: Tachycardia (less common than oral), Chronic use: atrophy of nasal lining and nasal septal defects.
  • Prolonged use (>3-5 days) → rhinitis medicamentosa (rebound vasodilation) with even more severe congestion.
  • Use: Drops or spray directly into the nose for short-term relief (e.g., during URI).
Systemic (Oral) [Click to reveal]
  • Examples: Pseudoephedrine, Phenylephrine.
  • Not as rapidly effective as topical.
  • A/E: Last longer. Mild central nervous system stimulation (insomnia, agitation), tachycardia, elevated BP.
    • Stroke can occur in patients with HTN and/or cerebral vasospasms.
    • Severe HTN can occur when pseudoephedrine is given with an MAOI.
    • CONTRAINDICATIONS: Patients with uncontrolled HTN, pheochromocytoma, angina pectoris, glaucoma, urinary retention (BPH), MAOI use within 14 days, and similar conditions. Use with caution in controlled HTN, heart disease, hyperthyroidism, diabetes.

Immunotherapy

Omalizumab (Anti-IgE) [Click to reveal]
  • MOA: Monoclonal antibody against IgE.
  • Use: Primarily for severe allergic asthma, sometimes used off-label for refractory allergic rhinitis.
  • Subcutaneous injection: Expensive, risk of anaphylaxis, time-consuming.
  • (Text mentions Sublingual and Oral - Sublingual immunotherapy (SLIT) exists for specific allergens (grass, ragweed, dust mite). Oral immunotherapy (OIT) primarily for food allergies, requires careful monitoring.)
  • Oral: effective for peanut and egg white allergies, but careful monitoring is needed.
Allergen Immunotherapy (SCIT/SLIT) [Click to reveal]
  • Subcutaneous (SCIT - allergy shots) or Sublingual (SLIT - tablets/drops).
  • Gradual administration of increasing doses of specific allergens.
  • Use: Moderate-severe allergic rhinitis/asthma unresponsive to meds/avoidance, or patient preference. Requires long-term commitment (3-5 years).
  • Risk of systemic allergic reactions (esp. SCIT).

Surgery

Indications [Click to reveal]

Endoscopic sinus and nasal surgery → patients with chronic rhinosinusitis refractory to medical therapy, nasal polyps, hypertrophied turbinates, severe septal deviation or other nasal anomalies causing obstruction.

Acute Rhinosinusitis (ARS) - Treatment Focused

Definition & Causes [Click to reveal]
  • Inflammation of mucosal lining of nasal passage and/or paranasal sinuses lasting up to 4 weeks.
  • Most common cause is viral.
  • Diagnosed by culture (rarely done clinically).
  • Common bacterial pathogens (if bacterial ARS):
    • 20-43%: S. pneumoniae
    • 22-35%: H. influenzae (non-typeable)
    • 2-10% M. catarrhalis
    • <2%: S. aureus
Antibiotic Selection (Bacterial ARS) [Click to reveal]
  • Initial therapy (standard dose): Amoxicillin + clavulanate (Augmentin) preferred over amoxicillin alone due to H. flu resistance.
  • High doses needed for: Geographic areas with rates of invasive PCN-non-susceptible S. pneumoniae (>10%), severe infections (fever >39C, systemic illness), daycare attendance, age < 2 years old or > 65 years old, recent hospitalization, ABX use in last 30 days, or immunosuppression.
  • Other considerations / Penicillin allergy:
    • Respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin or moxifloxacin) - use cautiously due to side effects.
    • Doxycycline for adults.
    • "Non-severe" PCN allergy (e.g., rash): Clindamycin + oral third-gen cephalosporin (e.g., cefixime, cefpodoxime).
  • Duration: 5-7 days for adults, 10-14 days for children (or based on clinical response).
Adjunctive Therapies [Click to reveal]
  • Nasal irrigation (saline).
  • Intranasal steroids for patients with a history of allergic rhinitis.
  • DO NOT give topical/oral decongestants (limited benefit, potential side effects) or antifungal therapy (unless fungal sinusitis confirmed). Antihistamines generally not helpful unless underlying allergy.
Therapy for Non-Responders [Click to reveal]
  • Broaden coverage (e.g., switch to high-dose Augmentin, fluoroquinolone, or consider IV abx if severe).
  • Image sinuses (CT scan) to look for complications (abscess, mucocele, anatomic issue).
  • Consider invasive procedure (sinus puncture/endoscopy) to obtain proper cultures if refractory.

Acute Pharyngitis (Strep Throat) - Treatment Focused

General Info & Diagnosis [Click to reveal]
  • Mostly virus, but could be Group A S. pyogenes (GAS).
  • Culture vs. "rapid strep test":
    • Risk of complications (rheumatic fever) higher in children, so confirmatory culture recommended after negative rapid test in kids/adolescents.
    • Culture not necessary for adults with negative rapid test (low risk of RF).
  • Centor criteria (suggest likelihood of GAS): Fever, absence of cough, anterior cervical lymphadenopathy, and/or tonsillar or pharyngeal exudates. (Age is often added in modified versions).
Complications of GABHS (Group A Beta-Hemolytic Strep) [Click to reveal]
  • Suppurative: Peritonsillar abscess, retropharyngeal abscess, otitis media, sinusitis, bacteremia or toxic shock syndrome.
  • Non-suppurative (Immune-mediated): Acute rheumatic fever (ARF), post-strep glomerulonephritis (PSGN).
  • Risk factors for ARF: history of rheumatic fever, valvular heart disease, immunosuppression and recurrent or chronic pharyngitis.
  • ABX treatment (within 9 days) prevents ARF, but NOT PSGN. ABX treatment for 24 hours makes patient non-contagious.
Treatment [Click to reveal]
  • Does not require ABX since mostly viral. Treat GAS if positive test or high suspicion based on Centor.
  • If S. pyogenes confirmed/suspected: ABX reduce duration of symptoms by ~1 day and risk of acute rheumatic fever.
  • Antibiotic Choices:
    • Penicillin (Oral Pen VK or IM Benzathine Pen G) is drug of choice.
    • Amoxicillin (often used in kids due to taste/dosing).
    • PCN Allergy: Erythromycin, clarithromycin, or azithromycin for patients with anaphylactic reaction to PCN. Cephalosporins (e.g., cephalexin) if non-anaphylactic allergy.

Pharmacology: Drugs for Respiratory Infections

General Antibiotic Classes for URIs/Pneumonia

Beta-lactams (Penicillins, Cephalosporins, Carbapenems) [Click to reveal]
  • MOA: Cell wall inhibitors - inhibit enzyme (transpeptidase) that removes terminal D-alanine, preventing cross-linking of peptidoglycan. Bactericidal.
  • A/E: Hypersensitivity (rash, urticaria, anaphylaxis), pruritus, candidiasis, pseudomembranous colitis (C. diff).
  • Spectrum varies by generation:
    • First gen (PCN G/V, Cephalexin): gram (+), some gram (-)
    • Second gen (Cefuroxime, Cefaclor): increased gram (+) coverage, better H. flu.
    • Third gen (Ceftriaxone, Cefotaxime, Ceftazidime): decreased gram (+), increased gram (-), some cross BBB.
    • Extended spectrum PCNs (Ampicillin, Amoxicillin): Broader G+/G-. Often combined with beta-lactamase inhibitor (Clavulanate, Sulbactam, Tazobactam).
    • Anti-pseudomonal PCNs (Piperacillin): Broadest spectrum PCNs.
    • Carbapenems (Imipenem, Meropenem): Very broad spectrum.
Macrolides [Click to reveal]
  • Protein synthesis inhibitors (bind to 50S ribosomal subunit). Bacteriostatic.
  • Examples: Azithromycin, Erythromycin, Clarithromycin.
  • Spectrum: Good atypical coverage (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Legionella), some Gram (+), some Gram (-).
  • A/E: GI distress (most frequent with erythromycin), hepatotoxicity, pseudomembranous colitis, QT prolongation. Erythromycin/Clarithromycin are CYP3A4 inhibitors (drug interactions).
Clindamycin [Click to reveal]
  • Protein synthesis inhibitor (binds 50S subunit). Bacteriostatic.
  • Spectrum: Excellent Gram (+) cocci (including MRSA strains) and Anaerobe coverage.
  • A/E: High risk of pseudomembranous colitis (C. diff), severe diarrhea. Rash.
  • Use: Skin/soft tissue infections, aspiration pneumonia, anaerobic infections, alternative for PCN-allergic patients with GAS pharyngitis or serious Staph/Strep infections.
Sulfonamides (TMP-SMX) [Click to reveal]
  • Antagonists for folate biosynthesis (inhibit two successive steps). Bacteriostatic alone, bactericidal in combination (Trimethoprim-Sulfamethoxazole).
  • Spectrum: Broad Gram (+) and Gram (-), Nocardia, *Pneumocystis jirovecii*.
  • A/E: Hypersensitivity (rash, Stevens-Johnson syndrome), photosensitivity, bone marrow toxicity (anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia), hemolytic anemia (in G6PD deficiency), hepatotoxicity, crystalluria (renal damage - maintain hydration).
  • Use: UTIs, *P. jirovecii* pneumonia (PJP) treatment and prophylaxis, Nocardiosis, some MRSA infections.
  • CONTRAINDICATED: G6PD deficiency, pregnancy (esp. 1st/3rd trimester), infants <2 months, sulfa allergy.
Tetracyclines (Doxycycline) [Click to reveal]
  • Protein synthesis inhibitor (binds 30S subunit). Bacteriostatic.
  • Spectrum: Atypicals (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia), some G+/G-, Rickettsia, Borrelia.
  • A/E: GI upset, photosensitivity, tooth discoloration (in children <8), deposition in bone.
  • Use: Atypical pneumonia, acne, tick-borne diseases, alternative for PCN-allergic in CAP.
Fluoroquinolones [Click to reveal]
  • Inhibit DNA gyrase (topoisomerase II) and topoisomerase IV. Bactericidal.
  • "Respiratory quinolones": Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin, Gemifloxacin (good Strep pneumo coverage). Ciprofloxacin has poor Strep pneumo coverage.
  • Spectrum: Broad G+/G-, atypicals, Pseudomonas (Cipro/Levo).
  • A/E: GI upset, CNS effects (dizziness, confusion), photosensitivity, QT prolongation, tendonitis/tendon rupture (Black Box Warning), peripheral neuropathy, aortic aneurysm/dissection risk, dysglycemia. May contribute to MRSA and C. diff development.
  • Use: CAP, HAP, sinusitis (alternative), UTIs (Cipro/Levo). Use reserved due to side effects.
Aminoglycosides (Gentamicin, Tobramycin, Amikacin) [Click to reveal]
  • Protein synthesis inhibitor (binds 30S subunit). Bactericidal.
  • Spectrum: Excellent aerobic Gram (-) coverage (including Pseudomonas). Often used synergistically with beta-lactams for Gram (+) infections (e.g., endocarditis).
  • A/E: Nephrotoxicity, Ototoxicity (vestibular and auditory), neuromuscular blockade. Require therapeutic drug monitoring.
  • Use: Serious Gram (-) infections, often in combination therapy (e.g., HAP/VAP, sepsis). Inhaled tobramycin for CF.
Vancomycin [Click to reveal]
  • Glycopeptide - inhibits cell wall synthesis by binding D-Ala-D-Ala terminus. Bactericidal.
  • Spectrum: Gram (+) only, including MRSA. Oral form used only for C. diff colitis (not absorbed).
  • A/E: Nephrotoxicity, Ototoxicity, Red Man Syndrome (infusion-related histamine release - flushing, itching). Requires therapeutic drug monitoring (troughs).
  • Use: Serious MRSA infections (pneumonia, bacteremia, endocarditis), empiric coverage in severe CAP/HAP/VAP.
Linezolid [Click to reveal]
  • Oxazolidinone - inhibits protein synthesis (binds 50S subunit, prevents initiation complex formation). Bacteriostatic.
  • Spectrum: Gram (+) only, including MRSA and VRE (Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus).
  • A/E: Bone marrow suppression (thrombocytopenia), peripheral/optic neuropathy (long-term use), serotonin syndrome (weak MAOI activity).
  • Use: MRSA pneumonia (HAP/VAP), VRE infections, skin/soft tissue infections.

Specific Infection Management Notes

Diphtheria [Click to reveal]
  • Agent: Corynebacterium diphtheriae
  • Key feature: Gray pharyngeal exudates with pseudomembranes
  • Tx: Antitoxin + Antibiotics (IV Pen G or erythromycin)
Acute Bronchitis [Click to reveal]
  • Usually viral (Influenza virus, parainfluenza virus, coronavirus, adenovirus, rhinovirus).
  • Tx: Primarily supportive. Antivirals (Oseltamivir or inhaled zanamivir) if Influenza A or B confirmed/suspected within 48h.
  • Antibiotics (e.g., azithromycin preferred, erythromycin, clarithromycin, trimethoprim + sulfa) generally NOT recommended unless pertussis suspected or bacterial superinfection likely.
Pneumonia - General Tx Principles [Click to reveal]
  • Signs: Temp > 100.4 F (38 C), sputum production, chest consolidation on PE. Cough, fever common symptoms.
  • Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP):
    • Previously healthy, no abx use prior 3 months: Oral amoxicillin (high dose), doxycycline, or macrolide (if local pneumococcal resistance <25%).
    • Adults with comorbidities (chronic heart/lung/liver/renal disease, DM, alcoholism, malignancy, asplenia) or abx use in past 3 months: Beta-lactam (e.g., Augmentin, cefpodoxime, cefuroxime) PLUS macrolide or doxycycline, OR monotherapy with "respiratory quinolone" (Levo, Moxi, Gemi).
    • Children 2 weeks or older: Oral oseltamivir if influenza suspected.
    • Previously healthy, fully immunized children: Amoxicillin (high dose). PCN allergy: respiratory quinolone, linezolid, macrolide (consider resistance).
    • Kids with atypical pathogen suspected: Macrolide.
    • Children 5 years old with resistant S. pneumo suspected: Add either macrolide or clavulanate to beta-lactam.
    • When to hospitalize: Use severity score (e.g., CURB-65, PSI). CURB-65 score ≥ 2 often suggests hospitalization, ≥3 ICU.
      • Confusion
      • Urea > 7 mmol/L (BUN > 19-20 mg/dL) (Text says >42 - likely typo, check units/value)
      • Respiratory rate ≥ 30
      • Blood pressure (systolic < 90 or diastolic ≤ 60)
      • Age ≥ 65
    • Pediatric Hospitalization criteria: Respiratory distress and hypoxemia, suspected bacterial CAP <3-6 months old, suspected pathogen with increased virulence (e.g., MRSA), concern about in-home observation/compliance.
  • Hospital-acquired pneumonia (HAP) / Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP):
    • HAP: Develops ≥ 48 hours after admission, was not incubating on admission.
    • VAP: Develops ≥ 48 hours after endotracheal intubation.
    • Management involves assessing risk factors for MDR (multi-drug resistant) pathogens.
    • Empiric Tx covers Gram (+) (including MRSA if risk factors - use Vanco or Linezolid) and Gram (-) (including Pseudomonas if risk factors - use anti-pseudomonal beta-lactam like Pip/Tazo, Ceftazidime, Cefepime, Carbapenem +/- second anti-pseudomonal agent like Aminoglycoside or Fluoroquinolone).
    • Pseudomonas Tx: pip+tazo, plus either an aminoglycoside or a "respiratory quinolone" or both (if severe/high resistance risk).
    • MRSA Tx: add linezolid or vanco.
    • Kids: ampicillin or pen G standard; add macrolide if M. pneumo suspected; Vancomycin if S. aureus suspected.
    • Management of VAP: Fever, leukocytosis, increased secretions, new or changing pulmonary infiltrates. Cultures needed. Triple therapy often used initially for broad coverage if MDR risk: Anti-pseudomonal beta-lactam + (Anti-pseudomonal FQ or Aminoglycoside) + Anti-MRSA agent (Vanco/Linezolid). Duration often 7 days.
  • Other Pneumonias:
    • Nosocomial MRSA: CA-MRSA often more virulent; HAP-MRSA may have fewer useful antibiotics.
    • Mycoplasma pneumonia: Pneumonia but often no leukocytosis. Extrapulmonary manifestations: skin eruptions, neuro sx, hemolytic anemia. Tx: Azithromycin, respiratory quinolone (but not cipro), tetracyclines.
    • Legionella pneumonia: Often severe. Tx: Azithromycin, respiratory quinolones for transplant patients. Very sick patients: both azithromycin and rifampin.
    • C. pneumoniae: Tx: Macrolide or tetracycline.
    • Anaerobic pneumonia (often aspiration): Metronidazole, amoxicillin + sulbactam, carbapenem. Clindamycin also option.
    • Pneumocystis jirovecii (PJP): See below.
Chronic Bronchitis Exacerbation [Click to reveal]
  • Pathophys: Inhalation of irritating noxious substance compromises normal secretory mucociliary function. Cough with purulent sputum (white to yellow green).
  • Tx: Bronchodilators (beta-2 agonists like theophylline - older agent, inhaled SABA/LABA standard), inhaled anticholinergics (SAMA/LAMA). Mucolytic aerosols of N-acetylcysteine (variable benefit).
  • Antibiotics: Only give ~5 days of abx if patient has increased dyspnea, increased sputum volume, AND increased sputum purulence (Anthonisen criteria), or requires mechanical ventilation. Also consider if comorbid conditions, age >65 years, hospitalized in last year, diabetes, heart failure, taking steroids. Choice depends on local resistance (e.g., Amox/Clav, Doxycycline, Azithromycin).
Pertussis (Whooping Cough) [Click to reveal]
  • Agent: Bordetella pertussis
  • Tx: Macrolides (Erythromycin estolate, azithromycin, clarithromycin). Azithromycin preferred due to dosing/tolerance. Treat patient and close contacts.
  • Infants <1 month: Azithromycin preferred (erythromycin associated with infantile hypertrophic pyloric stenosis).
  • Alternative: Trimethoprim + sulfa (third-line).
Adjunctive Therapies for Cough [Click to reveal]
  • Antitussives (e.g., dextromethorphan, codeine) are not usually beneficial in early stages of acute cough but are helpful when cough is >2-3 weeks or debilitating.
  • Bronchodilators may decrease cough in up to 50% of patients after 7 days but are not FDA-approved for acute cough alone.

Antifungal / Anti-TB / Anti-Parasitic / Other

Pulmonary TB [Click to reveal]
  • Standard initial phase (2 months): Isoniazid (INH), Rifampin (RIF), Pyrazinamide (PZA), Ethambutol (EMB).
  • Continuation phase (4 months): INH + RIF.
  • Drug interactions common (esp. Rifampin - potent CYP inducer). Anti-TB and anti-retrovirals interact significantly.
  • Monitor LFTs (INH, PZA, RIF), vision/color vision (EMB), uric acid (PZA).
Nocardiosis [Click to reveal]
  • Gram (+) branching filamentous bacteria.
  • Tx: Trimethoprim + Sulfa (TMP-SMX) is drug of choice.
  • Severe cases: TMP+SMX, amikacin, and either ceftriaxone or imipenem. Long duration of therapy needed.
Pneumocystis jirovecii Pneumonia (PJP) [Click to reveal]
  • Fungal pneumonia, common opportunistic infection in HIV/immunocompromised.
  • Tx/Prophylaxis: TMP + SMX is first line.
  • Alternatives:
    • TMP + dapsone
    • Primaquine + clindamycin (Check G6PD before primaquine - risk of hemolysis)
    • Atovaquone (less effective but better tolerated than TMP+SMX).
    • Very sick patients: Parenteral pentamidine (significant toxicity), or trimetrexate + leucovorin.
Histoplasmosis [Click to reveal]
  • Dimorphic fungus.
  • Tx: Mild/Moderate: Itraconazole. Severe/Chronic/Cavitary: Amphotericin B initially, followed by long course (3-12 months) of itraconazole. Acute severe/disseminated: 2 weeks of glucocorticoids may be added.
Coccidioidomycosis [Click to reveal]
  • Dimorphic fungus.
  • Tx: Mild/Moderate pulmonary: Often no treatment needed. Severe cases/disseminated: Itraconazole or Fluconazole. Meningitis: Fluconazole (lifelong). Amphotericin B for very severe disease.
Blastomycosis [Click to reveal]
  • Dimorphic fungus.
  • Tx: Mild/Moderate: Itraconazole. Severe/CNS: Amphotericin B initially, followed by long courses of itraconazole after stabilization. DIC complication: fluconazole? (Unclear context/accuracy).
Cryptococcosis [Click to reveal]
  • Encapsulated yeast.
  • Tx: Pulmonary (mild/mod, immunocompetent): Fluconazole. Severe pulmonary or Meningitis: Amphotericin B + Flucytosine (induction), followed by Fluconazole (consolidation/maintenance).
Mucormycosis [Click to reveal]
  • Opportunistic mold (Rhizopus, Mucor). High mortality. Risk factor: Diabetics (esp. DKA), immunosuppression.
  • Tx: Surgical debridement + Antifungal. Initial therapy is lipid formulation Amphotericin B. Step-down/salvage: Posaconazole or Isavuconazole.
Aspergillosis [Click to reveal]
  • Mold. Spectrum of disease (Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis - ABPA, Aspergilloma, Chronic Pulmonary, Invasive).
  • Tx: ABPA: Steroids + Itraconazole. Aspergilloma: Observation or surgery (antifungals don't penetrate well). Chronic/Invasive: Voriconazole is drug of choice for invasive disease. Itraconazole for chronic/allergic forms. Amphotericin B, Isavuconazole, Echinocandins are alternatives/salvage.
Candidiasis [Click to reveal]
  • Yeast. Rare respiratory infections (usually colonization or invasive disease in severely immunocompromised).
  • Tx: Fluconazole for susceptible species. Echinocandins or Amphotericin B for resistant/severe cases.
Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome (SARS) / Avian Influenza [Click to reveal]
  • SARS (Coronavirus): No effective specific therapy identified during original outbreak. Supportive care.
  • Avian influenza (e.g., H5N1): Supportive therapy. Antivirals (Oseltamivir) may be used but efficacy uncertain.
Misc Pharm Facts [Click to reveal]
  • Tetracyclines are contraindicated in pregnancy and kids < 8 years old (tooth staining).
  • True IgE mediated beta-lactam allergy (anaphylaxis): can use aztreonam (monobactam, low cross-reactivity, Gram-negative only coverage).

Pharmacology: Drugs Used in Asthma and/or COPD

General Asthma Concepts

  • Pathophys: Acute bouts of bronchoconstriction, associated with airway inflammation and hyperresponsiveness.
  • Goals: Short-term = airway relaxation (bronchodilation); long-term = anti-inflammation.
  • Allergic component: IgE → mast cell degranulation → bronchoconstriction (Leukotrienes LTB4, LTC4, LTD4; Prostaglandin LGD2 from Th2 cells) and inflammation.

Sympathomimetic Agents (Bronchodilators)

MOA [Click to reveal]

Relax bronchial smooth muscle and inhibit release of mediators (histamine) from mast cells, activate adenylyl cyclase via Gs → increases cAMP → bronchodilation.

Epinephrine (mixed α, β-1 and β-2) [Click to reveal]
  • MOA: Rapid-acting non-selective bronchodilator (and vasoconstrictor, cardiac stimulant).
  • PK: Onset of action 15 minutes.
  • Use: Inhalation or SubQ, only for those with an anaphylactoid reaction (drug of choice for anaphylaxis). Not for routine asthma/COPD.
  • A/E: Tachycardia, cardiac dysrhythmias, increased risk of angina pectoris, tremor, anxiety.
Ephedrine (α-1 and β-agonist) [Click to reveal]
  • Indirect and direct acting sympathomimetic.
  • PK: Longer duration of action than epi, higher oral bioavailability, lower potency, higher rate of CNS adverse effects.
  • Use: Largely obsolete for respiratory use due to side effects and better alternatives.
Isoproterenol (β-1 and β-2) [Click to reveal]
  • MOA: Non-selective β-agonist. Potent bronchodilator.
  • Use: Largely replaced by selective β-2 agonists due to cardiac (β-1) side effects.

Selective Beta-2 Agonists

Short-Acting Beta Agonists (SABA) [Click to reveal]
  • Action 15-30 minutes, duration 3-4 hours. "Rescue" inhalers.
  • Albuterol (Ventolin, ProAir, Proventil):
    • PK: Onset ~12 min, ½ life 4 hr, liver/kidney metabolism/excretion.
    • Use: Inhaler, nebulizer, tablets also exist (less common).
    • A/E: (Tablets/high dose inhaled) tremor (β-2), tachycardia (some β-1 activity/reflex), nervousness, muscle weakness, hypokalemia, hyperglycemia.
  • Levalbuterol (Xopenex): R-isomer of albuterol.
    • PK: Similar to albuterol. Claimed fewer side effects (less β-1 activity), debated clinical significance. More expensive.
  • Terbutaline:
    • PK: Onset ~30 min, ½ life 6 hr, liver/kidney.
    • Use: Tablet or injection (SubQ for severe exacerbations). Inhaler form less common.
  • Metaproterenol: Less β2-selective, more side effects. Onset 1-5 hr? (likely typo, should be faster), ½ life 6 hr, kidneys. Use: tablets.
  • Pirbuterol: Similar to albuterol. Onset 20 min, ½ life 20 min? (likely typo, longer duration), liver/kidneys. Use: tablets or solution in nebulizer.
  • General SABA A/E: Tremor, tachycardia, nervousness, hypokalemia, hyperglycemia, muscle weakness. (High doses) can cause cardiac dysrhythmias. Hypoxia can occur paradoxically in acute bronchodilation due to V/Q mismatch worsening initially.
Long-Acting Beta Agonists (LABA) [Click to reveal]
  • Duration ~12 hours or more. "Controller" medication.
  • CONTRAINDICATED as asthma monotherapy (increased risk of asthma-related death). Must be combined with an inhaled steroid (ICS) in asthma.
  • Can be used as monotherapy in COPD.
  • Formoterol / Salmeterol: Used in COPD and Asthma (with ICS). Available in combination inhalers (e.g., Symbicort = Budesonide/Formoterol; Advair = Fluticasone/Salmeterol). Formoterol has faster onset than Salmeterol.
  • Aformoterol: Nebulizer, COPD.
  • Indacaterol: Once-daily COPD.
  • Vilanterol: Once-daily. Available in combos:
    • COPD: with fluticasone (Breo Ellipta) or umeclidinium (Anoro Ellipta).
    • Asthma: vilanterol + fluticasone (Breo Ellipta).
  • Olodaterol: COPD once-daily, often combined with tiotropium (Stiolto Respimat).

Corticosteroids

MOA & General Effects [Click to reveal]

MOA: Inhibition of PLA2 (phospholipase A2), liberating arachidonic acid, starts leukotriene pathway; inhibit inflammatory cytokine production. Reduce bronchial hyperreactivity, decrease inflammation. Do NOT directly bronchodilate.

Chronic glucocorticoids: Reduce symptoms, bronchial hyperreactivity, frequency of medical encounters, improves pulmonary function.

Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS) [Click to reveal]
  • Cornerstone of persistent asthma control. Used in COPD often in combination with LABA for frequent exacerbators.
  • Examples: Fluticasone, Budesonide, Beclomethasone, Mometasone, Ciclesonide, Flunisolide, Triamcinolone.
  • Less systemic absorption, lower total dose, and fewer adverse effects compared to oral.
  • Used as an "asthma controller". Typically 2 puffs BID.
  • A/E (Local): Oropharyngeal candidiasis (thrush) - use spacers/gargle after use to prevent; hoarseness (dysphonia).
  • A/E (Systemic - high doses/long term): Reduction of growth ~1cm during first year of treatment in children (final adult height usually unaffected), potential for adrenal suppression, osteoporosis, cataracts, skin thinning (less common than oral).
  • Ciclesonide: Prodrug, hydrolyzed by esterases in bronchial epithelial cells and tightly bound to serum proteins (potentially fewer systemic effects).
Systemic Corticosteroids (Oral/IV) [Click to reveal]
  • Examples: Prednisone, Methylprednisolone.
  • Use: Acute exacerbations of asthma/COPD (short bursts). Long-term use for severe persistent asthma/COPD refractory to other treatments (minimize dose/duration).
  • PK: Oral onset ~12 hours.
  • A/E (Systemic): Hyperglycemia, dyslipidemia, osteoporosis, weight gain, adrenal suppression, immunosuppression, cataracts, glaucoma, psychological problems (mood changes, psychosis). Can be minimized by using lowest effective dose, alternate-day therapy if possible.
  • If patients don't respond to normal inhaled meds, take prednisone and taper.

  • Better protection if patients take their oral steroids mid-afternoon? (Contradicts typical morning dosing strategy to minimize HPA suppression).

Leukotriene Inhibitors

Receptor Antagonists (LTRAs) & Synthesis Inhibitors [Click to reveal]
  • Use: Primarily asthma (alternative/add-on), allergic rhinitis, conjunctivitis.
  • Less benefit than steroids (ICS), but less long-term toxicity concerns.
  • Montelukast (Singulair)**: CysLT1 receptor antagonist. Once daily dosing. See Rhinitis section for A/E (headache, GI, rash, **neuropsychiatric warning**).
  • Zafirlukast (Accolate): CysLT1 receptor antagonist. BID dosing. Risk of hepatotoxicity (rarely used).
  • Zileuton (Zyflo): Inhibits 5-lipoxygenase enzyme (blocks synthesis of LTB4 and cysteinyl LTs). QID or BID ER dosing. A/E: Hepatotoxicity → requires LFT monitoring, rarely used. Inhibits CYP3A4.

Phosphodiesterase Inhibitors

Theophylline (Methylxanthine) [Click to reveal]
  • Shares chemical structure and MOA with caffeine and theobromine (cocoa, chocolate).
  • MOA: Non-selective inhibit phosphodiesterase (PDE), decreasing breakdown of cAMP → bronchodilation. Also inhibits cell surface adenosine receptors, inhibits enhancement of histone deacetylation (anti-inflammatory effect).
  • Effects:
    • Lungs: Bronchodilation, increase strength of skeletal muscle contractions (diaphragm), improve diaphragmatic function in COPD.
    • CNS: Mild cortical arousal, increased alertness, deferral of fatigue.
    • CVD: Slight tachycardia, increase in BP, decrease blood viscosity, cardiac dysrhythmias, cerebral arterial constriction/HA.
    • GI: Increase gastric acid and digestive enzymes.
    • GU: Weak diuretic.
  • Use: Orally for chronic prevention of asthma (largely replaced by safer agents). IV (aminophylline) historically used for status asthmaticus.
  • PK: Loading dose needed, clearance varies widely (age, smoking, heart/liver disease, drug interactions - CYP1A2, 3A4), monitored during therapy. Narrow therapeutic index.
  • Target Levels: 5-15 mcg/mL (Text says 5-20, newer guidelines often target lower end).
  • A/E (Toxicity):
    • Levels > 15-20: N/V/D, abdominal discomfort, HA, anxiety, insomnia.
    • Levels > 40: Seizures and ventricular dysrhythmias.
Roflumilast (Daliresp) (PDE-4 Inhibitor) [Click to reveal]
  • MOA: Selective inhibitor of phosphodiesterase-4 (PDE4), increasing cAMP in inflammatory cells. Anti-inflammatory effect.
  • Use: Oral agent to reduce risk of COPD exacerbations in patients with severe COPD associated with chronic bronchitis and a history of exacerbations. NOT a bronchodilator, not for acute relief.
  • A/E: Nausea, headache, diarrhea, weight loss, neuropsychiatric effects (insomnia, anxiety, depression).

Antimuscarinic Agents (Anticholinergics)

MOA & General Effects [Click to reveal]

MOA: Inhibit effects of Acetylcholine (Ach) on muscarinic receptors (M3) in the lung, leading to relaxation of bronchial smooth muscle and reduced mucous secretion. Primarily affect vagal tone.

More effective bronchodilators in COPD than asthma, but used in both.

A/E (Inhaled - usually minimal systemic): Dry mouth, skin flushing, throat irritation. Less common: urinary retention, acute angle-closure glaucoma (if sprayed in eyes), atrial dysrhythmias/tachycardia, allergic phenomena.

Short-Acting Muscarinic Antagonist (SAMA) [Click to reveal]
  • Ipratropium Bromide (Atrovent):
    • Duration ~4-6 hours.
    • Use: COPD maintenance, acute asthma exacerbations (in combination with albuterol - e.g., Combivent/DuoNeb), allergic/non-allergic rhinitis (nasal spray).
Long-Acting Muscarinic Antagonist (LAMA) [Click to reveal]
  • Controller medication, usually once daily dosing.
  • Tiotropium (Spiriva):
    • Use: COPD maintenance (reduces exacerbations), add-on therapy for severe Asthma (inhaled steroid + LABA + Tiotropium).
    • Available as HandiHaler (dry powder) or Respimat (soft mist).
  • Aclidinium bromide (Tudorza Pressair): BID dosing for COPD.
  • Umeclidinium bromide (Incruse Ellipta): Once-daily dry powder for COPD. Available in combo with Vilanterol (Anoro) or Fluticasone/Vilanterol (Trelegy).
  • Glycopyrrolate (Seebri Neohaler, Lonhala Magnair): COPD alone or in combination with indacaterol (Utibron Neohaler) or Formoterol (Bevespi Aerosphere). BID dosing usually.

Monoclonal Antibodies

Anti-IgE Monoclonal Antibodies [Click to reveal]
  • Omalizumab (Xolair):
    • MOA: Humanized monoclonal antibody targeted to region of IgE that binds FcεR1 (high-affinity IgE receptor on mast cells/basophils). Prevents IgE binding, reduces receptor expression.
    • Use: SubQ injection every 2-4 weeks for moderate-severe persistent allergic asthma inadequately controlled on ICS+LABA, lowers serum IgE concentration to undetectable, reduces corticosteroid dependency, improves nasal and conjunctival sx in atopic patients, prevents asthma rehospitalization. Only for most severe allergic asthmatics with elevated IgE and perennial allergen sensitivity.
    • A/E: Injection site reactions, anaphylaxis (Black Box Warning - can occur anytime during treatment), potential increased risk of malignancy (controversial), Churg-Strauss syndrome (more associated with steroid withdrawal).
IL-5 Targeted Monoclonals [Click to reveal]
  • Target Interleukin-5 (key cytokine for eosinophil growth, differentiation, activation, survival).
  • Use: Add-on maintenance therapy for severe eosinophilic asthma.
  • A/E: Headache, injection site reactions, anaphylaxis (rare), myalgias, back pain and fatigue, possible increased risk of herpes zoster (mepolizumab). Cancer risk mentioned but not strongly established.
  • Mepolizumab (Nucala): SubQ injection every 4 weeks. Binds IL-5.
  • Reslizumab (Cinqair): IV infusion every 4 weeks. Binds IL-5. Higher risk of anaphylaxis.
  • Benralizumab (Fasenra): SubQ injection every 4 weeks for 3 doses, then every 8 weeks. Binds IL-5 Receptor Alpha (IL-5Rα) on eosinophils, leading to antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC).
Other Monoclonal Antibodies [Click to reveal]
  • Dupilumab (Dupixent):
    • MOA: Monoclonal antibody against IL-4 Receptor Alpha (IL-4Rα), blocking signaling of both IL-4 and IL-13 (key Th2 cytokines).
    • Use: Add-on maintenance for moderate-severe eosinophilic asthma or oral steroid-dependent asthma. Also used for atopic dermatitis and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps. SubQ injection every 2 weeks.
  • Lebrikizumab / Tezepelumab:
    • Lebrikizumab: MOA: anti-IL-13 monoclonal (Use: not yet approved - development ongoing/paused).
    • Tezepelumab (Tezspire): MOA: Anti-TSLP (Thymic Stromal Lymphopoietin - upstream epithelial cytokine). Use: Add-on for severe asthma regardless of eosinophil count. SubQ every 4 weeks.

Mast Cell Stabilizers

Cromolyn and Nedocromil [Click to reveal]

  • Use: Nebulized solution or MDI for asthma prophylaxis (especially exercise-induced or allergen-associated asthma). Nasal spray or eyedrop for allergic rhino conjunctivitis. Much less effective than ICS. Requires frequent dosing (QID). Very safe profile.

Asthma Management Approach

Staging of Asthma Therapy (General GINA/NHLBI Steps) [Click to reveal]
  • Based on severity (intermittent, mild/moderate/severe persistent). Goal is control.
  • Step 1 (Intermittent/Infrequent Sx): As-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol (preferred) OR as-needed SABA + low-dose ICS taken whenever SABA is used.
  • Step 2 (Mild Persistent): Daily low-dose ICS OR as-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol (preferred reliever). Alternative: LTRA.
  • Step 3 (Moderate Persistent): Low-dose ICS + LABA (combination inhaler preferred). Alternative: Medium-dose ICS or Low-dose ICS + LTRA.
  • Step 4 (Severe Persistent): Medium-dose ICS + LABA. Alternative: High-dose ICS + LABA, may add Tiotropium or LTRA.
  • Step 5 (Severe Persistent): High-dose ICS + LABA. Refer for phenotypic assessment +/- add-on therapy (e.g., Tiotropium, anti-IgE, anti-IL5/5R, anti-IL4R). Consider low-dose oral corticosteroids if needed (minimize use).
  • Note: Text mentions older classification: "mild intermittent", "mild persistent", "moderate persistent", "severe persistent". Treatment steps align generally.
Asthma Treatment: Specific Situations [Click to reveal]
  • "Mild" (Older definition likely Intermittent/Mild Persistent): Albuterol inhalers PRN, then leukotriene pathway inhibitors, then long-acting beta-agonists + inhaled steroids. (Order seems slightly off modern guidelines - ICS is mainstay before LABA). Then oral steroids followed by omalizumab (only for severe allergic).
  • Status asthmaticus (Severe Exacerbation): O2, nebulized SABA (albuterol) often continuously or frequently, nebulized SAMA (ipratropium) usually added for first few doses, systemic IV steroids (e.g., methylprednisolone), consider IV magnesium sulfate, BiPAP/CPAP with albuterol nebulization, ipratropium inhaler, and finally intubation with general anesthesia if refractory/impending failure.

COPD Management Approach

General Principles [Click to reveal]
  • Smoking cessation is paramount. Vaccinations (flu, pneumococcal). Pulmonary rehab.
  • Pharmacotherapy based on GOLD guidelines (symptom burden - CAT/mMRC score, exacerbation history).
  • Group A (Low risk, fewer symptoms): Bronchodilator (SABA or SAMA PRN, or LAMA or LABA scheduled).
  • Group B (Low risk, more symptoms): Long-acting bronchodilator (LAMA or LABA). If persistent symptoms, use LAMA + LABA.
  • Group E (High risk - ≥2 mod exacerbations or ≥1 hospitalization): LAMA + LABA. If eosinophils ≥300, consider adding ICS (LAMA+LABA+ICS). If eos <300 and still exacerbating, consider Roflumilast (if FEV1<50% and chronic bronchitis) or Azithromycin (former smokers).
  • Oxygen therapy if chronic hypoxemia (PaO2 ≤ 55 or SaO2 ≤ 88%).
